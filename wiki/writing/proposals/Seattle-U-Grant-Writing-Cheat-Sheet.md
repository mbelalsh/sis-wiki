# Grant-Writing Cheat Sheet — Foolproof Template + Strategic Language (Seattle U)

## One-Line Intuition
A two-page quick-reference that compresses a fundable proposal into a visual funnel — general topic → gap → research question → plan → conclusion — and then gives a sentence-level checklist for writing every line with strategic, confident, fluff-free language.

## Why This Matters
This is the corpus's shortest proposal-writing document — a "cheat sheet" deliberately built for the writer who has 20 minutes, not 13 pages of Kraicer to read. It earns its place because it does two things no other proposal page does as compactly: (1) it renders the proposal's macro-structure as a single visual funnel diagram, and (2) it gives a dense, copy-pasteable bank of NOT→INSTEAD sentence rewrites tuned specifically for the grant register. Where [[Kraicer-Art-Of-Grantsmanship]] explains the review process and [[Locke-Proposals-That-Work]] explains proposal functions, this sheet is the fast pre-flight checklist.

**Provenance:** published by the Seattle University Office of Sponsored Projects. Page 1 ("The Foolproof Research Proposal Template") is reproduced from Karen Kelsky, Ph.D. (*The Professor Is In*; McNair Scholars Program, University of Oregon) — "Dr. Karen's Foolproof Grant Template." Page 2 ("Tips for Using Language Strategically") is Seattle U's own compilation.

## Core Content

### 1. The Foolproof Research Proposal Template (Kelsky funnel)

The template is a funnel: each stage narrows from the broadest framing to the specific deliverable.

The **first two paragraphs** — all of them, "yes, I'm serious" — must do this:

1. **Large general topic of wide interest** — global warming, immigration, cancer, etc. Open on terrain the reviewer already cares about.
2. **Two brief references to literature** — establish you know the field. Brief, not a review.
3. **The "HOWEVER" pivot** — *"HOWEVER, scholars in these fields have not yet adequately addressed XXXX..."* This single word turns context into a gap.
4. **The gap in knowledge**, stated as two things:
   - **Urgency** — "This gap is bad!!!" — why the gap matters now.
   - **Hero narrative** — "I will fill this gap!!!" — you, specifically, are the one who closes it.
5. **Your research question** — *"I am applying to XXX to support my research on XXX."*

Then the body — "as many pages as they allow and no more":

6. **Specifics of your project** — background, location, history, context, limitations.
7. **Literature review** — multi-page, thorough, accurate, relevant.
8. **Methodology** — discipline-specific.
9. **Timeline** — a month-by-month plan.
10. **Budget** — realistic, legitimate expenses.
11. **Strong conclusion** — *"I expect this research to contribute to debates on XXXXX."*

The structural insight: the **gap (urgency + hero) is the hinge of the whole document**, and it must appear in the first two paragraphs.

### 2. Proposals That Stand Above the Rest

A checklist of properties that separate funded proposals. Successful proposals:

- **Clearly connect the project aims to the sponsor's objectives.**
- **Are structured repetitively** around a single thread that loops throughout.
- **Are consistent** — every component contributes to one coherent story.
- **Are focused and feasible** — not a "fishing expedition" or a mystery novel.
- **Own their value** — confidently assert the importance of anticipated contributions *without overselling*.
- **Present possible pitfalls and alternative solutions** — this builds reviewer trust.

And they have specific sections doing specific jobs:

| Section | Job |
|---|---|
| Introduction | Create empathy and urgency for solving a clear problem |
| Literature Review | Establish a clear gap that is important to fill |
| Research Questions / Hypotheses | Address the established gap; explain how the work extends preliminary work |
| Objectives | **SMART** — Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Reasonable, Time-bound |
| Procedures / Methods | Leave no pragmatic question unaddressed; demonstrate competence and credibility |
| Conclusions | Show how contributions address the urgent problem and advance sponsor objectives |

**Make the reviewer your champion:**

- Tell reviewers where to look — headings and highlighted text that **mirror the review criteria**.
- Assume reviewers are too tired for complex language and jargon.
- Follow the **20-minute Rule** — hook the reviewer within the first page.
- Don't let poor formatting or errors distract from good arguments.
- Write a strong concluding paragraph — **tell the reviewer what to write in their review.**

### 3. Tips for Using Language Strategically

This is the page's sentence-level engine — a bank of concrete rewrites.

**Mirror the language of the RFP and review criteria.** Use the funder's own evaluation vocabulary as sentence skeletons:

- "The **SIGNIFICANCE** of our results lies in..."
- "This approach is **FEASIBLE** because..."
- "This project is **INNOVATIVE** because..."
- "The **TEAM** is **WELL-QUALIFIED** to undertake this project because..."
- "This project will **ADVANCE KNOWLEDGE** by..."
- "The **BROADER IMPACTS** of this work are..."

**Project confidence — active and affirmative voice:**

| NOT | INSTEAD |
|---|---|
| "Spatial analysis will be conducted..." | "The PIs will conduct spatial analysis..." |
| "Research has been cited to demonstrate that an estimated..." | "Researchers estimate that..." |
| "The proposed work will *try to* understand..." | "The proposed work will *disentangle*..." |
| "We *hope* our findings will shed light on..." | "Our findings *will* shed light on..." |
| "I carry a heavy teaching load that leaves little time for research..." | "The proposed budget will enable me to devote time to advance this important work" |
| "X field has largely ignored Y variable" | "We will extend field X by examining the contributions of Y variable" |

Two principles inside this: use **affirmative language** ("will," not "try"); and **build on — don't tear down — the existing literature**, so the gap reads as an extension, not an attack.

**Every word serves a purpose — eliminate fluff:**

| NOT | INSTEAD |
|---|---|
| "are planned to" | "will" |
| "will allow" | "enable" |
| "the question of whether or not" | "whether" |
| "It is our expectation that" | "We expect" |
| "are dependent upon" | "depend on" |
| "all of the members of the Center" | "all Center members" |
| "It has been shown that" | "Jones (2019) found X and Y" |

Also: avoid empty verbs ("accomplished," "conducted," "facilitated," "implemented"); cut lengthy throat-clearing intros ("Indeed, it can be argued that...," "It is certainly worth noting, on the other hand, that..."); cut meaningless adjectives and unfettered enthusiasm ("exciting," "a lot," "really," "very").

**Avoid 'feel-good' platitudes.** Don't assume reviewers share your values: NOT *"We must save key species from extinction"*; INSTEAD *"X species plays a pivotal role in the sustainability of Y ecosystem."* Don't spend space on statements everyone already agrees with.

**Use precise language:**

- Explain the *how* — NOT "I will measure outcomes"; INSTEAD "I will measure X by Y metrics."
- Report comparisons ("more than," "better than," "improved") alongside their reference point.
- Use concrete verbs ("decrease," "increase"), not fuzzy ones ("change," "occur").

**Avoid jargon** — if you wouldn't use the word in conversation, don't use it; define technical terms. **Minimize acronyms** — aim for 3–4 total; they are hard to follow even when commonly used.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode:** [[Proposal-Writing-Agent]] — fast-pass scaffolding (DRAFT) and pre-submission checklist (AUDIT). This is a lightweight companion to the heavier Kraicer / Locke references, not a replacement.

**DRAFT mode — first-two-paragraphs scaffold.** Before anything else, draft the opening using the five-step funnel: general topic → two lit refs → "HOWEVER" pivot → gap (urgency + hero) → research question. This is the fastest way to get a defensible problem statement on the page; it pairs with the Proposal-Writing-Agent's specific-aims-first commitment.

**AUDIT mode — three concrete scans:**

1. **SMART-objectives scan** — every objective must be Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Reasonable, Time-bound. Flag any that is not.
2. **Fluff scan** — run the NOT→INSTEAD fluff table as a find-and-replace pass: "will allow" → "enable," "are dependent upon" → "depend on," "It has been shown that" → a named citation, and so on.
3. **Confidence scan** — flag every hedge ("try to," "hope," "attempt to") and every agentless passive ("analysis will be conducted"); rewrite with the PIs as the active subject.

**Mirror-the-criteria move.** When the solicitation/RFP is available, extract its review-criteria vocabulary (significance, feasibility, innovation, broader impacts, qualifications) and seed one labelled sentence per criterion — this operationalizes the Proposal-Writing-Agent's "every evaluation criterion a findable section" commitment.

**Delegation.** The sentence-level rewrite tables overlap heavily with Layer-0 craft — concision and fluff-cutting are owned by [[Generic-Writing-Agent]] ([[CMU-Concise-Sentences]], [[Strunk-White-Elements-Style]], [[Williams-Bizup-Style]]). This sheet's contribution is that it tunes those rules to the *grant register specifically* (confidence, affirmative voice, criteria-mirroring). Use it as the proposal-specific overlay; delegate generic concision to Layer 0.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Bilal's funding targets — NSF CAREER, AFOSR YIP, DoD MURI, SBIR/STTR — are exactly the "any peer-reviewed grant" audience this sheet addresses.

- **The funnel opening for a CTPC proposal:** general topic = the growing collision risk in an increasingly congested LEO environment (wide interest, no jargon); two lit refs = SGP4/TLE operational practice + recent physics-informed ML; the **"HOWEVER"** = current operational SDA tools produce uncalibrated covariances, so conjunction probabilities cannot be trusted; gap **urgency** = a missed or false conjunction warning has direct mission-loss cost; **hero narrative** = CTPC, a physics-as-architecture corrector, closes exactly this gap.
- **Mirror-the-criteria for each funder:** NSF wants "intellectual merit" and "broader impacts"; AFOSR wants "feasibility" and defense relevance; SBIR wants "commercialization." The criteria-vocabulary skeletons ("The BROADER IMPACTS of this work are...") should be instantiated per solicitation.
- **Confidence without overselling** is the precise tone for CTPC claims: "The architecture enforces dissipation as a hard constraint" (affirmative, true) — not "we hope the architecture will encourage dissipation" (hedged) and not "this solves uncertainty quantification for SDA" (oversold).
- **Build on — don't tear down** maps onto how SiS should frame SGP4: not "SGP4 is inadequate" but "CTPC extends SGP4 by learning the residual it leaves behind" — consistent with the CTPC predictor/corrector split, where SGP4 is kept as the physics predictor.

As writing-craft guidance, this is a soft, advisory document — `hard_constraint_possible: no`.

## Open Questions

1. Page 1's funnel is Karen Kelsky's template, written for humanities/social-science fellowship and dissertation grants. The "large general topic of wide interest" opening and the multi-page literature review fit NSF/NIH narrative proposals — but how does it compress into a 3-page AFOSR YIP or a 1-page SBIR Phase I problem statement? (Same compression question raised in [[Kraicer-Art-Of-Grantsmanship]].)
2. The "hero narrative" framing ("I will fill this gap!!!") is single-PI. For a multi-PI MURI or a SiS team proposal, does the hero become the team — and does that weaken the urgency hook?
3. The acronym ceiling ("3–4 total") is unrealistic for an SDA proposal (SGP4, TLE, RTN, CDM, SDA, UQ, LEO/GEO are all load-bearing). What is the right adaptation — a defined-terms box, or strict first-use-expansion discipline — for a heavily-acronymed technical domain?

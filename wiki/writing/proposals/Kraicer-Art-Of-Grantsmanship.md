# The Art of Grantsmanship (Kraicer)

## One-Line Intuition

Grantsmanship is the craft of writing peer-reviewed funding applications well enough that good science is not killed by poor presentation — because in a competitive field, writing quality routinely determines which proposals cross the funding cut-off.

---

## Why This Matters

Jacob Kraicer (MD, PhD, Professor of Physiology, University of Toronto) wrote this essay drawing on 30 years as a successful grant applicant, member of national and international reviewing bodies, and four years administering a peer-reviewed research grants program during which ~1,600 applications were processed. His core observation: with success rates at or below 50%, the proposals just below and just above the funding cut-off are scientifically indistinguishable. Writing quality — grantsmanship — is the decisive variable. Bad writing kills good science; good writing cannot save bad science, but bad writing reliably kills it.

The document is addressed to scientists writing **any peer-reviewed grant** (NSF, NIH, HFSP, NSERC, etc.). Its advice applies directly to NSF CAREER, AFOSR YIP, DoD MURI, and SBIR/STTR proposals that Bilal will be writing for SiS.

## Core Content

### 3.1 Before You Start to Write

A proposal does not begin with typing. The pre-writing phase is where fundability is built or lost.

#### Agency Alignment (Do This First)
- Read the Guidebook, Guidelines, and Application Forms **carefully** and follow them **exactly**. Get the **latest version**.
- Confirm that your proposal **"fits" the mission** of the agency. Make this fit **explicit** in the written application — do not assume reviewers will see it.
- Contact the agency program officer if you have any doubts. They want to help. Ask about:
  - The median funding level (use this to calibrate your budget)
  - What "sells" at that agency
- Find colleagues who have received grants from this agency — they provide insider knowledge on review culture and priorities.
- Find out (if you can) who the review committee members are and write accordingly.

#### Hypothesis Clarity (Before Drafting)
- Do you have a **clear, concise, testable hypothesis**?
- Are your objectives and aims coming into focus?
- What specific questions will be addressed?
- Can you design specific experiments that will **directly test the hypothesis**?

If these are not sharp, you are not ready to write.

#### Track Record Preparation
- Write up recent work and submit to peer-reviewed journals **well in advance** of the deadline.
- Published, in-press, or submitted manuscripts are acceptable. "In preparation" is typically **not acceptable**.
- Track record as judged by publications is an explicit reviewer criterion.

#### Preliminary Data
- Carry out pilot/preliminary studies before writing, especially for new applications.
- Preliminary data serve two functions: (1) demonstrate feasibility; (2) reveal pitfalls before reviewers find them for you.
- "A reviewer can think of a hundred reasons why something that you propose will not work. These objections vanish if you can show that you have done it."

#### Social Preparation
- Study successful grant proposals from colleagues — treat as models.
- Discuss your ideas with colleagues in the same and adjacent fields. Explanation forces clarity and exposes gaps in logic.
- Identify essential collaborators.

### 3.2 Timetable (Modified from Tutis Vilis, Survival Skills)

This timetable assumes a submission deadline as the reference point. It is the most concrete pre-submission schedule in the literature on grant writing.

| Timeline | Action |
|---|---|
| **12 months out** | Start thinking about the project; balance "sure" and "risky/innovative." Start reviewing literature. Complete current experiments; submit papers. Track record is the #1 element. |
| **9 months out** | Obtain preliminary data. Consider applying to local institution for pilot funding. Preliminary data are proposal-strengthening evidence that the approach works. |
| **6 months out** | Write the initial draft of the main proposal section. Requires ~1 month of intensive work: 3–6 hours/day, every day. Block this time off explicitly. Working a few hours per week will not produce a draft. |
| **5 months out** | Distribute draft to colleagues for comments. Do not accept "fantastic" — you want people who will read it word-by-word and flag every confusion. Sit down with each reviewer. Pay attention to what they **failed to understand**. Revise. Repeat. |
| **4 months out** | Submit proposed experiments to required local committees (animal care, human ethics, safety). This is time-gated and cannot be rushed. |
| **2 months out** | Reread guidelines and application. Take instructions seriously. Do what they ask. Work on ancillary parts: equipment quotes, collaborator letters, budget. |
| **1 month out** | Assemble final version on official forms, with figures and references. Circulate to colleagues again — obvious flaws become visible only when the whole package is seen together. |
| **2 weeks out** | Type the final version. Proofread. Have a fresh reader proof it. Do not trust spell check. |
| **1 week out** | Get the required number of copies made (assume the copy machine will break). Obtain all necessary signatures (up to 10% of applications are incomplete on the title page alone). |
| **2 days out** | Send by express mail/courier. Get some sleep. |

### 3.3 First / Title Page

- Fill in **completely and accurately**. Get all signatures. (~10% of applications have something missing here.)
- The **TITLE** is important:
  - Sets the **first impression**.
  - Used (with the Abstract) to **route the application** to the correct reviewers and committees.
  - Must be descriptive, specific, and appropriate, and must reflect the proposal's importance.
  - Should not be so specific that it requires changing at renewal.
  - **Recommended strategy:** Two-part title separated by a colon. The first part is general and stable across renewals; the second part changes. Example: "Neural network methods for space object tracking: physics-constrained propagation under observational uncertainty."

### 3.4 Abstract / Summary — The Most Important Section

**Kraicer's emphasis in the original is all-caps:**
> THE ABSTRACT SHOULD SERVE AS A SUCCINCT AND ACCURATE DESCRIPTION OF THE PROPOSAL EVEN WHEN IT IS SEPARATED FROM THE APPLICATION. IT MUST STAND ON ITS OWN.

This section warrants detailed treatment because it is the single highest-leverage page in the proposal.

#### Why the Abstract Is Critical
- It is probably the **most important section** in the application.
- It is **the first part read**, setting the first impression.
- It routes the application to the correct external reviewers, grants committee, and primary reviewers.
- It must be understood by **both experts and generalists**.
- Primary reviewers read the full application; **other committee members may only read the abstract** — yet ALL members may be required to give independent scores with equal weight to the primary reviewers' scores.
- Review committee members study applications weeks or months before meetings, then **quickly re-read abstracts just before the meeting** to recall the essentials.
- Write it **last** — after the bulk of the proposal is fine-tuned — but work on it extensively.

#### Required Contents of the Abstract
The abstract must contain, in compressed form:
1. **Hypotheses** to be tested
2. **Long-term objectives** — the scientific destination
3. **Specific aims** — the concrete steps in this funding period
4. **Research plan** — how you will do it (methods, design)
5. **Significance** — why this matters
6. **Relationship to agency mission** — explicitly stated
7. Why the proposal is **unique, important, and worth supporting**

#### Discipline
Stay within the allotted space. If you have nothing more to say, stop. Do not pad.

### 3.5 Recommended External Reviewers (If Requested)

- Think carefully. External reviewers are often used.
- They need not be Nobel laureates, but must be **recognized experts** in the field.
- They must be **tolerant of and sympathetic to** your hypothesis — a hostile expert will damage the review.
- If the form requests "fields of expertise," be specific (e.g., "orbital mechanics and Kalman filtering," not "applied mathematics").
- Must have an **arms-length relationship** with the applicant (defined by agency guidelines).
- Most agencies will also honor a request to **exclude specific reviewers** without requiring stated reasons — use this option if needed.

### 3.6 Proposed Research

#### 3.6.1 General Principles

- Keep the proposal **confined to the space allotted**.
- The proposal must be **focused, original, novel, innovative, and feasible**.
- Find a balance between **"sure" bets and risky/innovative** ideas.
- Set out **alternative strategies** in case the original approach fails — this preempts reviewer concerns.
- **Write and rewrite**: grantsmanship is rewriting.
- Use **diagrams, cartoons, and figures** liberally (a picture is worth a thousand words), but remember that copies will not appear in color.
- Make it a **joy to read**. You want reviewers to become your advocates, not your adversaries.
- Never justify a study by saying "it has never been done" or "there are no data on X" — that may be because the question is trivial.
- State clearly **what is novel** and **what is merely confirmatory**.
- State **explicitly how the proposal relates to the agency's mission**, objectives, and priorities.
- Use **clear headings and sub-headings** with a simple, obvious numerical classification.
- Cite potential external reviewers and committee members where appropriate — but not excessively.

#### 3.6.2 Specific Structural Plan

Kraicer recommends breaking the proposed research into the following sections, in order:

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Hypothesis and Long-Term Objectives | The "why" — testable claim and scientific destination |
| Specific Aims | The "what" — concrete deliverables in this grant period |
| Background and Significance | The "where we are" — gap analysis and rationale |
| Progress / Preliminary Studies | The "why us" — track record and feasibility evidence |
| Research Design and Methods | The "how" — experimental design per aim |
| Timetable | Sequencing and milestones |
| Strengths and Weaknesses | Self-aware critique — preempts reviewer objections |

##### 3.6.2.1 Hypothesis and Long-Term Objectives

- A **testable hypothesis-driven proposal is best**; primarily descriptive proposals are less favorably received.
- Begin with the stated hypothesis; tie it to the long-term objectives.
- Answer explicitly: What is the proposed research intended to accomplish? What is its significance and relevance?

##### 3.6.2.2 Specific Aims

- Distinguish from the long-term objectives: Specific Aims are the concrete projects, studies, and items that will be undertaken **to fulfill** the long-term objectives.
- Put them in a **logical and sequential order**.
- **Indicate priorities** — which aims are essential, which are exploratory?

##### 3.6.2.3 Background and Significance: Current State of Knowledge

This section must answer **three questions**:
1. What is known?
2. What is not known?
3. Why is it **essential** to find out?

How to execute:
- Begin with a brief outline of highlights in the background review.
- State where your own previous contributions fit in.
- **Critically evaluate** the relevant literature — not a compendium or list, but critical synthesis.
- Discuss fairly all sides of controversies, disagreements, and discrepancies in published results. (Caution: a participant in a controversy may be your reviewer.)
- **Identify specifically the gaps and contradictions** that your proposal will clarify.
- Carry this into the **rationale** for your proposal.
- Emphasize the importance and relevance of your proposal in bridging hypotheses and long-term objectives to the background review.
- Integrate your previous findings into the background to give reviewers a sense of your relevant contributions.

##### 3.6.2.4 Progress (as Related to Background and Significance)

This section differs depending on whether it is a renewal or new application.

**For a renewal:**
- Remind reviewers of the start and end dates of the previous award.
- Establish credibility of excellence in research and continuity of high quality.
- Summarize previous hypotheses, long-term objectives, and specific aims; give a succinct description of progress, emphasizing the most important and relevant findings.
- Describe how your specific aims may have changed as the work progressed — evolution is normal.
- Incorporate all publications, submitted/accepted manuscripts, and abstracts (if permitted) produced during the grant term.
- Convince reviewers, "in as subtle a way as possible," that your recent contributions were outstanding and of great importance.
- Ask: How has your work significantly advanced knowledge in the field? How will the proposal continue this record of achievement and excellence?
- **Do not complain about previously low or inadequate funding** — this is self-defeating.

**For a new application:**
- Convince reviewers of your excellent and relevant training and that you already have substantive **preliminary data and/or pilot studies**.
- Summarize relevant previous work, highlighting unique qualifications and skills.
- Review preliminary studies and results. **Present the actual data** — this establishes experience, competence, and credibility.
- List publications and submitted/accepted manuscripts (if permitted).

**For both:**
- If allowed, list all publications, abstracts, and other retrievable material related to your proposal.

##### 3.6.2.5 Preliminary Data / Studies

- Of **great importance** — include either in Background, in Progress, or as a separate section.
- Tie it directly to the hypotheses and long-term objectives.
- Show the **actual data** — not a narrative summary.
- Especially critical for new applications: it documents the credibility, experience, and competence of both the proposal and the proposer.

##### 3.6.2.6 Research Design and Methods

The Specific Aims state what you propose; this section describes how.

Key structural rules:
- Number research designs and methods to **correspond to the numbers of the Specific Aims**.
- Use **sub-numbering** within each part when describing several methods applicable to the same aim.
- Distinguish clearly between overall research design and specific methods.
- Do not repeat identical procedures that apply to more than one aim.
- **Reference but do not describe** well-known or standard procedures; **do describe** procedures that are new or unlikely to be known to reviewers.
- For new methods, **explain why they are better** than existing methods.
- Discuss **relevant control experiments** (this is often lacking in weak proposals).
- Explain the processes for **data collection, analysis, and interpretation**.
- Discuss potential difficulties and limitations of proposed procedures, and give **alternative procedures** to achieve the aims — this prevents criticisms and may "save" your application.
- **State clearly possible weaknesses and/or ambiguities and respond** to them — preempt the criticisms.
- Provide a brief tentative **sequence and timetable** for the project. List in order. Be realistic. Consider a diagram or table. Define priorities.
- Document all proposed **collaborative arrangements**, including letters from collaborators confirming specifics. The role of each collaborator must be clearly defined. Biographic sketches or relevant experience should be included in collaborator letters.

### 3.7 Budget

- In most agencies, review committee members are required to recommend an appropriate budget **independent of the scientific merit** of the proposal.
- The budget **stands alone** from the research proposal; everyone on the review committee participates in budget discussion (unlike the science, where only primary reviewers lead).
- Budget is typically considered **last**, after the scientific merit has been scored.
- Committees are often under obligation to reduce budgets — therefore:
  - Make the budget **well-documented, realistic, appropriate, and justified**.
  - Do not inflate, overbudget, or underbudget.
- Check carefully which items the agency supports (secretariat assistance, travel, books, etc.). **Do not request items that are not allowed**.
- Provide **sufficient details** for each item to make it difficult for reviewers to suggest arbitrary major cuts.
- **For equipment:** document convincingly why the piece is essential (not just "nice to have" or "faster and better"), and why the specified model is required.
- **For personnel:**
  - Make sure they are allowed.
  - Specify the unique and essential role each will play, and state how their qualifications are matched to the role.
  - Avoid "to be named" if possible.
- **For travel:** specify who will travel, to which meetings, and whether they will be presenting a paper. Justify requests for more than one meeting per year per person.

### 3.8 Other Grants Received and/or Pending

- Be honest and complete. The agency can verify from independent sources.
- Be careful if stating "no overlap." A more defensible formulation: "There are certain similarities in the systems and/or methods but there is no overlap in specific aims or objectives."

---

### 3.9 Appended Documents

- Include all required appendices; include allowed optional material only if supportive but not integral.
- The application without appendices **must stand on its own merit**.
- **Do not include documents if they are not required** — they will not be distributed to reviewers.
- A common mistake: attempting to extend beyond the page limit via an appendix. Unless specifically allowed, the appendix will not reach reviewers. Referring to an appendix that reviewers never receive leaves a gap or hole in the application.

---

### 3.10 Publications

- Many reviewers tend to "weigh" or "count" publications rather than assess quality, significance, and contribution — this is a reality to manage.
- Aim for a good number of **first-authored publications in first-order peer-reviewed journals**.
- A high ratio of abstracts to full-length papers is **not well received**.
- Other publication types (books, chapters, reviews, non-peer-reviewed articles) may not impress reviewers.

### 4. Common Errors Made

#### 4.1 By New Applicants

| Error | Description |
|---|---|
| Unrealistic scope | The proposal includes a lifetime's worth of work; no clearly defined priorities; timetable (if present) is unrealistic |
| Uncritical literature review | Reads like an undergraduate review — a compendium, not a critical synthesis |
| No preliminary data | No pilot studies or other preliminary data |
| Insufficient research time | Time listed to be spent on research should be at least 50%, preferably >75%; below 50% is unacceptable (smaller percent may be acceptable for established investigators) |
| Unrealistic budget | Budget is not calibrated to what the agency funds or what the work actually requires |

#### 4.2 By Established Investigators

| Error | Description |
|---|---|
| Fragmented application | Different parts obviously written by different junior colleagues, then hastily assembled — the proposal is disjointed |
| Resting on reputation | "I don't have to go into detail. Trust me and examine my track record. Rely on my reputation." This no longer works. |
| Too cautious | Proposals tend to be "more of the same" — they do not venture into new and unexplored areas |

### 5. The Review Process (Appendix) — Detailed Anatomy

This is Kraicer's most valuable section for understanding the reviewer's perspective, which every applicant must internalize.

#### Stage 1: Secretariat Pre-Screening

Before a reviewer ever sees the application, the agency secretariat screens for:
- Missing critical information or signatures
- Inappropriate format (type size, spacing, margins)
- Number of pages exceeding the allowed limit
- Application does not "fit" with the mission/objectives of the agency
- Missing sections
- Applicant does not qualify
- Extra (not required) information is included

Applications failing these checks may be rejected outright or returned for additional information. **~25% of applications at some agencies were incomplete.** A sloppy application signals a sloppy scientist.

#### Stage 2: External Reviewers

- Applications are assigned to external reviewers chosen from: (a) names recommended by the applicant, (b) names recommended by review committee members, (c) agency database.
- External reviewers submit **extensive written reviews** — available to review committee members before the meeting.
- The review format external reviewers follow typically includes:
  1. A concise summary paragraph emphasizing significance
  2. An evaluation of previously completed work (for renewals)
  3. An assessment of strengths and weaknesses across these specific dimensions:
     - Originality of hypotheses and significance of questions asked
     - Feasibility
     - Relationship to the applicant's previous work
     - Appropriateness of the critical review of the literature
     - Scientific and intellectual environment
     - Applicant's knowledge of the field as reflected in the literature reviewed
     - Appropriateness of the research plan and methodology
     - Significance of work done previously and potential of proposed work to elucidate new and important knowledge
     - Appropriateness of the budget

#### Stage 3: Primary Reviewers (Inside the Committee)

- Each application is assigned to **two primary reviewers** from the review committee for detailed analysis.
- They may or may not be experts in your exact field.
- They may or may not submit written reports.
- **Only the two primary reviewers may be required to study the entire application.** Other committee members may only receive the abstract/summary pages.
- This is why the abstract is critical: other committee members are scoring from the abstract alone.

#### Stage 4: The Review Committee Meeting

- Each application receives **no more than 15 minutes of discussion**.
- The two primary reviewers introduce the application and give their evaluations; external reviews are analyzed and comments made.
- Other committee members then participate in discussion.
- A **Final Score and/or Rating** is made; a **rank order** is decided on the basis of scientific excellence.
- All then participate in the **budget discussion**; a final budget recommendation is made.
- The committee knows the global budget available. **Demands for funding often far outweigh the funds available.** Many very good proposals fall below the cut-off. There will be "painful discussion concerning the 'trade off' of size of budget per application vs. number of applications funded."

#### Stage 5: Higher Body Review

- The review committee's recommendations are reported to a "higher body" (e.g., council, board of directors).
- The higher body usually accepts the rank order but debates budget further.
- This becomes most difficult when the cut-off is too high, with many very good applications being rejected.

#### Key Implication for Writing

The review process means:
- The **abstract is scored by people who may not read the rest** — make it complete and compelling.
- The **application must be readable in bits-and-pieces** — reviewers read under non-ideal conditions (evenings, weekends, holidays, at meetings, on planes). You cannot assume linear reading.
- You want reviewers to become **enthusiastic champions and advocates**. A lukewarm review is fatal.
- Budget reviewers are everyone — budget for this audience.

### 3.1 General Presentation Conventions (Synthesized)

These are scattered across sections 3.1 and 3.6 but warrant a consolidated list:

| Rule | Rationale |
|---|---|
| "A joy to read" — organize for non-linear reading | Reviewers read in bits-and-pieces; cannot go back to the beginning after each break |
| Avoid abbreviations, acronyms, and jargon | Non-experts on the committee; some agencies send abroad |
| Write for a reviewer in a somewhat related field, not a direct expert | The primary reviewer may not be an expert; generalists score too |
| Use a dictionary, not spell-check | "If you can't get the spelling right, how are you expected to get the research right?" |
| Extensive and intensive internal peer review is essential | Get at least two colleagues: one expert (content), one generalist (clarity), one good editor |
| Get free of mechanical errors before sending to internal reviewers | If reviewers are distracted by typos they may fail to identify fundamental problems |
| Give internal reviewers enough time to do a thorough job | |
| Do not submit additional information after the deadline | Many agencies will not accept late additions |
| Do not go over the maximum number of pages allowed | Many agencies will not accept applications that are one page too many |

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Primary mode: Structuring + Auditing.** This document is the most comprehensive proposal-structure reference in the corpus. The writing agent should deploy it in two primary modes:

### Structuring Mode (Early Draft)
When Bilal begins a new proposal (NSF CAREER, AFOSR YIP, DoD STTR Phase I, etc.):
1. Instantiate the 8-section structure from §3.6.2: Hypothesis and Long-Term Objectives → Specific Aims → Background and Significance → Progress/Preliminary Studies → Research Design and Methods → Timetable → Strengths and Weaknesses.
2. Use the timetable (§3.2) to set a realistic writing schedule backward from the submission deadline.
3. Draft the title using the two-part colon structure (§3.3) — stable general part, specific modifiable part.
4. Write the abstract **last** (§3.4) — but use the 7-element required-content checklist as a quality gate.

### Auditing Mode (Revision Pass)
When a draft exists, use this document to run a systematic audit against:
- **Abstract completeness audit:** does the abstract contain all 7 required elements? Can it stand alone?
- **Section-by-section audit:** does Background and Significance answer the 3 required questions (what is known, what is not known, why essential)?
- **Common errors audit:** check the new-applicant and established-investigator error tables against the draft.
- **Reviewer-perspective audit:** would a reviewer who only reads the abstract be able to give this proposal a fair score? Is the application readable non-linearly?
- **Budget audit:** is each line item justified with specifics? Are any disallowed items present?
- **Pre-submission checklist:** run the secretariat pre-screening checklist (§5, Stage 1) before submission.

### Concrete Moves
- Flag any sentence justifying a study with "it has never been done" or "there are no data on X" — replace with gap analysis.
- Flag any claim of "no overlap" with other grants — replace with the nuanced formulation from §3.8.
- Flag any appendix reference where the appendix may not be distributed to reviewers.
- Add "alternative strategies" sub-section to any aim that lacks one.
- Add "control experiments" sub-section to any methods section that lacks one.
- Verify the reviewer exclusion list is populated (§3.5) if the form allows it.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Bilal's primary funding targets are: **NSF CAREER**, **AFOSR Young Investigator Program (YIP)**, **DoD MURI** (multi-investigator), and **SBIR/STTR Phase I/II** for SiS commercialization. The Kraicer framework maps directly to these:

### Agency Mission Alignment (§2, §3.6.1)
- **NSF:** Broader Impacts must be explicit; CTPC must be framed in terms of scientific contribution to the field, not just commercial SDA value.
- **AFOSR:** SDA is a DoD priority area; the match between CTPC's physics-constrained neural propagation and AFOSR's "trustworthy AI for space systems" programs must be stated explicitly in both abstract and background sections.
- **SBIR/STTR:** The agency requires commercialization potential. The SiS runway from CTPC research to a TRL-6+ deployed system in the SSA/SDA market must be described explicitly.

### Hypothesis-Driven Framing (§3.6.2.1)
CTPC work should be framed as hypothesis-driven, not descriptive. Example formulation: "We hypothesize that encoding orbital mechanics as hard architectural priors in a neural corrector achieves statistically calibrated covariance propagation under atmospheric drag uncertainty that is not achievable by soft-constraint PINN approaches." This is testable and specific.

### Preliminary Data (§3.6.2.4–3.6.2.5)
For new applications: the CTPC architecture work, the SGP4 residual characterization, and any TLE pipeline benchmarks constitute the preliminary data portfolio. **Present actual numbers** — not narrative claims about what the approach can do.

### Strengths and Weaknesses / Alternative Strategies (§3.6.2.6)
For SiS proposals, known weaknesses to preempt proactively:
- Computational cost of physics-architecture networks vs. pure-ML baselines
- Dependence on TLE data quality from Space-Track
- Generalization across orbital regimes (LEO vs. GEO vs. HEO)

For each, provide an alternative strategy or mitigation plan. This demonstrates scientific maturity.

### Budget (§3.7)
For SBIR Phase I (~$300K over 6 months): Kraicer's budget advice directly applies. Every line must be documented. Personnel (Bilal as PI + graduate student/postdoc) must have explicitly defined roles. Equipment (GPU compute) must be justified as essential, not merely faster.

### Track Record (§3.10)
For early-career proposals: first-authored papers in peer-reviewed venues (ICML, NeurIPS, JGCD, ASR) carry the most weight. Abstracts and ArXiv preprints alone will not impress reviewers.

## Open Questions

1. **How does the Kraicer structure map specifically onto AFOSR YIP solicitation format?** AFOSR YIP has a 3-page technical proposal + 2-page summary. The 8-section Kraicer structure must be severely compressed. Which sections can be merged or abbreviated?

2. **What is the NSF CAREER "Broader Impacts" analog to Kraicer's agency-mission-fit requirement?** Kraicer assumes a single-program fit. NSF evaluates research merit AND broader impacts as co-equal criteria — how should the abstract allocate space across both?

3. **Preliminary data threshold:** For a PhD student applying for their first independent grant (NSF CAREER), what constitutes sufficient preliminary data? Kraicer says "show that you have done it" — but you may not have had resources to run the full experiment yet. Consult [[Locke-Proposals-That-Work]] for more on this.

4. **The "alternative strategies" requirement in defense proposals:** SBIR reviewers are evaluating feasibility and risk mitigation. Does Kraicer's advice to include alternative procedures align with or conflict with the SBIR preference for confident, commercially-focused narratives?

5. **Timetable realism for dual-role PI:** Bilal is simultaneously a PhD student and CTO. Kraicer's 6-month, 3–6 hours/day intensive writing block assumes uninterrupted focus time. How should the timetable be adapted for a split-role principal investigator?

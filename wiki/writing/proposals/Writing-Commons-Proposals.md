# Writing Commons — Proposals

## One-Line Intuition
A proposal is a persuasive argument that defines a problem, presents a specific solution, establishes the writer's credibility to carry it out, and asks readers to act or fund — whether the context is academic research, journalism, consulting, or government grants.

## Why This Matters
Proposals are the primary funding instrument for academic researchers and deep-tech startups. Every NSF CAREER award, AFOSR grant, SBIR Phase I/II, and DoD contract starts as a written proposal. Understanding the genre — its purposes, audiences, rhetorical moves, and key features — directly determines whether SiS secures the resources to build CTPC and pursue Space Domain Awareness research. This source (Writing Commons, authored by Joseph M. Moxley, 2023) is an open peer-reviewed treatment of proposal writing as a genre, organized around rhetorical analysis and practical key features.

---

## Core Content

### 1. What Is a Proposal?

A proposal is a **persuasive text** that articulates ways to solve a problem, conduct needed research, or provide a service. Proposals may:

- Attempt to persuade readers to act (call for action)
- Seek funding to conduct promised work
- Offer to trade services for money or goods

Writers support claims with reasoning, library and internet research, original research (questionnaires, interviews, ethnography). Crucially: **even a proposal that seeks funding to conduct research must be firmly grounded in prior research** — you must conduct research to craft a credible proposal, even if the goal is to secure funding for more research.

**Core definition (Moxley, p. 8):** Proposals typically outline a problem, detail solutions to the problem, and define the costs for solving the problem. They provide information about the qualifications of the person or people suggesting the solution. When funds are sought, a detailed budget is provided. More formal proposals contain evaluation information — a plan to evaluate the success of the proposal once implemented.

### 2. Three Major Proposal Types (Diverse Rhetorical Situations)

| Type | Description | Typical Media |
|---|---|---|
| **Research Proposals** | Outline a topic, describe its significance, present a schedule for conducting research. Used in college classes, professional settings, laboratories. | Lab reports, research reports, NSF-style formatted submissions |
| **Essay / Editorial Proposals** | Go beyond arguing one side — they present a call for action. Published as editorials or essays to influence public opinion. | Magazines, newspapers, op-eds |
| **Consulting Proposals** | Seek funding for necessary services; a business selling its capabilities to a client or government agency (e.g., environmental consulting firm selling a water contamination report to the EPA). | External proposals, formal bids |

**Rhetorical dimension matrix (p. 6):**

| Purposes | Audiences | Voices/Persona | Media |
|---|---|---|---|
| Call for specific action | Corporation | Professional | Letters |
| Conduct research | Foundation | Academic researcher | Memos |
| Provide services | Business | Activist | External proposal |
| | Newspaper | Persuasive | Editorial, Email, Web sites |

**Scale:** Proposals can be page-length or book-length (hundreds of pages). They can be essay form, internal memo, or online submission (e.g., NSF's FastLane/Research.gov system). In the U.S., university research is largely funded by government agencies and private foundations — the Community of Science database identifies $33 billion in funding opportunities; IRIS is another major source.

### 3. What Are Proposals? (Structural Definition)

Every proposal addresses these structural elements, scaled to its formality:

1. **Problem statement** — outline the problem, including severity and urgency
2. **Solution(s)** — detail specific, step-by-step activities and objectives
3. **Methods** — explain how the work will be carried out
4. **Costs / Budget** — define the costs; associate activities with dollar figures when seeking funds
5. **Qualifications** — establish that the author/team has the skills, resources, and experience
6. **Evaluation plan** — in formal proposals: a plan to evaluate success once implemented

### 4. Key Features of Proposals (The Practical Checklist)

The source presents key features as "considerations" rather than a rigid blueprint, because a proposal seeking public policy change differs structurally from one seeking research funding. The six major feature areas are:

#### 4.1 Focus
Bring focus by:
- Highlighting the **urgency** of the problem
- Providing **evidence** readers need to believe the proposed solution can work
- Adopting an appropriate voice and persona (read sample proposals in your target venue to calibrate)

#### 4.2 Development — Define the Problem(s)

The problem definition section can range from a single sentence (for an obvious problem) to many pages (for a subtle or contentious one). Some writers omit the problem statement entirely when they judge it obvious to their audience.

**Two reader questions to answer in the introduction (p. 11):**
1. Who benefits from the proposal? Will the project have significant impact?
2. Who is submitting the proposal? What is their interest? Are they qualified?

**How to answer them:** Provide specifics — statistics, quotes from authorities, results from past research, interviews, questionnaires. Use striking, concrete data to establish the stakes.

**Example technique (p. 11):** "Stun readers in the introduction with gruesome statistics" — the Summers AIDS proposal opens with: "Three hundred million people live on less than US$1 per day. Life expectancy is 48 years and falling. More than one-third of all children are malnourished..."

**Scaling rule:** Obvious problems can be defined briefly. Subtle problems may need considerable development. Writers sometimes view the problem as so obvious to their audience they skip the introduction entirely (e.g., the Union of Concerned Scientists' "What You Can Do About Global Warming").

#### 4.3 Development — Define the Method(s)

How will you gather information? Secondary or primary research?

- **Humanities:** writers often do not explicitly state methods
- **Sciences and social sciences:** methods are explicitly stated

Three method scenarios:
1. **Conducting research** → explain: internet/library research? Interviews with authorities?
2. **Laboratory research** → demonstrate: access to lab and tools needed
3. **Proposing a service** → demonstrate: capacity to actually provide the service

#### 4.4 Development — Present the Solution(s)

Successful proposals are not vague. They outline **step-by-step activities and objectives**, potentially associating each activity with a dollar figure.

**Critical readers are predisposed toward inaction** (inaction costs nothing; action involves risk). They will ask three questions about the proposed solution:

1. **Can it work?** Is the solution technically and practically feasible?
2. **How much time will it take** to complete the proposal/project?
3. **Will other factors resolve the problem over time anyway?** (i.e., is the problem urgent enough to justify action now?)

**Organizational note:** Most research/service proposals use **classical argument structure** — state problem briefly, propose solution. For controversial solutions, **inductive organization** works better — build the case first, reveal the solution in the middle, as in Trager's libertarian airport-security proposal (p. 21-22), which critiques three competing solutions before presenting four of his own.

#### 4.5 Appeal to Character / Persona (Ethos)

This is the most important rhetorical element for funded proposals. The persona you project determines whether reviewers trust you to deliver.

**How reviewers assess character:**
- How you define the problem
- How you consider counterarguments
- How you marshal support for claims

**Opening sentences establish persona and tone** — they frame the entire proposal's credibility.

**Two modes:**
- **Formal institutional voice** (e.g., ACLU): "We at the Brown American Civil Liberties Union are proposing the following changes to the Code of Student Conduct in an effort to ensure a consistent, unambiguous, and Constitutionally acceptable disciplinary code..." (p. 15) — note: acknowledges readers' concerns head-on, then pivots.
- **Personal voice** (undergraduate research): "I am very enthusiastic to continue working with the multidisciplinary team... Because I am a student majoring in economics, minoring in psychology, and I possess a strong interest in management science, this multidisciplinary research initiative... really feels like a 'nice fit'..." (p. 17-18) — note: makes personal qualifications and alignment explicit.

**When proposing a service or research project:** readers want to ensure the author has the resources, skills, and experience necessary to successfully provide the service / complete the project. Establish this directly.

**Critical readers wonder:** How does the author/organization benefit from this proposal? Why are they presenting it? Addressing this transparently builds credibility.

#### 4.6 Appeal to Emotion (Pathos)

Proposals are more firmly grounded in logic and character than emotion. Appeals to emotion can seem unethical or unprofessional to critical readers.

**Strategic guidance:**
- Slip emotional appeals into the **introduction and conclusion** — be discreet
- Modern, well-educated reviewers see through manipulative emotional appeals; keep the **bulk grounded in research and logic**
- Emotional appeals are appropriate at the opening to establish stakes, but should not dominate

**Emotional appeal sub-types (use cautiously):**
- **Appeals to authority** ("According to the EPA, global warming will raise sea levels")
- **Appeals to pity** — use sparingly; can undermine credibility
- **Ad hominem attacks** — never; rhetorically disqualifying

#### 4.7 Appeal to Logic (Logos)

Successful proposals are firmly grounded in logic. Educated reviewers expect:
- Logical reasoning
- Personal observations
- Expert testimony
- Facts and statistics
- Results from past research
- Citations of authorities

**Add depth** by citing authorities, interviewing experts, and researching past attempts to solve the problem.

#### 4.8 Consider Counterarguments

In professional grant contexts, writers compete with hundreds or thousands of others for limited pages. Each word is precious.

**When to address counterarguments:**
- When readers may find alternative solutions more feasible
- When the proposed solution is unusual or controversial
- When the audience is skeptical

Counterargument treatment can occupy the bulk of a proposal — e.g., Trager's proposal critiques three mainstream airport-security proposals at length before advancing his four solutions (inductive structure, p. 21-22).

**When to skip:** straightforward proposals to receptive audiences — devote space to your solution instead.

#### 4.9 Use Visuals

Readers respond positively to visual representations of proposals. Visuals engage readers at a visual level and let them "see" the proposal before reading it in full.

**Practical use:** Timelines (Gantt charts), org charts, budget breakdowns, figures of proposed architectures, diagrams of methods.

#### 4.10 Chunk Your Contents

Consider chunking — structural segmentation that aids skimmability. Some proposals call for:
- 50-word abstract
- 500-word executive summary
- Section-by-section breakdown with headers

Many funding agencies provide formatting templates; chunking follows those requirements while serving readers who scan before reading.

#### 4.11 Organization

Two primary organizational patterns:

| Pattern | When to Use |
|---|---|
| **Classical argument** (deductive) | Most research and consulting proposals — state problem, then propose solution. Default for most funders. |
| **Inductive** | Controversial solutions — build case first, reveal solution midway. Useful when readers may resist the proposed solution if stated up front. |

#### 4.12 Style

Make the proposal persuasive through:
- **Unambiguous, concrete language**
- **Appeals to the reader's senses** — make abstract technical work tangible
- **Given-to-new information flow** — relate new concepts to what readers already know (see [[CMU-Known-New-Information-Flow]])
- **Moving from known to new** — readers accept new claims more readily when anchored to what they already believe

### 5. Rhetorical Analysis Framework for Reading Sample Proposals

Before writing your own proposal, read samples and analyze:

- **Context** — what prompted this proposal? What is the urgency?
- **Audience** — who decides? Corporation, foundation, government, newspaper editorial board?
- **Purpose** — call for action? Funding request? Service offering?
- **Media** — letter, memo, online submission, editorial?
- **How ideas are developed** — personal experience? Authority citations? Questionnaires? Empirical research?
- **Persona** — professional? Activist? Academic researcher? Persuasive?

Use this framework when studying successful funded proposals (NSF publishes sample proposals; NEH publishes successful proposals in DOC/HTML/PDF; EPA provides hundreds of sample proposals).

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode: Proposal-Writing-Agent (Layer 1b)**

This source provides the genre-level scaffold for every grant proposal Bilal writes. Use it at two points in the proposal workflow:

### Planning phase (before drafting)
1. **Identify the proposal type** — research, essay/editorial, or consulting. For NSF/AFOSR/SBIR: research proposal with consulting elements.
2. **Map the funder's audience** — who reviews? Program officers? Peer review panels? What is their domain (space science, AI, national security)?
3. **Choose organization pattern** — classical (default) or inductive (if the proposed approach is unconventional or likely to face resistance).
4. **Plan the persona** — institutional/professional voice for NSF/AFOSR; can add personal voice in researcher bio sections.

### Drafting phase (section by section)
Use the key-features checklist as a drafting rubric:

| Proposal Section | Key Feature to Apply |
|---|---|
| Abstract / Project Summary | Chunk; 50–word limit common; Focus move |
| Introduction / Problem Statement | Define problem; statistics; urgency; reader questions (who benefits? who is submitting?) |
| Background / Significance | Appeal to Logic; cite authorities; show prior research |
| Approach / Methods | Define methods explicitly; show access to tools/data |
| Expected Outcomes / Results | Present solution step-by-step; associate with timeline milestones |
| Broader Impacts / Significance | Appeal to Emotion (discreetly); who benefits? national/societal impact |
| Team Qualifications / Bio | Appeal to Character/Persona; establish credentials |
| Budget | Associate activities with dollar figures; be specific |
| Evaluation Plan | Include in formal proposals — plan to measure success |
| Counterarguments (if needed) | Address competing approaches; acknowledge skeptical readers |

### Revision phase
- Verify every solution claim is concrete and step-by-step (not vague)
- Check that opening sentences project the right persona
- Ensure given-to-new information flow in technical sections
- Add visuals (architecture diagrams, timelines) — reviewers respond positively
- Strip emotional appeals except in intro/conclusion framing

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

SiS's primary funders will be NSF, AFOSR, DARPA, and SBIR/STTR programs. Every engagement is a proposal-writing problem:

**Problem statement (SiS frame):** Current SSA relies on SGP4 with TLE-based orbit propagation that produces physically unrealistic covariance estimates, degrading conjunction analysis. The gap between physics-predictor fidelity and real orbital dynamics is documented but unaddressed in deployed systems. This is the "problem" that every SiS proposal must define — with statistics (miss-distance errors, false-alarm rates, conjunction probability errors).

**Solution specificity (Moxley's key feature applied):** CTPC must be described with step-by-step activities: (1) Deploy dissipation-enforced PHNN corrector in RTN frame; (2) Validate on public TLE catalog; (3) Benchmark against CARA Pc computation. Associate each with timeline and budget line.

**Persona moves for SiS:**
- Bilal's dual role (PhD researcher + CTO) must be framed carefully — reviewers for NSF/NSF SBIR want academic rigor; DoD/AFOSR want operational relevance. The persona should project both.
- Coordinated Systems Lab affiliation at Iowa State establishes academic credibility; SiS startup establishes transition pathway (critical for SBIR).
- Acknowledge the "why are they proposing this?" question directly: SiS has operational skin in the game — this is not just academic interest.

**Counterargument handling (SiS specific):** Physics-informed ML for SDA is contested. Reviewers may prefer purely analytical approaches or commercial SSA solutions. Address these alternatives directly:
- Pure analytical: fails to model unmodeled perturbations
- Commercial solutions (LeoLabs, ExoAnalytic): black-box, no uncertainty calibration
- CTPC's hard-constraint architecture answers the interpretability and reliability objections simultaneously

**Organizational pattern:** Use classical argument for NSF CAREER / AFOSR YIP (reviewers are sophisticated; state the approach upfront). Use inductive for DoD program managers unfamiliar with physics-ML (build the operational problem case first, reveal the ML solution later).

**Funders and their purposes:**
- **NSF** — conduct research; needs academic framing, broader impacts, educational component
- **AFOSR** — conduct research with national security relevance; SDA is a direct fit
- **DoD SBIR Phase I** — consulting proposal + research; needs commercialization path
- **DARPA** — high-risk/high-reward; aggressive problem statement, bold solution, counterintuitive approach welcomed

## Open Questions

1. **NSF-specific persona calibration:** Moxley identifies persona as the load-bearing element, but what specific persona signals do NSF program officers in CISE/ENG weight most heavily? Does the dual academic+startup identity help or hurt for NSF CAREER?

2. **SBIR counterargument strategy:** SBIR reviewers include both technical and commercial evaluators. How should counterarguments be split — technical objections in the technical volume, commercial objections in the commercialization volume?

3. **Inductive vs. classical for DARPA BAAs:** DARPA explicitly rewards unconventional approaches. Does the inductive pattern (build case, reveal solution late) backfire with DARPA reviewers who want to see the bold claim immediately?

4. **Budget line-activity association:** Moxley says successful proposals associate specific activities with dollar figures. What level of granularity is appropriate for NSF vs. AFOSR vs. SBIR budgets? NSF requires budget justifications; SBIR requires cost-volume detail.

5. **Evaluation plan for ML systems:** For CTPC, what constitutes a credible "evaluation plan" in a proposal context? Trajectory prediction accuracy benchmarks? Conjunction probability calibration metrics? Reviewers unfamiliar with ML may not know what good evaluation looks like.

6. **Emotional appeal placement in SBIR:** SBIR has a commercialization section that is structurally similar to a call-for-action proposal. Does Moxley's guidance to place emotional appeals in intro/conclusion extend to the commercialization narrative, or does that section warrant a different register?

# Proposals That Work (4th ed., Chapter 1) — Locke, Spirduso & Silverman

## One-Line Intuition

A research proposal is simultaneously a communication document, an action plan, and a binding contract — and its quality as a plan is the single best predictor of whether the research will succeed.

## Why This Matters

The PDF is a single-chapter excerpt: **Chapter 1, "The Function of the Proposal"** from *Proposals That Work: A Guide for Planning Dissertations and Grant Proposals*, Fourth Edition (Sage Publications, 2000), by Lawrence F. Locke, Waneen Wyrick Spirduso, and Stephen J. Silverman. The book's table of contents is also reproduced, indicating the full book spans at least two major parts with chapters on writing, developing the proposal, qualitative research, grant proposals, and specimen proposals with commentary. This excerpt covers pages 3–23 of the published book.

This chapter is the foundational theory of what a proposal IS — its purposes, its regulations, its general format requirements, and the specific communication tasks every proposal section must perform. It defines the vocabulary and standards that the rest of the book applies.

## Core Content

### 1. The Three Functions of a Proposal

Every proposal performs three simultaneous functions:

#### 1a. Communication
- The proposal is the **primary resource** on which thesis/dissertation committees base their review, consultation, and approval decisions.
- For grant agencies, it is the document on which funding decisions rest.
- Quality of assistance, economy of consultation, and probability of financial support all depend **directly on clarity and thoroughness**.

#### 1b. Plan
- A proposal is a plan for systematic, preplanned observation of a restricted phenomenon.
- The acceptability of research results is judged **exclusively** by the adequacy of the methods for making, recording, and interpreting planned observations.
- The plan is the basis on which the thesis, dissertation, or research report will be judged.
- **Hallmark of a good proposal:** "a level of thoroughness and detail sufficient to permit another investigator to replicate the study, that is, to perform the same planned observations with results not substantially different from those the author might obtain." (p. 4)
- Exception: plans for qualitative research (see book's Chapter 5) operate under different standards.

#### 1c. Contract
- An approved, signed proposal constitutes a **bond of agreement** between student and advisors.
- A grant proposal approved by a funding source results in a **legal contract** between the investigator (and often the university) and the funder.
- Once approved, changes should occur only when arguments can be made for "absolute necessity or compelling desirability." (p. 4)
- **Neither the student nor the sponsoring faculty should be free to alter the fundamental terms by unilateral decision.**
- For theses/dissertations: substantial revisions should be made only with explicit consent of the full committee.

### 2. Regulations Governing Proposals

Multiple overlapping regulatory sources govern proposals; the writer must identify and conform to all of them:

| Regulatory Source | What It Controls | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Funding agency guidelines | Form, page limits, style, content sequence | Must be followed exactly; e.g., "not to exceed 25 typewritten pages" |
| University regulations | Thesis/dissertation format, report standards | Generally broad; e.g., "must conform to APA style" |
| Departmental policy | Format for final report, procedural norms | Informal but real; communicated through department culture |
| Dissertation/thesis committee | Writing style, working procedures, format preferences | Most idiosyncratic; individual committee member preferences can create conflicting demands |
| Publication-related regulations | Standards for the final report that the proposal must anticipate | If the plan requires a report meeting certain standards, the proposal must address them |

**Key rule:** Anticipate conflicting demands from different committee members and attempt their resolution before data collection begins. It is always wise to anticipate conflicting demands.

**Alternative dissertation formats:** Some universities now allow a research paper (or series of papers) with an expanded literature review and supporting appendix materials. This can save considerable time in converting the dissertation to publication. However, alternative formats do not alter the need for a complete, sound proposal.

**General format principle:** "No universally applicable and correct format exists for the research proposal." (p. 7) Flexibility — not rigidity — makes strong proposal documents. Format guidelines quickly become perceived as mandatory prescription, which produces awkward and illogical proposal documents. What is written must fit the real topic, not a preconceived format ideal.

### 3. General Considerations

The most common obstacles in proposal preparation are not subtle design or data management problems — they are:
- What is the proper question to ask?
- Where is the best place to look for the answer?
- What is the best way to standardize, quantify, and record observations?

**Writing standard:** Simplicity, clarity, and parsimony. Complicated matters are best communicated when they are objects of simple, well-edited prose.

**Drafting process — the proposal as evolving document:**
1. Prepare clean, updated copies of the evolving proposal and submit them to advisors or colleagues **in advance** of scheduled consultations.
2. Prepare an agenda of questions and problems to be discussed and submit them **in advance** of scheduled consultations.
3. Keep a carefully written and **dated record** of all discussions and decisions that occur with regard to each item on the consultation agenda.

**Proposal-to-report conversion:** When the task of preparing a proposal is well executed, the task of preparing the final report is more than half done. Under ideal conditions, minor changes (such as altering verb tense) will convert the proposal into the opening chapters of the thesis or dissertation, or into initial sections of a research report.

### 4. Specific Communication Tasks (Section-by-Section)

These are the tasks present in nearly all empirical research proposals. They are not necessarily labeled with these headings — they may appear as "Background," "Importance of the Study," "Review of Literature," "Methodology," "Definitions," or "Limitations," or merged into single sections. The order can vary. Grant proposals may demand additional tasks.

#### Task 1: Introducing the Study
- Use a **short, meticulously devised statement** that establishes the overall area of concern, arouses interest, and communicates essential orientation.
- The standard: a **"gentle introduction"** — avoids tedious length and the shock of technical detail or abstruse argument.
- A careful introduction is the precursor of three other tasks: statement of purpose, rationale, and background. It may be written as the first paragraph(s) of an opening section that includes all three.
- **Best technique:** Identify and define the **central construct(s)** involved. Constructs are abstract symbolizations of observable phenomena.
  - When the reader asks "What is this study about?" the answer is to present the key constructs and explain how they will be represented in the investigation.
  - Sketch the study in bold strokes without usurping the detailed sections that follow.
- **Note on relationships:** Relationships among constructs that will be of particular interest, or about which explicit hypotheses will be developed, should be briefly noted. Constructs the reader probably already knows may be omitted from the introduction.
- **Most common error:** Failure to get to the point — usually from engaging in grand generalizations.
  - Bad opening: "The child's capacity to maintain balance is a factor of fundamental importance in the design of elementary school curriculum." (A true statement but far from the heart of a study on muscle-strength-to-balance relationships.)
  - Better opening: "The task of maintaining static balance requires muscular action to hold the pelvis in a horizontal position. When muscle strength is inadequate to accomplish this, performance is impaired." (Gets directly to the construct-relationship under study.)
- Do not load the first paragraph with citations. "As a general rule, the first paragraph of the introduction should be free of citations. Documentation of important points can wait until a full discussion of the problem is launched." (p. 9)
- Technical language, quotations, and extensive references are intrusions on the preliminary statement.

#### Task 2: Stating the Purpose
- State an explicit **purpose** early in the proposal — often in the introductory paragraph(s).
- "Purpose" = a statement of **why** you want to do the study and **what** you intend to accomplish.
- Two broad categories:
  1. Purposes related to the desire to **improve** something (practical purposes)
  2. Purposes related to the desire to **understand** something (theoretical purposes)
  - In some cases, it is wise to be explicit about **personal** purposes as well (curiosity, social responsibility, career demands).
- A purpose statement is NOT an exhaustive survey of intentions, nor need it be written in formal research-question language (those are more specific).
- Make it early, forthright, simple, and brief. The purpose statement satisfies the reader's pressing questions: What is this about? Why is this study being proposed?
- **Succinct answers allow the reader to attend to subsequent exposition without waiting to discover the main objective.**

#### Task 3: Providing a Rationale
- After establishing topic and purpose, address: **"Why bother with that?"**
- Rationale involves both logical argument and documentation with factual evidence.
- The intention: persuade the reader that (a) the investigation (with its component questions/hypotheses) is worthy of attention, and (b) the problem has been correctly defined.
- **Diagramming technique:** Drawing a diagram of factors and relationships that support the problem formulation is often helpful.
  - Example (from text, Figure 1.1): Three convergent evidence boxes (cognitive function better at sea level than high altitude; older adults who are hypoxic exhibit cognitive dysfunction; patients with cerebrovascular disease perform better on cognitive tests under high oxygen) → intermediate node (oxygen is necessary for glucose metabolism: the brain's fuel) → final node (oxygen therapy for six months will increase cognitive function in older adults).
  - With constructs defined, the rationale is developed by documenting information within each box and explaining the consequences of a positive finding.
- A sound rationale: convinces the reader that you are raising the right question and that the answer is worth finding.
- **Scope:** Rationale at this stage should be limited to basic matters of defining what is to be studied and why it is worth doing. Detailed rationale for specific method choices can be deferred to the methodology section.

#### Task 4: Formulating Questions or Hypotheses
- All proposals must arrive at a **formal statement of questions or hypotheses**.
- Language must be **carefully constructed**, specifying each variable in explicit terms.
  - Poor: "Studying each day should result in improved learning."
  - Better: "Sixty minutes of studying each day will result in significantly increased scores on a standardized test of achievement." (p. 11)

**Research Questions:**
- Most appropriate when research is **exploratory**.
- Specificity of questions signals that the problem has been subject to thorough analysis.
- Questions should be directed toward outcomes foreshadowed by the literature or pilot work, not toward scanning for potentially interesting findings.
- Three tests of clarity and inclusiveness for quantitative studies:
  1. Is the question free of ambiguity?
  2. Is a relationship among variables expressed?
  3. Does the question imply an empirical test?
- The most common difficulty: reducing everything to a single, unambiguous question.
- Clarity hinges on adequate **specificity** and correct **degree of inclusiveness**: major elements must be identified in a way that permits no confusion with other elements, but the statement must maintain simplicity by including nothing beyond main variables and relationships.
- In qualitative research: questions are often tentative, contingent on the unfolding of the study, and may sound highly generalized (e.g., "What is going on in this urban school classroom?"). Nevertheless, their careful formulation is no less important — they give initial direction, bring theoretical constructs to the analysis, and reflect sophisticated thought in determining the focus of inquiry.
- Experienced qualitative researchers sometimes elect not to package their curiosity into explicit research questions. Graduate students, however, should in most cases formulate explicit questions — advisors are reassured when the proposal contains a careful accounting of what the data are expected to reveal.

**Research Hypotheses:**
- A hypothesis **indicates the question in testable form** and **predicts the nature of the answer**.
- A clear question is readily transformed into a hypothesis by casting it as a declarative statement that can be tested so as to show it to be either true or false.
- Hypotheses differ from questions in that they **exert a direct influence on each subsequent step** from design to preparation of the final report. By specifying a prediction about outcome, the hypothesis creates a bridge between theoretical considerations and the ensuing research process.
- Advantage over questions: permits more powerful and persuasive conclusions. A research question at study's end only permits "Here is how the world looked when I observed it." A confirmed hypothesis permits "Based on my particular explanation of how the world works, this is what I expected to observe — and that is exactly how it looked! Therefore my explanation must be given credibility." (p. 14)
- **Two forms:**
  - Null hypothesis: "There is no difference between..." (preferred when inferential statistics will be used to analyze data)
  - Directional hypothesis (research or directional): "When this, also that" (positive) or "When this, not that" (negative) — permits more persuasive logic and more statistical power
- **Rule of thumb for novice:** Use directional hypotheses when pilot data clearly indicate a direction, or when the theory is sufficiently robust to include persuasive evidence for directionality. Use null format when the investigation is a preliminary exploration with no well-established theory and no pilot data.
- **Formulation rules:**
  1. Evaluate by the same criteria as research questions: lack of ambiguity, expression of relationship, implication of appropriate test.
  2. The entire prediction must be dealt with in a single test. If one portion could be rejected without also rejecting the remainder, rewrite.
  3. "But" signals trouble — it often signals two hypotheses joined into one amorphous statement.
  4. Two discrete dependent variables foreshadow difficulty — the implied statistical model may not be able to accept/reject the hypothesis as a unit.
  5. **Several small, perfectly testable hypotheses are always preferable to one that is larger and amorphous.**
  6. Primary hypotheses should be stated first; secondary or confirmatory hypotheses may be separated to give prominence to main intent.
  7. Formulate hypotheses with an eye to the **qualitative characteristics of available measurement tools** — the correlation between scores from two tests cannot exceed the square root of the product for reliability in each test (illustrated: if self-esteem test reliability = .68 and reading test reliability = .76, a hypothesis of positive correlation greater than .80 is doomed to failure: √(.76 × .68) = .72).
- Note on significance testing debate: Current debate exists (Schmidt, 1996; Thompson, 1996, 1997) about whether statistical significance testing has technical limitations and whether other types of analyses provide greater benefits. Graduate students should discuss with advisors until a consensus emerges.

#### Task 5: Delimitations and Limitations
- **Delimitations:** Describe the populations to which generalizations may safely be made. Defined by the subject sample and the analysis employed. "Delimit" literally means to define the limits inherent in the use of a particular construct or population.
- **Limitations:** Refer to limiting conditions or restrictive weaknesses — e.g., when all factors cannot be controlled as part of the study design, or when the optimal number of observations cannot be made due to ethics or feasibility.
  - If the investigator has given careful thought to these problems and determined that the information to be gained from the compromised aspect is nevertheless valid and useful, the investigator proceeds but duly notes the limitation.
- All studies have inherent delimitations and limitations. Whether they appear as a separate section or simply as they arise is an individual decision.
- If they are few in number and perfectly obvious, the latter treatment (discussing them as they arise) is desirable.
- **The investigator's responsibility:** understand these constraints and assure the reader that they have been considered during formulation of the study.

#### Task 6: Providing Definitions
- All proposals use systematic language that may be specific to that field of research or to that proposal.
- Discussed in detail in the book's Chapter 6, "Clarity and Precision: Speaking in System Language."

#### Task 7: Discussing the Background of the Problem (Literature Review)
The author must answer three questions:
1. **What do we already know or do?** (One or two sentences; purpose is orientation. Major discussions of significance go under "rationale for the study.")
2. **How does this particular question relate to what we already know or do?** (Purpose: explain and support the exact form of questions or hypotheses that serve as the focus for the study.)
3. **Why select this particular method of investigation?** (Purpose: explain and support the selections made from among alternative methods of investigation.)

**Rules for the literature review:**
- Indicate the main directions taken by workers in the area and the main issues of methodology and interpretation that have arisen.
- Give critical analysis of previous methodology and exposition of the advantages and limitations inherent in various alternatives.
- Give close attention to conceptual and theoretical formulations that are explicit or implicit within the selected studies.
- Select only those studies that provide a foundation for the proposed investigation; discuss them in sufficient detail to make their relevance clear; note explicitly the ways in which they contribute to the proposed research; give some indication of how the proposal is designed to move beyond earlier work.
- **Resist the impulse to display the extent of personal labors in achieving what you know and the volume of interesting but presently irrelevant information accumulated in the process.**
- The rule in selecting studies: limit discussion to what is essential to the main topic.
- A complete list of all references used in developing the proposal (properly called a bibliography, as distinct from the list of references) may be placed in an appendix — providing a service to the interested reader and some psychological relief to the writer.

**Organizing conceptual frameworks:**
- Whenever possible, be conceptually or theoretically clear by creating organizing frameworks that encompass both reviewed studies and the proposed research.
- This is the most important single opportunity for the application of original thought in communication.
- A category system that allows division of diverse ideas or recondite events into easily perceived and remembered subsets is an organizational convenience and also identifies distinctive threads of thought.
- The task is to isolate the parallel ways by which researchers, working at different times and in varying degrees of intellectual isolation, have conceived of reality.
- The most elegant kind of research proposals use the review framework as a bridge connecting existing knowledge, a proposed theory, and the specific, theory-based hypotheses to be empirically tested.

#### Task 8: Explaining Procedures (Methodology)
The methodology section will be subject to the closest critical scrutiny. Must include:

**Required sub-elements:**
1. Identification and description of the target population and sampling methods to be used
2. Presentation of instruments and techniques for measurement
3. Presentation of a design for the collection of data
4. Presentation of procedures for collecting and recording data
5. Explanation of data analysis procedures to be used
6. Development of plans for contingencies such as subject mortality

**Presentation standard:** The proposal must provide a **step-by-step set of instructions** for conducting the investigation. The discussion of method must include sources of data, collection of data, and analysis of data. In addition, the discussion must show that the specific techniques selected will not fall short of the claims established in previous sections.

**Diagrams:** Strongly advocated by the authors for methodology sections. Diagrams can replace pages of explanatory text while providing greater clarity:
- Figure 1.2 (Method Flow Chart): shows a counterbalanced treatment design with branches for interview and film-viewing groups — described as needing "no more than a brief paragraph of accompanying text to provide a clear account of the procedure."
- Figure 1.3 (Sample With Multiple Subgroups): shows a subject population divided into 16 subgroups using 4 variables (gender, role, subject matter, affective state) with immediate transparent clarity.
- Figure 1.4 (Statistical Model): ovals represent clusters of variables; boxes show the variables in each cluster; arrows represent interrelationships. "Imagine how many words it would take to describe these relationships!"

**Rule:** The methodology section must be freely adapted to the purpose of the study. Whatever the format, the proposal must provide a step-by-step set of instructions.

#### Task 9: Providing Supplementary Material (Appendices)
For clarity and economical presentation, many items may be placed in appendices keyed to appropriate references in the main text. Items appropriate for appendices:

1. Specifications for equipment
2. Instructions to subjects
3. Letters and other relevant documents
4. Subject consent forms
5. Raw data or tabular material from pilot studies
6. Tabular materials from related research
7. Copies of paper and pencil instruments
8. Questions for structured interviews
9. Credentials of experts, judges, or other special personnel to be employed in the study
10. Diagrammatic models of the research design
11. Diagrammatic models of the statistical analysis
12. Schematics for constructed equipment
13. Chapter outline for the final report
14. Proposed time schedule for executing the study
15. Supplementary bibliographies

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode:** Proposal-writing agent, diagnostic and generative modes.

**Concrete moves the agent should make:**

1. **Structural scaffold check:** When given a draft proposal or asked to write one, map the draft against the 9 specific tasks. Identify which tasks are missing, thin, or misplaced. Flag: Is the purpose statement early and brief? Is the rationale diagrammable? Are hypotheses unitary and testable?

2. **Introduction audit:** Check first paragraph for (a) presence of a clear central construct, (b) absence of citations, (c) absence of grand generalizations, (d) getting to the point about what the study is actually about.

3. **Hypothesis quality check:** Apply the three tests (free of ambiguity, expresses a relationship, implies an empirical test). Flag any hypothesis with "but" as likely compound. Check whether the measurement tools' reliability ceilings make the predicted effect magnitude achievable.

4. **Literature review framing:** Ask: Does the review have an organizing conceptual framework? Does it answer all three background questions (what we know, how this relates to what we know, why this method)? Flag any tendency to display volume of reading rather than relevance to the proposed research.

5. **Methodology completeness:** Verify all 6 required sub-elements are addressed. Recommend diagrams where prose is growing long — especially for sampling designs, counterbalanced designs, and statistical models.

6. **Regulations check:** When Bilal is writing for a specific funder or committee, check whether all known guidelines have been addressed: page limits, style requirements, section ordering requirements.

7. **Contract frame:** Remind Bilal that once the committee approves the proposal, the approved document becomes the contract. The proposal should be written to be defensible as a plan, not merely as a draft.

8. **Replication standard:** As a quality heuristic, ask: could another investigator pick up this proposal and replicate the study with results not substantially different from what Bilal would obtain? If not, the methodology section needs more detail.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Bilal sits at the intersection of three proposal-writing contexts: (1) dissertation proposal for his PhD committee at Iowa State / Coordinated Systems Lab; (2) NSF/AFRL/DoD grant proposals for SiS funding; (3) potentially SBIR/STTR proposals as CTO.

**How this chapter maps onto Bilal's specific situation:**

- **The triple-function frame is directly applicable.** The CTPC framework paper is simultaneously a communication document (to reviewers, program managers, and committee members), a research plan (specifying what will be observed and how), and a contract (committing SiS to specific deliverables and Bilal to a dissertation structure). Writing each section with all three functions in mind — not just "what sounds good" — changes how specific the methodology must be.

- **The replication standard is the right test for SiS proposals.** A CTPC methodology section should be detailed enough that another orbital mechanics + ML team could reproduce the dissipation-constrained corrector architecture, the RTN/RSW frame decomposition, the SGP4 predictor interface, and the Cholesky parameterization for the covariance block — and get results not substantially different from Bilal's. This is a high bar that will force productive specificity.

- **The hypothesis formulation rules directly govern SiS research questions.** "The CTPC corrector, constrained by Ḣ ≤ 0 as a hard architectural prior, will produce more realistic covariance propagation than a soft-penalty PINN baseline" is a directional hypothesis with a clear relationship, an implied empirical test, and a single testable prediction. The book's criteria flag whether such a hypothesis is testable with the measurement tools available (conjunction probability metrics, covariance realism scores).

- **Rationale diagramming is exactly right for SDA proposals.** The "diagram of logic" technique (Figure 1.1) maps onto the kind of argument Bilal needs to make: [orbital debris population growing] + [SGP4 covariance errors documented] + [physics-as-architecture ML proven in adjacent domains] → [SGP4 error is systematic and learnable with hard dissipation constraint] → [CTPC with hard Ḣ ≤ 0 will reduce conjunction probability estimation error]. Drawing this diagram before writing the rationale section will organize the argument and reveal missing evidence nodes.

- **Organizing frameworks for the lit review:** The most important opportunity for original thought. Bilal's organizing framework — Physics-as-Architecture vs. soft-penalty approaches — is exactly the kind of distinctive conceptual schema the book recommends. It should be made explicit as the organizing principle of the background section, not left implicit.

- **The proposal-as-contract implication:** For DoD/AFRL proposals, the approved SOW is a legal contract. Bilal should write the technical approach section with the awareness that vague claims will later be held against him, while overly specific claims may constrain the research. The right level is: specific enough to be replicated, flexible enough to accommodate contingencies documented in the methodology.

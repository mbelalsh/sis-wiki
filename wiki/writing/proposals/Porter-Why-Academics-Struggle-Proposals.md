# Why Academics Have a Hard Time Writing Good Grant Proposals (Porter)

## One-Line Intuition

Academic writing explains ideas to expert insiders who already care; grant writing sells a future project to skeptical generalists who control money and owe you nothing.

---

## Why This Matters

Robert Porter (Program Development Manager, Virginia Tech Research Division) wrote this paper for research administrators — the people whose job is to coach faculty who have strong publication records but consistently lose at grant competitions. The paper won Best Paper of the Year at the 2006 SRAI Annual Meeting and was published in *The Journal of Research Administration*, Vol. XXXVIII, No. 2, 2007.

The central claim: **the writing style that earns prestige in scholarly journals actively hurts proposals.** The habits drilled into academics over a decade of PhD training — dispassionate tone, past-oriented framing, theme-centered structure, insider vocabulary, solo drafting, tolerance for length — are precisely the wrong habits for competitive grant writing. Understanding why each habit fails, and what replaces it, is the complete practical content of the paper.

For Bilal, this is doubly relevant: he comes from an ML/SDA paper-writing background, writes for expert technical audiences, and now needs to write NSF/DARPA/AFRL proposals. Every contrast Porter draws maps directly onto the transition from "IEEE/NeurIPS paper" to "DARPA BAA response."

## Core Content

### The Smell Test: How Winning Proposals Look Different Immediately (p. 37–38)

Porter opens with a simple heuristic: pick up any winning grant proposal and compare its first page to a typical journal article. The visual differences are diagnostic:

- **Sentences are shorter**, with key phrases **underlined or bolded** to make them stand out
- **Lists are printed bullet style** rather than embedded in prose
- **Graphs, tables, and drawings abound** — visual density is a feature, not a distraction
- The writing is **energetic, direct, and concise**
- The subject matter is **easy to understand** — fewer highly technical terms
- Reading a winning proposal feels like each page teaches you something new and exciting

The *feel* of a winning proposal is the feel of a good *Scientific American* article: authoritative but accessible, specific but not dense.

Porter's key lesson (p. 38): "Success in grant writing is a matter of style and format as much as content." A well-written proposal will not win money for a weak idea — but many good ideas fail funding because the proposal is poorly written.

The killer reviewer comment that frames the whole paper: *"Reads like a journal article."* (p. 38)

### Table 1: The Master Contrast Table (p. 38)

Porter provides an explicit eight-row contrast table. Every row is a mindset shift academics must make. This is the paper's core diagnostic tool.

| Dimension | Academic Writing | Grant Writing |
|---|---|---|
| **Motivation** | Scholarly pursuit: individual passion | Sponsor goals: service attitude |
| **Time orientation** | Past oriented: work that has been done | Future oriented: work that should be done |
| **Structural focus** | Theme-centered: theory and thesis | Project-centered: objectives and activities |
| **Rhetorical mode** | Expository rhetoric: explaining to reader | Persuasive rhetoric: "selling" the reader |
| **Tone** | Impersonal tone: objective, dispassionate | Personal tone: conveys excitement |
| **Social mode** | Individualistic: primarily a solo activity | Team-focused: feedback needed |
| **Length norms** | Few length constraints: verbosity rewarded | Strict length constraints: brevity rewarded |
| **Vocabulary** | Specialized terminology: "insider jargon" | Accessible language: easily understood |

Each row is developed in a dedicated subsection. Understanding *why* each academic habit persists — not just that it's wrong — is what makes Porter's framework actionable.

---

### Contrast 1: Scholarly Pursuit vs. Sponsor Goals (p. 39)

**The academic habit:** Scholars pursue their own intellectual interests and seek funding to pursue those interests further. They are passionate about their area, expect funders to share that passion, and write about their work as personal, self-motivated scholarship.

**Why it fails:** Funding agencies have little interest in advancing the careers of ambitious academics. They fund projects with a good chance of achieving *their* goals. Denial letters routinely read: "A sound concept, but it does not fit our current funding priorities."

**The grant-writing shift:** Adopt a **service attitude**. Seasoned grant writers spend significant time parsing grant announcements, highlighting passages about what sponsors want to accomplish, and then finding ways to adapt their expertise to match those objectives. They check with program officers before writing to test whether the idea is a good fit.

**Porter's key quote (p. 39):** "My epiphany came when I realized that grant programs do not exist to make me successful, but rather my job is to make those programs successful."

**Prescription:** Before writing a single sentence, answer: What does this sponsor want to accomplish? How does my project serve that goal? Frame the entire proposal as a response to their mission, not a pitch for your interests.

---

### Contrast 2: Past Orientation vs. Future Orientation (p. 39)

**The academic habit:** Academic writing describes work already done — literature reviewed, hypothesis formulated, experiments run, conclusions drawn. The mode is retrospective: here is what we found.

**Why it fails:** A proposal describes work that *has not yet happened*. It is, in Porter's memorable phrase, **"science fiction"** — it must be grounded in solid science, but the research design itself is a set of logical yet *imagined* activities that have yet to take place.

**The grant-writing shift:** Flip the temporal frame. The proposal is not a report on past accomplishments (though those may be cited for credibility); it is a detailed, convincing description of *what will be done* and *what outcomes will result*. Grant proposals end with their projects' expected outcomes; academic essays end with their authors' final conclusions.

**Key implication:** Past achievements appear only as evidence of capability, not as the substance of the pitch. The substance is the future work.

---

### Contrast 3: Theme-Centered vs. Project-Centered (p. 39)

**The academic habit:** Scholarly writers dwell on theme, thesis, and theory. Essays and books can be devoted to original thinking, contributions of past and present scholars, or the evolution of entire schools of thought. The goal is to draw readers into the realm of ideas.

**Why it fails:** Sponsors do not fund intellectual exploration. Academic writers often seek funding to "study," "examine," or "explore" some theme or issue — language that signals the work has no defined deliverables. Sponsors rarely pay for open-ended inquiry.

**The grant-writing shift:** Structure the proposal around a **specific problem → creative approach → objectives and activities → expected outcomes** arc. Grant writers sketch an important problem, then move quickly to describing a creative approach to addressing it with a set of activities that will accomplish specific goals and objectives. "The overall project is designed to make a significant contribution to a discipline or to a society as a whole." (p. 39)

**Prescription:**
1. Open with the problem (not the background theory)
2. State the approach immediately
3. List specific objectives with measurable outcomes
4. End with expected impact, not open conclusions

---

### Contrast 4: Expository vs. Persuasive Rhetoric (p. 39–40)

**The academic habit:** Academic writing is expository — it aims to build a logical progression of thought, helping readers share the writer's intellectual journey and agree with core themes. The goal is understanding, not action.

**Why it fails:** A grant proposal must do something stronger: convince a skeptical reviewer that scarce dollars should be spent on *this* project rather than competing ones. "Grant reviewers are a notoriously skeptical lot who reject a majority of proposals." (p. 40)

**The grant-writing shift:** Shift from explaining to **selling**. The whole effort must be geared toward building a winning argument, a compelling case that the proposed work is uniquely deserving. "In effect, a good proposal is an elegant sales pitch." (p. 40)

**What this means in practice:**
- Every section must answer the implicit reviewer question: "Why fund this instead of something else?"
- Claims must be backed by evidence strong enough to overcome skepticism
- The proposal must "win their reluctant support" — the reviewer's default is rejection

---

### Contrast 5: Impersonal vs. Personal Tone (p. 40)

**The academic habit:** From undergraduate papers through doctoral dissertations, scholars are trained to write in proper academic style — cautious, objective, dispassionate, with all evidence of the writer's persona hidden from view. First person is avoided; hedging is virtuous ("results suggest that...").

**Why it fails:** Grant writers need reviewers' enthusiastic endorsement. Reviewers must be excited about the project. Dispassionate, hedged prose fails to generate excitement.

**The grant-writing shift:** Write in **active voice**, with **strong, energetic phrasing**, and make **direct references to the project team in first person**. Convey genuine enthusiasm. Porter provides three example sentences that violate journal editorial rules but are effective grant writing (p. 40):

> "Our aim with this innovative curriculum is to improve the supply of exceptionally skilled paramedics with National Registry certification."

> "This project will provide your grant program with a powerful combination of cutting edge nanoscale science and frontier research in applied geochemistry."

> "Though we launched this large and ambitious program just two years ago, we are gratified by the regional and national awards it has garnered."

Note: "innovative," "powerful," "ambitious," "gratified" — emotional and evaluative words that journals would reject but that effective proposals require.

**Prescription:** Remove hedges. Replace "results suggest" with "this project will." Replace passive constructions with active ones. Use "we" and "our." Tell reviewers why to be excited, in language that itself conveys excitement.

---

### Contrast 6: Solo Scholarship vs. Teamwork (p. 40–41)

**The academic habit:** Academic writing is mostly a solo activity. A paper or book chapter may be passed to one or two readers for final proofing, but the overall endeavor is highly individualistic. The scholar fills pages with prose and considers the work done when it is complete.

**Why it fails:** Grant success depends on nearly unanimous approval from a sizeable group of reviewers. A proposal that has not been tested on multiple readers before submission is flying blind.

**The grant-writing shift:** Require **teamwork from the outset**. Before the first draft, the basic concept is sounded out with colleagues. It is then sent to a grant program officer to test fit. For large multi-investigator proposals, sections are written and rewritten by several researchers, compiled and edited by the lead writer. Many large proposals go through **red team** internal review before submission.

**Process prescribed:**
1. Sound out concept with colleagues before writing
2. Check with program officer — is this a good fit?
3. Circulate drafts early for substantive feedback
4. For major grants: form a red team of senior colleagues for internal review
5. Leave sufficient lead time before the sponsor deadline

"Documents that bypass this essential process have a much greater chance of failure." (p. 41)

---

### Contrast 7: Verbosity vs. Brevity (p. 40–41)

**The academic habit:** In academia, verbosity signals thoroughness. Extended lectures, journals without page limits, books that expand simple ideas to multiple chapters — the culture rewards length. A common technique is stretching sentences and paragraphs to extreme lengths.

Porter illustrates with the famous **Bad Writing Contest winner** (Butler, 1997, cited p. 40–41): a sentence about hegemony, structuralism, and temporality that runs to over 100 words with zero information.

**Why it fails:** Grant reviewers are impatient readers with limited time. They look for any excuse to stop reading. "If the proposal does not intrigue them by the very first page, they will not read any further." (p. 41) When they are annoyed, they start looking for reasons to justify why the proposal should not be funded.

**Porter citing Porter (p. 41):** "When asked to describe the characteristics of good grant writing, senior reviewers put qualities such as 'clear' and 'concise' at the top of the list." Brevity is not only the soul of wit; it is the essence of grantsmanship. "Less is more." (Mies van der Rohe, cited p. 41)

**Prescription:**
- Strict page limits are a feature, not a constraint to fight
- Every sentence must earn its place
- Cut sentences that decorate rather than inform
- Front-load: the most important material appears on page 1, not buried in section 3

---

### Contrast 8: Specialized Terminology vs. Accessible Language (p. 41–42)

**The academic habit:** Every discipline uses specialized terminology. Academics are trained to use it precisely. There is also a psychological dynamic: writers inflate prose with large words and complicated sentences to appear serious and scholarly. "A spell comes over us when we know our writing will be evaluated, either by editors or by grant reviewers: We want our work to appear scholarly, so we habitually inflate our prose with large words and complicated sentences to achieve the effect of serious thinking." (p. 41)

**Why it fails:** Unfortunately, complex language has the opposite effect on readers. Alley (1996) shows too many big words and convoluted expressions result in muddled jargon. Porter's example of a bad grant sentence (p. 41):

> "The objective of this study is to develop an effective commercialization strategy for solar energy systems by analyzing the factors that are impeding commercial projects and by prioritizing the potential government and industry actions that can facilitate the viability of the projects."

Porter's rewrite (Alley's simpler version, p. 42):

> "This study will consider why current solar energy systems have not yet reached the commercial stage and will evaluate the steps that industry and government can take to make these systems commercial."

The rewrite is shorter, clearer, and more compelling. "Fewer words with greater clarity — a tradeoff that will improve the score of any grant proposal." (p. 42)

**The standard to aim for:** NIH advises grant writers to study articles in *Scientific American* — world-class scientists writing about cutting-edge work for a general readership. "Good proposals do the same." (p. 42) Porter's *Scientific American* stem cells example demonstrates the target register: clinically accurate, easily understandable, suitable as a grant introduction.

**Prescription for mixed panels:** Grant review panels often include specialists and generalists. Write for the generalist. The specialist will understand; the generalist will not, and it is the generalist's confusion that kills proposals.

### Five Remedial Strategies (pp. 42–43)

Porter provides five institutional strategies for research administrators to help academics adopt grant-writing habits. These double as self-prescriptions for the individual writer.

#### Strategy 1: Home-Grown Workshops (p. 42)

Grant writing workshops teach good techniques at low cost. Beginning workshops on basic grant writing skills should be offered regularly, supplemented by workshops focused on specific funding agencies. "Especially popular are presentations by successful grant writers and copies of winning proposals." (p. 42)

#### Strategy 2: Reading Successful Proposals (p. 42)

Winning grants teach by example. Perusing several, a new grant writer will note common differences from accepted academic style and can be encouraged to mimic them. Sources:
- Institution's own successful proposals (online, access-limited)
- The Grant Center (www.tgcigrantproposals.com — reasonable rates)
- Federal agencies under Freedom of Information Act (wait several months; sensitive information deleted)

#### Strategy 3: Editing by a Grants Specialist (p. 42–43)

"While no amount of editorial polishing can save a weak idea, a seasoned grant writer can add value to a sound concept by judicious editing." Labor-intensive at first, but once the writer catches on to the simpler, livelier style of grant writing, the need for personal attention drops off rapidly.

#### Strategy 4: Red Team Reviews (p. 43)

For major multidisciplinary grants, form an internal review team of experienced senior colleagues. If carefully selected for expertise and reputations, their written comments can have great impact. "Be warned, however: A considerable degree of gentle but persistent nagging is required to get the writers to have the document ready for internal review with sufficient lead time before the sponsor's deadline." (p. 43)

#### Strategy 5: Writing Tips Posted by Research Office (p. 43)

Post simple writing tips on the research office website with side-by-side examples: bad writing contrasted with effective revisions. "Seeing them side by side, readers will quickly spot which bad characteristics are their own, and will note how they can craft better versions." (p. 43)

Porter endorses Alley (1996) and Strunk & White (2000) as foundational craft references — "its instructions for crisp and vigorous writing will give heart to academics who are trying to break old habits." (p. 43)

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode:** Proposal-writing agent. This document is Tier 1 retrieval — a high-priority diagnostic framework that should be loaded whenever the agent is helping Bilal draft or revise any grant proposal section.

### Concrete moves the agent should make:

1. **Diagnosis before drafting.** Before writing any section, check each of Porter's eight dimensions against the planned content. Is the framing past or future? Is the tone impersonal or personal? Is the structure theme-centered or project-centered?

2. **Translate the Table 1 columns into section-level prompts:**
   - "What does the sponsor want to accomplish, and how does this project serve that goal?" (sponsor goals)
   - "What will happen, and what will be the measurable outcomes?" (future orientation)
   - "What is the specific problem, approach, objectives, and activities?" (project-centered)
   - "Why should a skeptical reviewer fund this over competing proposals?" (persuasive rhetoric)
   - "Where can 'we' and active voice replace passive and hedged constructions?" (personal tone)
   - "Has this draft been read by non-experts? Would a generalist understand?" (accessible language)

3. **Red-flag phrases.** Flag any instance of: "we will study/examine/explore" (replace with a specific deliverable); "results suggest" or "it appears that" (replace with a direct claim); passive constructions with no agent; sentences over 40 words; first appearances of unexplained acronyms.

4. **Brevity pass.** After a draft is complete, run a cut pass: every sentence that does not directly advance the reviewer's understanding of problem, approach, or expected outcome is a candidate for deletion.

5. **Tone injection.** After cutting, scan for sentences that can be made more energetic: replace nominalization with verbs, replace weak verbs (is, has, provides) with strong ones (delivers, produces, enables), inject specific claims about impact.

6. **Audience test.** For every paragraph of technical content, ask: "Would a smart program officer who is not a specialist in this subfield understand this?" If not, simplify before adding back precision where essential.

7. **Service attitude audit.** Check that the proposal's opening paragraph answers the reviewer's question: "Why does this sponsor care?" If it opens with the PI's background or the state of the literature, reframe to open with the sponsor's problem.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Bilal's primary writing mode is ML/SDA paper writing: explaining novel technical contributions to expert audiences who read every word. His academic conditioning is deep. The SiS grant-writing context (NSF CAREER, DARPA BAA responses, AFRL STTR/SBIR) requires every habit to flip:

| Bilal's current default | Grant-writing target |
|---|---|
| Explain CTPC architecture in detail | Lead with the SDA problem and the gap; architecture is the approach, not the theme |
| Past-tense framing ("we developed...") | Future-tense framing ("this project will deliver...") |
| Rely on technical precision to signal quality | Use accessible language targeted at the program officer generalist |
| Solo draft, share only near-final version | Sound out with colleagues and program officer before writing |
| NeurIPS-style concise contribution statement | DARPA-style "what will be different in SDA after this project" impact statement |
| Passive hedging ("results suggest that...") | Active claims ("this architecture eliminates the need for...") |
| Length as thoroughness signal | Every page must fight for its place; reviewers stop at page 1 if not compelled |

**The SiS mission statement is already written in grant style** (sponsor goals, future orientation, societal impact). The challenge is keeping that frame *throughout* a multi-section proposal, especially in technical sections where the academic reflex is to drop into expository mode. Porter's eight dimensions serve as a checklist to apply at the end of every proposal writing session.

**Specific CTPC application:** When writing the significance/impact section of any SDA-related proposal, the opening must not explain what CTPC is — it must explain what problem in Space Domain Awareness goes unsolved without CTPC, and why that matters to the sponsor's mission. The technical description follows only after the reviewer already cares.

## Open Questions

1. **How does Porter's framework interact with DARPA BAA structure?** DARPA uses a specific section order (technical approach, team, management plan, cost) that may constrain how much freedom the writer has to reorder for rhetorical effect. Where does Porter's advice apply unconditionally vs. where must it adapt?

2. **Service attitude vs. intellectual integrity.** Porter says adopt the sponsor's goals as your own. How far does this go? When Bilal's research direction and a sponsor's stated priorities only partially overlap, what is the honest framing that avoids misrepresentation while still applying the service attitude?

3. **Brevity in technical sections.** Porter's brevity prescription applies most clearly to narrative sections. In the technical approach section of an SBIR/STTR, extreme compression can leave reviewers unable to evaluate feasibility. What is the right calibration for technical depth vs. brevity in SDA-adjacent proposals?

4. **Academic writing as ethos signal.** Some NSF review panels (especially for CAREER awards) value scholarly depth and the investigator's demonstrated expertise. Is Porter's prescription universally applicable, or does the academic style retain value in certain agency/panel contexts?

5. **Red team logistics at a startup.** The red team model assumes a university research office infrastructure. For SiS as a startup, what is the equivalent? SBIR reviewers? Advisory board members? Program officer pre-submission conversations?

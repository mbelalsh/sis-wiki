# CMU Proposals — Purpose, Structure, and Strategy

## One-Line Intuition
A proposal is a persuasion document: prove there is an urgent problem or gap, show your work fills it, and give the reader a clear reason to act (fund, approve, advise).

## Why This Matters
Most researchers write technically well but fail to frame their work as a proposal — they explain what they did instead of persuading a reader why it matters and why it is feasible now. This handout names the rhetorical purpose explicitly and gives a modular structure that applies across every proposal type Bilal will encounter (NSF, AFOSR, SBIR, dissertation committee, fellowship panels).

## Core Content

### Purpose
A proposal must do three things simultaneously:
1. **Identify an urgent problem or gap** in knowledge or practice.
2. **Claim that your work provides a solution** (or meaningfully closes the gap).
3. **Issue a call to action** — request funding, approval, resources, or advising.

The handout cites Novelty Moves explicitly as the recommended tool for establishing the gap. BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) is cited as the tool for making the main idea stand out.

### Audience
The audience varies by type:

| Proposal type | Likely audience |
|---|---|
| Dissertation | Advisor, committee |
| Fellowship | Foundation, panel |
| Research / Grant | Instructor, corporation, foundation |
| Business | Corporation, business, newspaper |

**What the audience is evaluating:**
- Feasibility — can this actually be done?
- Gap-filling — does it advance current scholarship?
- Value to the field or organization
- For dissertation proposals: compatibility between the student's work and the advisor's expertise
- For grant/business proposals: alignment with the funder's mission and financial benefit to them

**Key principle:** Tailor the proposal to align with the audience's goals and priorities. The proposal is not about you — it is about what your work does for them.

---

### Questions to Ask Yourself While Writing

1. What am I pursuing with this proposal?
   - a. What is my core research question or proposed solution?
   - b. What is the motivation or problem behind my proposal?
2. Who is my audience?
   - a. What is their goal or need?
   - b. Is my work a good match with that goal?

---

### Quality Research Questions
The research question is the "highlight of the proposal" — it summarizes the main motivation. A quality research question must be:

1. **Clear and explicit** — no ambiguity about what is being asked
2. **Relevant** — relevant to current scholarship in the field AND to the writer's own background/interests
3. **Realistic** — able to be researched; has a clear, bounded scope

The handout emphasizes that the question must be well-developed because the entire actionable plan centers on answering it. Showing you have a clear understanding of the topic area and an actionable forward plan is mandatory.

### Structure of a Proposal

The handout states explicitly: not all proposals have the same structure. Research the organization and purpose to structure effectively. The canonical components are:

#### Introduction
- Cover page, project summary or abstract, or table of contents (as applicable)
- State the purpose of the proposal
- Provide a brief overview of the project
- Introduce the research question
- Justify the need for the project

#### Background and Statement of Need
- Detailed description of the need for the project and/or the gap it fills
- Present your perspective on the current status quo
- Explain how your proposal addresses the status quo
- For dissertation proposals specifically: this section takes the form of a **literature review** demonstrating preliminary research

#### Research Question and Plan
Covers: Project Description, Methodology, Expected Outcomes, Schedule

- Express the feasibility of the project
- Detail the outcome you plan to achieve
- For dissertations: include the **theoretical framework** and the **methodology** for studying the topic
- Describe the **contribution to the field**
- May include a **schedule** or general project outline
- Audience must leave with: (a) a clear understanding of project scope and (b) confidence that you have a realistic completion plan

#### Resources Required
- Include only if there are costs associated with the project
- Not necessarily applicable to dissertations

#### Conclusion
- Return the reader to the main purpose of the project
- Leave them with a sense of the project's potential

#### References
- Academic proposals must include proper citations

### Other Tips (verbatim from handout)
- Use **Novelty Moves** to describe the gap in research/problem you're identifying and solving.
- The tone should be **professional and persuasive**, tailored to the type of work.
- Use the **BLUF strategy** to give readers your Bottom Line Up Front so your main idea stands out.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode:** Proposal-Writing-Agent — planning and drafting phase.

**Concrete moves:**

1. **Opening audit:** Before drafting any section, run the two self-questions (What am I pursuing? Who is my audience?). For SiS proposals, the answer to Q2a is typically: the funder wants dual-use technology that advances national space security and is commercially scalable. Map SiS capabilities to that goal explicitly.

2. **Research question test:** Apply the three-criterion checklist (clear, relevant, realistic) to every proposed research question before drafting the Background section. If a question fails "realistic," narrow scope or add a feasibility qualifier.

3. **Section scaffolding:** Use the six-section structure (Introduction → Background/Need → Question+Plan → Resources → Conclusion → References) as the default scaffold for any proposal where the funder has not imposed their own template.

4. **NSF compliance checklist:** When writing an NSF proposal, use the 12-section table above as a compliance checklist. Confirm each section is present before submission.

5. **Tone and strategy layer:** After drafting, apply BLUF (main claim in first sentence of each section), Novelty Moves (four-move gap establishment), and professional-persuasive register — not academic hedging, not marketing hyperbole.

6. **Audience alignment:** For each funder (NSF, AFOSR, DARPA, SBIR), explicitly state the funder's mission, then write one sentence per section explaining how that section demonstrates alignment with that mission.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

SiS will submit proposals to NSF (SBIR Phase I/II), AFOSR (basic research), DARPA (advanced tech), and DoD/Space Force (SDA transition programs). This handout maps directly to each:

- **NSF SBIR:** The 12-section NSF structure is mandatory. The "Statement of Need" section must establish that current SDA tools (SGP4 + TLE pipeline) are inadequate for high-accuracy conjunction analysis — this is the gap that CTPC fills. The "Research Question" must be explicit: can a physics-as-architecture neural corrector achieve calibrated uncertainty propagation for conjunction probability at operationally relevant timescales?
- **AFOSR / AFRL proposals:** Feasibility (the Plan section) is paramount — reviewers are program managers who must defend funding decisions to their chains. The CTPC roadmap (SGP4 baseline → residual ML corrector → uncertainty-aware CDM) provides exactly the milestone-based schedule the plan section requires.
- **SBIR commercialization:** The "Resources Required" section must connect to the commercial pathway; for SiS this means satellite operator subscription model and Space-Track data pipeline costs.
- **Audience alignment principle:** Each funder has a distinct mission. NSF wants scientific merit and broader impact. AFOSR wants basic science with clear defense relevance. DARPA wants high-risk, high-payoff breakthroughs. SBIR wants commercial viability. The same CTPC content must be reframed for each audience — this handout's audience analysis framework (What is their goal? Is my work a match?) is the reframing protocol.

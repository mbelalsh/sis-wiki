# "On the Art of Writing Proposals" — Przeworski & Salomon (SSRC)

## One-Line Intuition

A proposal is not a research plan for yourself — it is a persuasive document written for an overworked, multidisciplinary committee that is scanning for three answers: what will we learn, why does it matter, and how will we know it is true.

---

## Why This Matters

Adam Przeworski (University of Chicago, Political Science) and Frank Salomon (University of Wisconsin, Anthropology) wrote this essay for the Social Science Research Council (SSRC) to demystify the unspoken customs, norms, and needs that govern fellowship and grant competitions. First published 1988, revised 1995.

Their central insight: writing for committee competition is an art *distinct* from doing research. A committee reviewing 100+ proposals on voluntarily-donated time cannot mine your text for hidden answers. The proposal that wins is the one that makes its merit unmistakable — not necessarily the best project, but the best-communicated project among projects that all clear the bar of conceptual innovation, methodological rigor, and substantive richness.

This makes the essay unusually actionable: it is written from inside the selection process, naming exactly what reviewers look for, why they look for it, and how proposals fail.

## Core Content

### 1. The Rhetorical Situation

The committee context drives every structural and stylistic choice:

- **Audience is multidisciplinary.** Reviewers are scholars from *other* fields. Jargon that signals sophistication within your discipline reads as obscurantism to an outsider. The reviewer accepted the appointment for the intellectual pleasure of touring many disciplines' frontiers — do not cheat her of that by trafficking in parochial idiosyncrasy.
- **Audience is time-constrained.** A committee member reading a tall stack of proposals on voluntarily-donated time cannot comb for buried answers. Front-load everything.
- **Audience is judging equity across unlike circumstances.** Committees must compare proposals from applicants at resource-rich and resource-poor institutions, from established and emerging research traditions. Clarity and completeness compensate for institutional disadvantage.
- **Three kinds of merit are universally valued:** conceptual innovation, methodological rigor, and rich substantive content. A proposal must convincingly display all three.
- **The unspoken problem:** committees must choose among proposals that all possess those three virtues. The winning proposal gets its merits across *more forcefully* by addressing unspoken needs and norms as well as the overt rules.

### 2. The Three Questions Every Proposal Must Answer

Every reviewer scans for clear answers to exactly these three questions. If the answers are not on the first page, the proposal is at risk.

1. **What are we going to learn as the result of the proposed project that we do not know now?**
2. **Why is it worth knowing?**
3. **How will we know that the conclusions are valid?**

These are not rhetorical — they are the literal decision heuristic of the committee. A proposal that answers all three crisply in its opening paragraph has already done most of the work.

### 3. Capture the Reviewer's Attention (Opening Strategy)

- The opening paragraph, or the first page at most, is the moment to grab the reviewer's attention. Use it. **This is the moment to overstate, rather than understate, your point or question.** You can add conditions and caveats later.
- **Two effective opening moves:**
  1. **Clearly posed, non-rhetorical question** — one whose answer is far from obvious. Not "Is democracy good?" but "Are strong party systems conducive to democratic stability?" The question has effect precisely because the answer is not predetermined.
  2. **A stated central claim, hypothesis, or interpretation** — a pointed assertion that will remain in the reviewer's mind: "Workers do not organize unions; unions organize workers."
- After reading many proposals and discussing them for hours, reviewers identify proposals by their central claim, not by the applicant's institution. You want to be referred to as *the one who argues that Argentina never had a liberal democratic tradition*, not as *oh yes, the one from Chicago*.
- If your project is too complex for a telegraphic opening, step-by-step argumentation is acceptable — but you must still leave the reviewer with something to remember. A memorable, sticky formulation of the central claim is mandatory.

### 4. Aim for Clarity

- Most proposals are reviewed by **multidisciplinary committees**. The reviewer from another field expects the proposer to meet her halfway.
- **Avoid jargon** as much as possible. Restrict technical language to terms that truly lack equivalents in common language.
- **Keep the spotlight on ideas**, not disciplinary apparatus. An archeologist should argue the concepts latent in the ceramic typology more than the typology itself. A historian should argue the tendency latent in the mass of events.
- When additional technical material is needed, put it in **appendices** — this decongests the main text without losing rigor.
- Technical terms that are genuinely necessary should be introduced and explained; do not assume the reader knows them.

### 5. Establish the Context (Literature Review)

Your proposal must tell the committee not just what will be learned, but **what will be learned that somebody else does not already know**. This requires:

- **A sharply focused summary of the current state of knowledge** — not a comprehensive review, but a precise view of the specific body or bodies of knowledge to which you will add.
- **An up-to-date, comprehensive bibliography.** Committees treat bibliographies as a sign of seriousness. Some committee members devote considerable effort to evaluating them. A good bibliography proves the author did enough preparatory work to ensure the project complements rather than duplicates existing efforts.
- **Failure mode:** Missing even a single reference to directly relevant work can be very costly. It signals failure to connect with the conversation your project claims to join.
- The bibliography is not decoration; it is evidence of preparatory rigor and intellectual honesty.

### 6. What's the Payoff? (Significance / Justification)

Disciplinary norms for justifying research differ widely. The authors identify several legitimate payoff arguments, ranked roughly by persuasiveness:

1. **Theoretical significance:** For most social scientists, explicit theoretical interest counts heavily. The proposal should situate the local problem in terms of live, sometimes controversial, theoretical currents. Show where your inquiry intersects main theoretical debates and how it puts established ideas to the test or offers new ones. Demonstrate awareness of alternative viewpoints and argue your position in a way that addresses the field broadly, not just one sectarian tendency.
2. **Understudied terrain:** The fact that less is known about your chosen case, period, or country than about comparable ones can work in your favor. (Between two methodologically identical projects, reviewers prefer the one set in less-studied territory.)
3. **Intrinsic importance of subject matter:** Turning points, crucial breakthroughs, central personages, fundamental institutions — appeals to the significance of the object of research are effective *if argued* rather than merely asserted.
4. **Current urgency:** Linking the project to a timely problem can work — but with a critical condition: the urgency must be argued as a *window into a more abiding problem*, not just a timely topic. Timely topics draw crowded fields; the decisive and exciting contribution has often already been made by the time you complete funding, fieldwork, and write-up.
5. **Novelty of approach (surprise, puzzle, apparent contradiction):** Presenting a finding or premise that violates conventional expectation is a powerful rhetorical move, especially for reviewers whose disciplinary superego enforces systematic model building. Example used in the essay: strong unions do not strike but instead restrain workers' wage demands — the opposite of what the slogan "One Big Union" predicted.

**Crowded-field warning:** Topics of current theoretical or real-world salience are *likely to be crowded*. Unless you have something original to say, you may be well advised to avoid topics about which everyone is writing. Follow your instinct toward problems far from the pack's course — "nothing is more valuable than a really fresh beginning."

### 7. Use a Fresh Approach

- Surprises, puzzles, and apparent contradictions persuade reviewers whose disciplinary superego enforces systematic model building or formal theorizing.
- It is often worthwhile to help readers understand how the research task grows from the **intellectual history or current intellectual life** of the country or region that generated it. Council committees strive to build linkages across diverse national and international intellectual traditions. The interplay of diverse traditions is *itself a reason committee members accept appointment* — it is a motive to which proposals can legitimately appeal.
- Fresh approaches signal genuine intellectual engagement rather than programmatic execution of a predetermined result.

### 8. Describe Your Methodology

Two non-negotiable requirements for methodological sections:

1. **Specify the research operations** you will undertake *and* the way you will interpret the results of those operations in terms of your central problem. Do not just state what you mean to achieve — tell how you will spend your time while doing it.
2. **Argue why those tasks add up to the best attack on the problem.** A methodology section is not a list of tasks to perform — an agenda by itself normally does not suffice because the mere listing of tasks does not prove they add up to the best feasible approach.

**Common failure mode — vague operations:** "I will look at the relation between x and y" is not a research operation. Specify: are you digging through archives, conducting interviews, observing and taking standardized notes, collecting and testing statistical patterns? Tell the reviewer exactly what you will do.

**Specificity requirements:** Name the archives, the sources, the respondents, and the proposed techniques of analysis.

**Help readers calibrate:** The process of gathering data and moving from data to interpretation follows disciplinary customs that are more standard in some fields than others. Help readers from other fields recognize which parts of your methodology are standard and which are innovative. Be as specific as possible about:
- Activities to collect information
- Techniques to analyze it
- Tests of validity to which you commit yourself

**Most proposals fail** because they leave reviewers wondering what the applicant will actually do.

**Comparative design:** A research design proposing comparison between cases often has special appeal. All research is implicitly comparative; making the comparison explicit raises its value as scientific inquiry. Evaluators ask:
- Are the cases chosen so that their similarities and differences illuminate the central question?
- Is the proposer in a position to execute both legs of the comparison?

When both answers are yes, the proposal fares particularly well.

**Mastery of technical requirements:** The proposal should prove that the researcher either possesses, or cooperates with people who possess, mastery of all technical matters the project entails (language skills, statistical expertise, laboratory access, collaboration arrangements).

### 9. Specify Your Objectives (Closing / Deliverables)

- A well-composed proposal, like a sonata, **ends by alluding to the original theme.** Return to the central question: How will the research procedures and their products connect back to that question? How will you know if your idea was wrong or right?
- In some disciplines this means a **falsifiable hypothesis** — while respecting this canon, committee members are also open to less formal approaches.
- What matters is to convince readers that:
  - Something is genuinely at stake in the inquiry
  - The project is not tendentiously moving toward a preconceived end
  - The unknowns will yield interesting, orderly propositions
- **Describe the final product:** article, book, chapter, dissertation. Specifying the kind of journal in which you hope to publish, or the kind of audience you hope to address, helps readers understand features of the proposal that might otherwise seem odd.
- Include all requested enclosures and appendices.

### 10. Final Note — Process Advice

- To write a good proposal takes a long time. **Start early.**
- Begin thinking about your topic well in advance; make it a habit to collect references while working on other tasks.
- Write a first draft **at least three months in advance**, revise it, show it to colleagues.
- Let it gather dust, collect colleagues' comments, revise again.
- If possible, share it with a seminar or similar group — the debate will help anticipate what reviewers will eventually think.
- Revise the text again for substance.
- Then go over language, style, and form.
- **Resharpen your opening paragraph** or first page last — so that it drives home exactly what you mean as effectively as possible.

### 11. Failure Mode Taxonomy (Synthesized from the Essay)

| Failure Mode | Description |
|---|---|
| Buried lede | The central question / claim is not on page 1 |
| Jargon overload | Technical language legible only within one discipline |
| Stale bibliography | Missing key references; outdated state-of-the-art |
| Vague operations | "I will look at x and y" — no named research operations |
| Agenda without argument | Task list without argument for why tasks add up to the best approach |
| Tendentious framing | Project appears to be marching toward a preconceived conclusion |
| Missing technical credibility | No evidence that researcher can execute required technical tasks |
| Crowded-field proposal | Hot topic where decisive contribution has already been made |
| No memorable hook | Reviewer cannot identify proposal by its claim after reading 100 others |
| Significance by assertion | Importance of topic stated but not argued |

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode: Proposal-writing agent (Layer 1b)**

This document should be the primary rhetorical framework for any fellowship or research grant proposal Bilal drafts. Concrete agent moves:

### Pre-drafting checklist
- Confirm the proposal answers all three core questions (learn / worth knowing / validity) — if any is unclear, force articulation before drafting.
- Identify the central claim or non-obvious question that will serve as the memorable hook.
- Audit the bibliography for completeness and currency; flag missing references before submitting.

### Opening paragraph audit
- Apply the "overstate, add caveats later" heuristic. If the opening paragraph hedges, qualifies, or provides background before making a claim — rewrite it.
- Test: could a committee member identify this proposal by its central claim alone after reading 100 others?

### Significance section
- Identify which payoff argument(s) apply (theoretical, understudied terrain, intrinsic importance, urgency-as-window, novel approach).
- Ensure significance is argued, not merely asserted.
- Check whether the topic is crowded; if so, articulate what makes this proposal's angle genuinely fresh.

### Methodology section
- Replace every vague operation ("I will investigate") with a named research operation (specific dataset, analysis technique, validity test).
- Make the argument explicit: why do these particular tasks add up to the best feasible attack on the problem?
- Separate standard disciplinary procedure from innovative elements — explain both to a multidisciplinary reader.

### Closing section
- Verify the sonata structure: does the ending return to the opening theme/question?
- Name the final product (paper, dissertation chapter, conference presentation).
- State what would count as evidence that the central claim is wrong.

### Jargon pass
- Scan for technical terms that lack common-language equivalents; define each one.
- Move technical ancillary material to appendices.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Bilal is writing proposals at two levels: (1) academic fellowships and PhD research funding (NSF, SSRC, AFRL-adjacent programs), and (2) SBIR/STTR and government contracts for SiS as a company working on Space Domain Awareness.

Przeworski & Salomon's framework applies directly to the academic tier and carries strong analogues to the commercial tier:

**The three questions, translated to SiS:**
1. *What will we learn?* → What does CTPC enable that SGP4 + standard ML cannot? (Precise answer: real-time uncertainty quantification with dissipation-preserving architecture, without sacrificing physical consistency.)
2. *Why is it worth knowing?* → Conjunction Analysis / space traffic management for LEO megaconstellations requires calibrated covariance propagation; current tools fail on non-conservative perturbations at timescales shorter than TLE update cadence.
3. *How will we know the conclusions are valid?* → Calibration metrics (ECE, Brier score), conjunction probability accuracy against CARA ground truth, on-orbit TLE residual benchmarks.

**Fresh approach / surprise move:** SiS's core claim — that physical dissipation can be enforced as a hard architectural constraint (Cholesky parameterization, Port-Hamiltonian blocks) rather than a soft penalty — is exactly the kind of "surprising, puzzle-resolving" framing Przeworski & Salomon say persuades reviewers whose disciplinary superego demands systematic model building. Frame it that way: "Everyone assumed soft-penalty regularization was sufficient. Here is why it is not, and what happens when you bake the constraint into the architecture."

**Crowded-field awareness:** Physics-informed ML is currently a very crowded field. The Przeworski & Salomon warning applies directly: unless SiS proposals articulate what makes the SDA / orbital-mechanics application genuinely distinct (not just another PINN paper), reviewers will discount novelty. The distinctive angle is the *deployment context* (real-time, uncertainty-critical, SDA-specific) and the *hard-constraint architecture* — lead with those, not with the general category "physics-informed."

**Multidisciplinary committee translation:** NSF panels, AFRL technical reviewers, and SBIR evaluators are all multidisciplinary in the relevant sense — an astrodynamicist may not know Port-Hamiltonian theory; an ML reviewer may not know TLE error statistics. The essay's clarity heuristics apply: argue concepts, not apparatus; use appendices for technical depth; define every acronym.

**Bibliography rigor:** The SiS wiki's ingest corpus is itself a bibliography-building exercise. Before any proposal submission, cross-reference the proposal's claims against the full wiki index to verify no directly relevant reference is missing.

## Open Questions

1. **SBIR/STTR applicability:** Przeworski & Salomon write for academic fellowship committees. How much of this transfers to SBIR/STTR Program Managers, who are evaluated on portfolio impact and transition probability, not pure academic merit? Where do the heuristics break down?
2. **Theoretical interest for engineering proposals:** The essay emphasizes theoretical interest as the top-ranked payoff argument for social scientists. Does this translate to NSF/AFRL engineering proposals, where practical impact and TRL advancement may outrank theoretical novelty?
3. **The "not tendentiously moving toward a preconceived end" requirement:** CTPC has a clear design commitment (physics-as-architecture). How does a proposal demonstrate genuine inquiry rather than predetermined advocacy for a method the lab has already built?
4. **Opening overstatement calibrated to SBIR:** SBIR program announcements often specify technical requirements. Can the "overstate now, qualify later" heuristic survive SBIR's highly structured solicitation format?
5. **Comparative design for SDA:** The essay notes comparative designs have special appeal. What would a meaningful comparative proposal look like for CTPC — e.g., comparing dissipation-preserving vs. soft-penalty approaches on controlled orbital regimes?

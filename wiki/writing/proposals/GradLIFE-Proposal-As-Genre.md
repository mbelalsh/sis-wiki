# The Art of Proposal Writing: Proposal as Genre

## One-Line Intuition

A research proposal is not a report of what you found — it is a **hero's quest narrative**: you identify an unexplored intellectual territory, argue why venturing into it matters, and convince the funder to bankroll the expedition.

## Why This Matters

Most graduate students approach proposals as scaled-up paper abstracts or lab-report introductions. This guide reframes the genre entirely. Understanding *what kind of writing a proposal is* — a rhetorical genre with its own conventions, audience expectations, and narrative arc — is the prerequisite to writing one well. Getting the genre wrong means the reviewer reads competent prose that fails to persuade, even if the science is sound. For Bilal, whose proposals must win over NSF, AFOSR, DoD, and SBIR reviewers from very different communities, genre fluency is a force-multiplier that no technical depth alone can substitute.

## Core Content

### 1. The Genre Definition: Quest, Not Report

The document opens by distinguishing the proposal genre from the paper genre:

| Document type | What it lays out |
|---|---|
| Academic paper | The **results** of an investigation already completed |
| Research proposal | The **path and the quest** — an investigation not yet done |

This is the load-bearing insight: a proposal is prospective and narrative, not retrospective and evidentiary. The reader must be made curious about an **unfinished story**, curious enough to fund the next chapter.

Otto Yang's framing (quoted in the document):

> "Research progress is very much like an ongoing story, with plot twists and surprises. A well-written application creates a tale that appeals to the reader. … Unlike a novel, however, the story is unfinished … and the questions you propose should reveal how you will unfold the next chapter."

The explicit goal stated in the guide: **"capture the reviewers' imagination and make them curious about the outcome of the research — curious enough that they want to give you money to pursue it."**

### 2. The Hero-Tale Frame

The guide frames proposals as a form of the universal **hero tale**:

- Every culture has stories of a hero going on a quest, overcoming challenges, and gaining a reward.
- In academia, the hero is the scientist or scholar whose hard work brings new knowledge.
- Reviewers are looking for **the person whose clear sense of purpose will motivate them to complete their quest**.

Practical implication: a proposal must not only describe a project — it must characterize the *proposer* as the right hero for this particular quest. Reviewer confidence in the *person* (vision, drive, preparation) is as important as confidence in the *plan*.

### 3. The Four Orienting Questions

The guide structures good proposal writing around four diagnostic questions. Every proposal must answer all four, explicitly or implicitly.

#### Q1: What quest are you pursuing?

- Your quest = your **research question**.
- A good question is **new** (not already answered) and **important** (the field needs to know).
- Articulate it clearly; give context for why it matters.
  - Will it solve a pressing problem?
  - Will it explore a neglected area?
- The language for framing a research question differs by discipline, but the requirement is universal: **the reviewer must be clear on the fundamental question you are addressing**.
- **Mini-quest caveat**: if your full project exceeds the fellowship duration, identify and foreground the **mini-quest** completable within the funding period. Show the funder exactly what their money buys: preliminary research, data collection, dissertation chapters, etc.

#### Q2: What broader vision drives your question?

- Graduate students are at the start of a long career; funders are investing in that career, not just one project.
- Articulate a **broad vision** for long-term contributions — not only to your field, but to **society as a whole**.
- The research you anticipate pursuing during and beyond graduate school may:
  - Help us understand certain social or cultural phenomena
  - Improve health or education outcomes
  - Lead to medical and technological advances
- **"Make that connection explicit for the reviewer."** Do not assume the reviewer will infer societal value; state it.

#### Q3: What is the funder's mission?

- Reviewers are charged with selecting applicants whose research **supports the funder's vision**.
- Funders vary enormously in focus:
  - Narrow: "egg nutrition," "cave and karst studies"
  - Broad: addressing specific societal challenges, fostering interdisciplinary work
- Action: **review the funder's website carefully** to understand their goals; **spell out the fit** between your work and their mission in the proposal itself.
- This is not optional flattery — it is genre compliance. Proposals that don't demonstrate funder-mission fit fail on a structural level.

#### Q4: Who is your audience?

- Three possible audience types (the guide identifies all three):
  1. **Disciplinary audience** — experts in your specific field
  2. **Topical audience** — knowledgeable about a broader domain, not your subfield
  3. **Broad interdisciplinary audience** — generalist reviewers from many fields
- The audience type controls:
  - **Terminology** (can you use field jargon, or must you define everything?)
  - **Which elements of your vision to foreground** (technical depth vs. broader stakes)
- Identify your audience before you write a single sentence of the proposal narrative.

### 4. Proposal as "Story of Ideas"

The guide frames the proposal as "a story of ideas that explores uncharted intellectual territory." This has structural consequences:

- The narrative must have a **gap** (something unknown or unsolved)
- A **hero** (you, with the skills and knowledge of the field)
- A **means** (your proposed method for filling the gap)
- A **reward** (what the field and society gain)

This four-part story arc maps onto the standard proposal sections: significance/background establishes the gap; PI qualifications establish the hero; methodology establishes the means; broader impacts establish the reward.

### 5. The "Unfinished Story" Convention

Unlike a novel, the proposal's story is **unfinished by design**. The questions you propose should reveal how you will unfold the next chapter — not answer it. This is a genre convention that trips up technical writers who feel compelled to show they already know the answer. A proposal that reads like a foregone conclusion undermines the funder's reason for funding: they are paying for the *discovery*, not for a report you could already write.

### 6. Funder-Mission Alignment as Genre Requirement

The guide is explicit that funder-mission fit is not a formality — it is structurally required. Some funders have narrow mandates (e.g., a specific organism or geological formation); others have broad mandates (societal challenges, interdisciplinary work). In both cases:

- Read the funder's website as carefully as you read the solicitation.
- Identify the exact language they use to describe their mission.
- Mirror that language (accurately, not just lexically) in your proposal.

### 7. Audience Calibration Controls Everything

Audience identification is presented as a prerequisite to *all* other writing decisions. It governs:

- **Terminology**: disciplinary terms vs. plain language
- **Framing of significance**: technical contribution vs. societal benefit
- **Level of methodological detail**: assumed vs. explained
- **Which aspects of vision to highlight**: narrow precision vs. broad impact

The guide does not recommend one audience over another — different mechanisms (NSF GRFP, NIH, AFOSR, SBIR) attract different reviewer pools. The writer's job is to diagnose the audience first, then write.

### 8. Summary of Moves (Genre Checklist)

| Move | What to do |
|---|---|
| Identify your quest | State the research question clearly; give importance context |
| Scope the mini-quest | If full project > funding period, define what the funding period accomplishes |
| Articulate broad vision | Connect your work to long-term field and societal contributions |
| Align with funder mission | Research funder's goals; spell out the fit explicitly |
| Identify your audience | Choose: disciplinary / topical / broad; calibrate terminology and framing |
| Write the hero narrative | Position yourself as the right person with the preparation and drive |
| Leave the story unfinished | Propose questions, not pre-answered results |

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Agent:** Proposal-Writing-Agent  
**Mode:** Pre-draft framing / structural planning  
**When to invoke:** Before any proposal drafting begins — this page provides the genre contract that governs all subsequent decisions.

### Concrete moves for the Proposal-Writing-Agent:

1. **Genre check first**: Before touching a draft, ask: "Does this read like a quest narrative (prospective, gap-driven, hero-positioned) or like a paper abstract (retrospective, results-summarizing)?" If the latter, flag and reframe.

2. **Four-question audit**: Run every proposal draft through the four questions — quest clarity, broader vision, funder-mission fit, audience identification. Any question with a weak or absent answer is a structural deficit, not a stylistic one.

3. **Mini-quest scoping**: For SBIR Phase I (6 months) or NSF GRFP (3-year arc), explicitly scope the mini-quest: "What does *this* funding period accomplish?" This prevents scope creep and gives the funder a clear deliverable.

4. **Funder-mission mirroring**: Pull the funder's mission language (NSF: "broader impacts," "intellectual merit"; AFOSR: "basic research that underpins warfighter advantage"; SBIR: "commercialization potential," "technical/scientific merit"). Mirror it explicitly.

5. **Audience tagging**: Tag each proposal with its audience type before drafting. NSF GRFP panels are broad interdisciplinary — minimize subfield jargon, maximize societal stakes. AFOSR BAA panels are topical-to-disciplinary — technical depth is expected.

6. **Hero positioning**: Ensure the proposal characterizes *Bilal* (or the PI) as the right hero: the preparation, the track record, the clear sense of purpose. The method alone is insufficient; the reviewer must believe in the person.

7. **Unfinished story test**: Scan the proposal for sentences that sound like "we expect to find X" or "our preliminary results show X." These risk making the proposal feel like a foregone conclusion. Reframe as: "We will investigate whether X holds under conditions Y and Z, which remain unresolved."

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

SiS targets NSF (SBIR, potentially GRFP for Bilal's PhD funding), AFOSR (basic research in SDA), DoD/DARPA (systems-level space defense), and NASA. Each funder has a distinct mission, audience type, and hero expectation:

| Funder | Audience type | Mission language to mirror | Key hero trait to emphasize |
|---|---|---|---|
| NSF GRFP | Broad interdisciplinary | "intellectual merit," "broader impacts" | Graduate student with broad vision, societal commitment |
| NSF SBIR | Topical (program officers) | "commercialization potential," "innovative research" | Technical founder with path to market |
| AFOSR | Disciplinary to topical | "basic research," "warfighter advantage," "autonomy," "resiliency" | Rigorous researcher whose work underpins future capability |
| DoD SBIR/STTR | Mixed | "transition to military use," "TRL advancement" | Engineer with deployment-ready thinking |
| NASA | Disciplinary | "mission relevance," "science return" | Domain expert with mission-specific knowledge |

The **CTPC framework** (Continuous-Time Probabilistic Corrector) is the quest at the center of every SiS proposal. The genre framing from this document maps directly:

- **The gap**: SGP4 produces biased, non-calibrated covariances; no physics-constrained ML corrector exists for operational SDA.
- **The hero**: Bilal, with training in geometric DL, physics-constrained architectures, and orbital mechanics.
- **The means**: CTPC — a physics-as-architecture corrector with hard dissipation constraints, trained on TLE residuals.
- **The reward**: calibrated conjunction probability for Space Domain Awareness operators; reduced collision risk for LEO/MEO.

The **broader vision** for SIS proposals must explicitly connect CTPC to:
- Sustainability of the space environment (societal)
- National security (DoD/AFOSR)
- Commercial space economy (NSF SBIR)

This document's instruction — "make that connection explicit for the reviewer" — means the SiS proposal must not assume reviewers will connect "better orbital covariances" to "fewer Kessler-cascade-inducing collisions" on their own.

## Open Questions

1. **Fellowship vs. research grant genre differences**: This guide focuses on fellowship proposals (where the hero is a graduate student). How do the genre conventions shift for research grants (where the hero is a lab or PI team)? Do funders like AFOSR/DARPA expect less hero-characterization and more institutional credibility?

2. **Audience calibration for hybrid panels**: SBIR panels often include both technical reviewers and commercialization reviewers with very different backgrounds. Which audience type governs the core narrative, and which governs supplementary sections (commercialization plan, team)?

3. **The "unfinished story" tension with preliminary results**: AFOSR and NIH expect substantial preliminary data. How do you show enough evidence to be credible without making the story feel complete? Where is the line between "we have evidence this is tractable" and "we already know the answer"?

4. **Career-stage calibration**: The guide is written for graduate students. As SiS matures from startup to established company, does the "hero tale" shift — from "promising graduate student with a vision" to "experienced team with a track record"? What changes in the genre conventions?

5. **Funder-mission mirroring depth**: When should mirroring go beyond language to restructuring entire sections around funder priorities? E.g., for an AFOSR proposal, should the significance section foreground "warfighter advantage" before "scientific contribution," or is that too explicit?

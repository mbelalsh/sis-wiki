# The Art of Proposal Writing: Proposal as Roadmap

## One-Line Intuition

A proposal is the detailed roadmap you hand your reviewers so they can confidently follow your intellectual journey from the known territory of your field into uncharted territory — and believe you will arrive.

## Why This Matters

The companion piece [[GradLIFE-Proposal-As-Genre]] establishes *what* a proposal is rhetorically (a story of an intellectual journey). This document is the operational sequel: *how* to structure the map you hand the reviewer for that journey. It makes concrete the section-by-section moves a proposal must execute, and names the reviewer-psychology behind each section. For Bilal, writing NSF GRFP, AFOSR YIP, DoD SBIR, or fellowship proposals, this is the minimum viable checklist for structural completeness.

## Core Content

### The Central Metaphor

The proposal is a **roadmap** for an intellectual road trip into uncharted territory. The reviewers are excited travelers who want to come along — but they need the map. Each proposal section is one layer of that map:

| Section | Roadmap function |
|---|---|
| Introduction | Overview of the whole journey: where you're going and how |
| Background | The roads already traveled — why your next leg is the logical next step |
| Research Plan | The route into uncharted territory — general to specific |
| Resources | The vehicle and supplies — why you can complete the trip |
| Pitfalls & Contingency Plans | The spare tire — proof you've anticipated flat tires |
| Anticipated Outcomes | The destination — what you'll have when you arrive |
| Fit with Funder's Mission | Why the funder cares about *this* destination |
| Dissemination | The travelogue — how others will hear about what you found |
| Funder-Specific Sections | Custom waypoints required by the particular funder |

### Section-by-Section Principles

#### Introduction
- Give the reviewer a **general sense of where your journey is taking you and how you plan to get there**.
- Construct the opening as a **mini-proposal**: broader issue → central question or hypothesis → overall approach → anticipated outcome.
- This is *not* the place to dive into details. Stay at 30,000 feet.
- Reviewer psychology: they need to be oriented before they can evaluate. A confused reviewer is a hostile reviewer.

#### Background
- Establish **why your question or topic is important**.
  - Identified a gap in current understanding?
  - Pursuing a promising avenue of inquiry?
  - Offering a new interpretation of evidence?
- Provide a **selective overview of research in your field** upon which you intend to build — not a complete literature dump, but the lines of work that logically lead to your project.
- Highlight recent advances or controversies.
- Discuss preliminary research you have already carried out.
- **Exit criterion**: by the end of the Background, the reader should understand that your proposed project is a **logical next step in a succession of advances in research** — those advances are the intellectual road trip you've been on *with other people in your field*.

#### Research Plan
- "Now we're moving into uncharted territory!"
- Lead the reader **from general to specific**.
- Outline:
  - The type of data you will collect
  - The methodology you will use to analyze it
- A primary research question may decompose into **several smaller sub-questions**, or you may pursue **multiple independent aims**, each with its own data-collection plan and method of analysis.
- This section is the heart of the roadmap — the route itself.

#### Resources
- Goal: give the funder **confidence in your ability to complete the journey**.
- Identify:
  - The lab, center, or institute where the work will be carried out
  - Advisors, mentors, or hosts
  - Special equipment or materials (and that they will be at your disposal)
  - Access to field sites, subjects, informants, or datasets — including what arrangements have already been made
- All permutations carry the same message: **"I will have the resources I need and the guidance to use them effectively."**

#### Pitfalls and Contingency Plans
- Metaphor: "What's a road trip without a flat tire? Do you have a jack and a spare tire in your car?"
- You do **not** need to discuss routine, well-established methodologies.
- You **do** need to show:
  - You have anticipated the **major problems** that could derail the project
  - You have given thought to **alternatives** for each
- Consult your advisor and experienced researchers to identify non-obvious failure modes.
- Reviewer psychology: this section signals intellectual maturity and de-risks the investment.

#### Anticipated Outcomes
- Answer: at the end of your journey, what do you expect to have **accomplished**?
- What **new knowledge or understanding** will you be able to share upon successful completion?
- The Background section cited others' work — Anticipated Outcomes explains why **others might cite you** on their own intellectual journeys at some future point.
- This is a high-level impact statement, not a deliverable list.

#### Fit with the Funder's Mission
- Reviewers work on behalf of the funder and want to understand **how your project advances the funder's mission**.
- Return to your vision and to your understanding of the **target audience**.
- Funders often have **multiple goals** (e.g., "support basic science" *in order to* "improve cancer treatment") — address each one explicitly in your narrative.
- Research the funder's program goals, strategic plans, and recent award portfolios before writing this section.

#### Dissemination
- "At the end of your journey, will there be a travelogue?"
- For work to have impact, others must know about it — tell the reviewer **how you intend to share your results**.
- Be specific:
  - Which conferences you plan to submit to
  - Which journals you will target
- Note any **funder-specific dissemination requirements** (e.g., presentation at the funder's annual meeting) and commit to fulfilling them.

#### Sections Specific to the Funder
- Common additions: Timeline, Originality section, Interdisciplinarity section.
- "Include a timeline" is obvious; less obvious moves:
  - If a funder says the work must be "original" but provides no required section heading, **create an "Originality" section** — it helps the reviewer find and credit the information.
  - Same for "Interdisciplinarity" or other criterion-driven attributes.
- Principle: **make it easy for the reviewer to match your proposal to the evaluation criteria**. Don't make them hunt.

### Cross-Cutting Principles

1. **No single right structure.** Disciplinary norms and funder guidelines vary. Read successful proposals in your field and read the program solicitation carefully before choosing a structure.

2. **There are commonalities across all proposals.** The eight sections above appear in some form across disciplines and funders — treat them as the universal skeleton.

3. **Collaborate on the roadmap.** Work with your advisor and committee members to build a clear map. Then get reviewers on board and help them envision the journey from start to finish.

4. **Reviewer-centric writing throughout.** Every section asks: what does the reviewer need to know *right now* to stay on board? Feed them information in the order they need it to maintain confidence.

5. **Logical succession is the spine.** The Background-to-Research-Plan transition is the most critical joint in the proposal: the reader must feel that your project is the *inevitable* next step given what the field has already done.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode: Proposal-Writing-Agent (Layer 1b)**

This document is a **structural checklist and section-purpose reference**. Invoke it in the following scenarios:

### During proposal outlining
- Use the eight-section skeleton as the default structural template before adapting to funder guidelines.
- For each section, apply the "roadmap function" column of the table above to test whether the section is doing its job.

### Diagnosing a weak proposal draft
- Is the Introduction acting as a mini-proposal (issue → question → approach → outcome)? If not, flag and restructure.
- Does the Background end with the "logical next step" framing? If the reader cannot see why this project follows from what the field has done, the Background has failed.
- Is the Research Plan moving general → specific? A plan that starts with specific methods before orienting the reviewer will lose them.
- Is there a Pitfalls section? Its absence signals intellectual naivety to reviewers.
- Does Fit with Funder address *all* the funder's stated goals, not just the primary one?
- Is Dissemination specific (named venues), not vague ("we will publish and present")?

### Reviewing a near-complete draft
- Check for funder-specific criterion sections. If the solicitation mentions "originality," "interdisciplinarity," "broader impacts," or any other evaluative criterion — is there a clearly labeled section for each? If not, add one.
- Confirm the roadmap is navigable: can a reviewer who reads only section headings reconstruct the shape of the journey?

### Concrete moves
- **Introduction opener**: state the broader field problem in one sentence, then the specific gap in one sentence, then the proposed question/hypothesis, then the approach in two sentences, then the anticipated contribution in one sentence. Total: ~5–6 sentences maximum.
- **Background exit sentence**: "Together, these advances position [your project] as the logical next step in [field trajectory]."
- **Research Plan lead**: "The proposed work moves from [general objective] to [specific aims], structured as follows: ..."
- **Resources confidence-builder**: list institution + advisor + key infrastructure + any pre-arranged access to data/subjects in a tight paragraph ending with an explicit capability claim.
- **Pitfalls framing**: "The primary risk to Aim X is [failure mode]. Should this occur, we will [contingency]. This alternative is feasible because [evidence]."
- **Anticipated Outcomes framing**: "Successful completion will produce [knowledge type], enabling [downstream use by field]. This work will be citable by researchers pursuing [related questions]."
- **Funder-fit framing**: open with the funder's stated mission verbatim, then connect your project explicitly to each sub-goal.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

SiS targets NSF GRFP (Bilal's PhD fellowship), AFOSR YIP, DoD SBIR Phase I/II, and potentially NASA and DARPA programs. Each of these funders has distinct but overlapping proposal requirements. The roadmap framework applies directly:

- **Introduction as mini-proposal**: SiS proposals must orient reviewers quickly — AFOSR program managers are technical but not orbital-mechanics specialists; SBIR reviewers are often industry-adjacent. The mini-proposal opener (SDA problem → uncertainty-propagation gap → CTPC approach → mission-assurance outcome) must work for all audiences.

- **Background → logical next step**: The SiS narrative arc is well-defined: SGP4 error characterization → physics-informed ML correctors → CTPC. The Background section must show that CTPC is the inevitable synthesis of these threads, not an arbitrary design choice.

- **Resources**: SiS can leverage Iowa State's Coordinated Systems Lab (CSL), Bilal's advisor, access to Space-Track TLE data, and computational resources. These need to be stated explicitly to de-risk the proposal for DoD funders who worry about student-led startups lacking infrastructure.

- **Pitfalls**: The known failure modes for CTPC include TLE data sparsity, distributional shift across orbital regimes, and the difficulty of enforcing strict Lyapunov stability during training. Each needs a named contingency in research proposals.

- **Fit with Funder**: AFOSR cares about basic research → warfighter readiness. NSF cares about broader impacts. SBIR cares about commercial transition. The Fit section must be rewritten for each funder, not recycled.

- **Dissemination**: SiS targets AAS/AIAA SciTech for SDA venues and ICML/NeurIPS for ML venues. These should be named explicitly, not left vague.

- **Funder-specific sections**: NSF requires an explicit Broader Impacts section; SBIR requires a Commercialization Plan; DoD proposals often require an S&T Transition section. Each is a criterion-mapping section in the spirit of this document.

## Open Questions

1. How does the roadmap structure adapt for **SBIR Phase I vs. Phase II**? Phase I is a feasibility study (shorter Research Plan, heavier Resources/Feasibility section); Phase II requires a Commercialization Plan that has no analog in the academic proposal skeleton here. A separate SBIR-specific checklist page may be warranted.

2. The document is silent on **length norms** per section. What proportion of narrative space should each section receive? For NSF GRFP (2-page Research Proposal), the allocation is radically compressed. A per-funder allocation table would be a useful extension of this page.

3. **Interdisciplinary proposals** (e.g., SiS sits at the intersection of orbital mechanics, ML, and systems engineering) face a challenge: the Background must establish credibility in multiple literatures simultaneously. The document gestures at an "Interdisciplinarity" section but doesn't explain how to write a Background that spans disciplines without losing any reviewer.

4. The document recommends reading "successful proposals in your discipline" — where can Bilal access funded NSF GRFP, AFOSR YIP, or SBIR proposals in the physics-informed ML / SDA space? Building a proposal exemplar corpus (analogous to the paper exemplar corpus in `raw/writing/exemplars/`) would be high-value.

5. The Pitfalls section advises consulting advisors — but for a startup CTO writing an SBIR, the advisor may be the PI or a technical board member. How does the Pitfalls section read differently when the "researcher" is also responsible for commercialization? The contingency plans need to cover both technical and go-to-market failure modes.

# CMU Literature Reviews — Connecting, Synthesizing, and Evaluating Prior Work

## One-Line Intuition

A literature review is not a list of summaries — it is a synthesis that groups prior work into trends, exposes the gap those trends leave open, and positions your contribution as the answer.

## Why This Matters

Reviewers and readers use the literature review to decide two things simultaneously: (1) does the author know this field? and (2) is there actually a gap that justifies this paper? Most failed literature reviews fail on the second question — they summarize sources one-by-one (becoming annotated bibliographies) instead of synthesizing them into a coherent field-level narrative. This handout from CMU's Student Academic Success Center teaches the structural and sentence-level moves needed to write a review that does both jobs at once.

---

## Core Content

### What a Literature Review IS and IS NOT

**It IS:**
- A selective, purposeful grouping of prior work into themes, trends, agreements, and disagreements
- A synthesis that puts authors in conversation with each other, not with you individually
- An argument that culminates in a gap — the gap that your paper fills

**It IS NOT:**
- An annotated bibliography (one paragraph per source)
- A data dump of every relevant paper you found
- A summary of what each author said in isolation

**Three purposes (all three must be served):**

| # | Purpose | What it signals to the reader |
|---|---------|-------------------------------|
| 1 | Situate your work in the broader scholarly community | Your work grew organically from current trends; it is not invented in a vacuum |
| 2 | Show you have "done your homework" | Credibility: you know the major agreements, debates, and critical findings |
| 3 | Illustrate a gap in prior research | Necessity: your unique contribution fills something real |

The third purpose is load-bearing for research papers — without it, reviewers cannot evaluate novelty. The handout explicitly cross-references the "Novelty Moves" handout for gap-construction moves (see [[CMU-Establishing-Novelty-Four-Moves]]).

---

### Structural Rule: One Paragraph = One Trend, Not One Author

This is the central rule. Each paragraph should:
- Open with a topic sentence that names a **trend across multiple studies**, not a single author
- Cite multiple works inside the paragraph to support the trend claim
- Assess limitations shared across the studies (not just one study's limitations)
- End with a connecting sentence to the next trend or to the current study's gap

**Exception:** A single formative/foundational paper may earn its own paragraph if it is genuinely singular in the field. This should be rare.

### Topic Sentence Craft: The Before/After Framework

The handout provides two annotated before/after example pairs. This is the most teachable content.

#### Move 1 — Replace author-centric openers with trend-centric openers

**BEFORE (wrong pattern) — Praise/Feedback example:**
Each paragraph starts with an author name as subject and summarizes only that one study:
- "Carol Dweck states that praise has a distinct effect on the way students view intelligence…"
- "Larry Van Brimner conducted a study at a high school writing center and found that…"
- "Richard Straub's examination of students' response to written teacher feedback found that…"

Annotation labels from the handout: *Summarizes one text / Too many details on single author / Overexplains individual study*

**AFTER (correct pattern):**
Topic sentences lead with the trend; individual authors are cited parenthetically inside the paragraph body:
- "Research has shown that praise is more productive than criticism or negative feedback." → then multiple authors (Hyland & Hyland, Brimner, Dragga, Zak) cited inside
- "Studies also show that praising students for their intelligence is less helpful than praising for their effort…" → Dweck cited parenthetically
- "Research on more advanced students have echoed those findings." → Straub and Dragga cited inside

Annotation labels from the handout: *Focuses on trend / Connects multiple studies / Situates individual authors within trend*

#### Move 2 — Replace per-study data dumps with trend groupings that culminate in a gap

**BEFORE (wrong pattern) — Wind/CAES storage example:**
Three consecutive paragraphs each open with a specific author name, summarize only that study, and provide data with no connecting tissue:
- "Denholm and Sioshansi (2009) compared the costs of…"
- "Greenblatt et al. (2007) model CAES and conventional gas generators…"
- "DeCarolis and Keith (2006) optimize the use of…"

Then a fourth paragraph tries to compare them retroactively, creating a "data dump" with "no connecting sentence after data dump, forcing the reader to reread."

Annotation labels: *Summarizes only one study / Doesn't relate to current study / Only one study discussed / Data dump / No connecting sentence after data dump*

**AFTER (correct pattern) — Password corpora example:**
Three paragraphs each open with a named research trend:
1. "A review of the literature shows that many researchers use password corpora collected from various security leaks…" → cites [14, 24, 26, 35, 53, 55] → assesses shared limitation: no contextual information, difficult to verify
2. "On the other hand, there is an extensive literature that describes researchers asking users to self-report password information…" → cites [7, 33, 37, 47, 49, 58] → assesses shared limitation: reliability concerns
3. "Finally, a small number of researchers have been able to work with large organizations to collect authentic data." → covers Florêncio/Herley + Bonneau → shared limitation: "researchers were able to record only extremely limited information about the content of the passwords"

The paragraph then pivots directly to the current study: "In contrast, our paper connects information about each user with analysis of that user's plaintext…"

Final paragraph explicitly names the overcome gap: "In this paper, we overcome many limitations of past studies."

Annotation labels: *Summary of research trends / Research trend / Assesses limitations of previous research / Indicates method weakness with all studies / Connects to current study / Illustrates how current study overcomes limitations of previous research*

### Sentence-Level Phrase Bank

The handout provides explicit phrases for two functions. Use these as slot-fillers when drafting topic sentences.

**Agreement phrases (topic sentences showing consensus):**
- "One trend in the research is…."
- "Research seems to agree that...."
- "Numerous authors support the claim that…."
- "There is strong convergent evidence for...."

**Disagreement phrases (topic sentences showing debate):**
- "The evidence on X is mixed for...."
- "There is overall debate regarding...."
- "A lack of consensus exists on the point of…"
- "There are two conflicting camps on the issue of…."

---

### Stand-Alone vs. Embedded Literature Reviews

- **Embedded (most common):** introductory section of a research paper, proposal, journal article, or thesis — the review serves the three purposes above and terminates in a gap that the paper fills
- **Stand-alone:** surveys the state of a field without advocating for a specific contribution — common in review journals and systematic reviews

For research papers and proposals, always assume the embedded form and the three-purpose structure.

---

### The Gap Construction Pattern (Structural Capstone)

Every effective embedded literature review terminates with an explicit gap statement that bridges from "what has been done" to "what we do." The password corpus example demonstrates the canonical form:

1. Survey Trend A → limitation of Trend A
2. Survey Trend B → limitation of Trend B
3. Survey Trend C → limitation shared across all trends
4. **"In contrast, our paper…"** / **"In this paper, we overcome many limitations…"**

This is the structural equivalent of [[CMU-Establishing-Novelty-Four-Moves]]'s Move 3 (gap creation) + Move 4 (gap filling). The two handouts are designed to be used together.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode:** Active drafting of the Related Work / Prior Art section; also Background section of proposals.

**Which agent:** Paper-Writing-Agent (Layer 1a). Also directly applicable in proposal Background sections (Layer 1b), since proposals require the same three-purpose literature review structure.

### Concrete moves for the agent

**On first draft of Related Work:**
1. Cluster the citations the author has assembled into 2–5 thematic groups (not per-paper groups).
2. For each group, draft a topic sentence using one of the agreement/disagreement phrase templates above. The sentence should name the trend, not the first author's name.
3. Cite individual papers parenthetically inside the paragraph body.
4. After each group paragraph, add one sentence assessing the shared limitation across that cluster.
5. After all cluster paragraphs, draft the gap-construction closing: "In contrast, the present work…" or "This paper overcomes these limitations by…"

**On revision of an existing Related Work draft:**
1. Flag every paragraph whose first sentence names a single author as subject — these violate the trend-first rule.
2. Check whether any paragraph discusses only one source — merge it into a thematic cluster paragraph or justify the exception.
3. Check whether there is a gap statement at the end of the section — if not, draft one using the password corpus "In this paper, we overcome…" pattern.
4. Cross-check against [[CMU-Establishing-Novelty-Four-Moves]] to ensure the novelty framing is consistent with the gap claim.

**Phrase injection (copy-paste ready):**
- Agreement: "Research seems to agree that…" / "There is strong convergent evidence for…"
- Disagreement: "The evidence on X is mixed…" / "There are two conflicting camps on the issue of…"
- Limitation: "While this approach has many benefits, [shared limitation]…"
- Gap: "In contrast, the present work…" / "This paper overcomes many limitations of past studies by…"

**Note on proposals:** NSF/DARPA/AFRL proposals require a Background section that functions identically to an embedded literature review. The same three-purpose structure (situate → credibility → gap) applies. The gap statement in a proposal becomes the "need" that the proposed work fills. Use this handout's structure directly when drafting proposal Background sections with [[CMU-Proposals]] or [[GradLIFE-Proposal-As-Genre]].

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

The CTPC paper's Related Work section must accomplish all three literature review purposes:

1. **Situate CTPC:** Position it within the physics-informed ML, neural ODE, and orbital mechanics ML literature. The trend groupings should be: (a) physics-informed ML (PINNs, HNNs, PHNNs), (b) neural correctors for orbital propagation (GP-based, RNN-based), (c) uncertainty propagation in SDA.

2. **Demonstrate field knowledge:** Show awareness of SGP4 limitations, equinoctial element parameterizations, covariance realism literature, and the SDA-specific gap between propagation accuracy and uncertainty calibration.

3. **Construct the gap:** The shared limitation across all three trend clusters should converge on: prior work either enforces physics hard (but ignores stochastic residuals) or models residuals softly (but violates conservation structure). CTPC is the first approach that does both — hard architectural dissipation constraint plus calibrated probabilistic corrector.

**Specific draft move:** The Related Work closing sentence for the CTPC paper should follow the password corpus pattern exactly:
> "In this paper, we overcome these limitations. CTPC enforces [hard dissipation constraint] while simultaneously [calibrated UQ]. Unlike prior methods, [differentiating claim]."

This follows directly from the CMU gap-construction capstone move demonstrated on p. 4.

## Open Questions

1. The handout recommends trend-grouping but does not address chronological organization within a trend — should trends be presented chronologically or by logical dependency? (Relevant for CTPC where some methods build on each other.)
2. How many trend clusters is optimal for an ML conference paper Related Work section with a 1-page limit? The handout examples use 3–5 clusters; ML papers often compress to 3.
3. The handout treats the gap as a concluding move at the end of the review. Peyton Jones argues the gap should come before the review (contribution first, then related work). Which is appropriate for NeurIPS vs. ICML vs. AAS venues? Flag for Bilal to decide per-venue.
4. The before/after examples use humanities and engineering domains. Are there ML-specific anti-patterns not covered here that Langley or Lipton address?

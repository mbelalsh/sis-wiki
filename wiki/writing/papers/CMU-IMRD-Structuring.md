# CMU IMRD — Structuring Your Paper

## One-Line Intuition
IMRD (Introduction–Methods–Results–Discussion) is the universal skeleton of an empirical research paper: each section has an exact job, and the jobs do not overlap.

## Why This Matters
Most rejected or hard-to-read papers are structurally confused: results language bleeds into the introduction, discussion commentary hides in the results, methods bury the lede. Knowing the *function* of each section — not just its name — lets a writer allocate content deliberately and lets a reader (or reviewer) navigate instantly. The CMU SASC cheat sheet distills this into a one-page decision procedure that applies across every STEM field.

## Core Content

### Abstract — the IMRD in miniature

The abstract mirrors the full paper's structure. CMU prescribes specific proportional budgets:

| Section represented | Space allocation | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | ~25% | Importance of the research |
| Methods | ~25% | What you did |
| **Results** | **~35%** | What you found — **the most important part** |
| Discussion | ~15% | Implications of the research |

Key rule: Results get the largest share. An abstract that front-loads motivation and skimps on findings violates reader expectations and buries the news.

---

### I — Introduction & Importance ("Make a case for your new research")

**Primary job:** Convince readers that the problem is real, that existing research has a gap, and that your work is the timely and necessary solution to that gap.

**Required content and moves:**
1. State the problem you researched and why the research is necessary.
2. Discuss the current state of research in your field — establish the landscape.
3. Expose a "gap" or problem in the field (what is missing, wrong, or unresolved).
4. Explain why your present research is a timely and necessary solution to that gap.

The introduction does NOT report findings. It ends with the reader convinced they must keep reading.

> Cross-reference: [[CMU-Establishing-Novelty-Four-Moves]] develops the gap-move in detail. [[CMU-Literature-Reviews]] covers how to survey the field without losing the thread.

---

### M — Methods ("What did you do?")

**Primary job:** Provide a reproducible account of what was done so the study can be evaluated and replicated.

**Conventions:**
- Written in **past tense** and **passive voice** (standard for methods reporting).
- Uses **lots of headings and subheadings** to make it scannable.
- This is the **least-read section** of an IMRD report — readers who need it will find it; readers who don't will skip it.

Methods contain procedure, not argument. Justification for choices belongs in the introduction or discussion, not here.

---

### R — Results ("What did you find?")

**Primary job:** Report findings and outcomes of the research. This is the section that delivers the news.

**Two-part internal structure:**

| Part | Name | Moves | Placement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Part 1 | **Report** | Moves 1–4 | Always in Results |
| Part 2 | **Comment** | Moves 5–8 | Can go in Results or Discussion |

The reporting function **always stays in Results**. The comment function is flexible — it can migrate to Discussion, but if it stays in Results, it must follow the report.

**Figure and table rules:**
- All tables and figures must be **labeled and numbered separately**.
- Captions go **above tables** and **beneath figures**.

**The 8 Moves of Results (with starter phrases):**

**Report moves:**
1. **Refer to your table or figure and state the main trend**
   - *"Table 3 shows that Spam Filter A correctly filtered more junk emails than Filter B"*
2. **Support this trend with data**
   - *"Filter A correctly filtered…" / "The average difference is…"*
3. *(If needed)* **Note any additional, secondary trends and support them with data**
   - *"In addition…" / "Figure 1 also shows…"*
4. *(If needed)* **Note any exceptions to your main trends or unexpected outcomes**
   - *"However…"*

**Comment moves:**
5. *(If needed)* **Provide an explanation**
   - *"A feasible explanation is…" / "This trend can be explained by…"*
6. *(If needed)* **Compare to other research**
   - *"X is consistent with X's finding…" / "In contrast, Y found…"*
7. *(If needed)* **Evaluate whether the findings support or contradict a hypothesis**
8. **State the bottom line: what does the data mean?**
   - *"These findings overall suggest…" / "These data indicate…"*

Move 8 is not optional — every results section must close with the bottom-line interpretation. Moves 1 and 2 are also non-negotiable; moves 3–7 are conditional on the data.

---

### D — Discussion ("What does it mean?")

**Primary job:** Zoom out from the data and situate the findings in the broader field.

**Five moves of Discussion (in order):**
1. **Summarize the main findings of the study.** This allows readers to skip to the beginning of the discussion and immediately understand the main "news" in the report.
2. **Connect these findings to other research.**
3. **Discuss flaws in the current study.**
4. **Use these flaws as reasons to suggest additional, future research.**
5. *(If needed)* **State the implications of the findings for future policy or practice.**

The discussion opens with a summary (not a repeat) of findings so that the section is self-contained. Flaws are not buried or omitted — they are the bridge to future work. Discussion does NOT introduce new data.

---

### Section Job Summary Table

| Section | Core question | Tense/voice | Reader behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| Abstract | Why? What? How? So what? | Varies | Read first, always |
| Introduction | Why does this matter? | Present (field state) | Must convince reader to continue |
| Methods | What did you do? | Past, passive | Often skipped; must be reproducible |
| Results | What did you find? | Past | Most important; deliver news clearly |
| Discussion | What does it mean? | Present/future | Situates contribution; opens future work |

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Mode:** Paper-Writing-Agent, **drafting** and **section-level review**.

**Concrete moves for the agent:**

1. **During section allocation (pre-draft):** Use the section job table to assign each idea, result, and argument to exactly one section. Flag anything that doesn't fit cleanly — that's a structural smell.

2. **During Introduction drafting:** Check that moves (1) current state → (2) gap → (3) this research as solution are all present and in order. If the intro lacks an explicit gap statement, insert one. Cross-reference [[CMU-Establishing-Novelty-Four-Moves]].

3. **During Results drafting:** Walk moves 1–8 explicitly. Confirm that Move 1 (point to figure/table + state main trend) and Move 8 (bottom line) are present. Flag missing captions or mislabeled figures.

4. **During Discussion drafting:** Confirm the section opens with a summary of findings (Move 1), not new data. Confirm flaws are named explicitly (Move 3) and used to motivate future work (Move 4).

5. **During abstract drafting:** Apply the 25/25/35/15 budget rule. Count sentences; if Results gets fewer than ~35% of the sentences, expand the findings statements and compress the motivation.

6. **During Methods review:** Confirm past tense and passive voice. If the methods section contains justification for choices, flag for relocation to intro or discussion.

7. **During review-response (revise and resubmit):** Use the section job descriptions as a checklist against reviewer complaints about structure. "You don't discuss limitations" → Discussion Move 3 is missing. "Results are hard to follow" → check the 8-move sequence.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Every SiS paper (CTPC, dissipation enforcement, equivariant corrector, AFRL demo) fits the IMRD frame:

- **Introduction:** The gap is orbital prediction error from unmodeled non-conservative forces (drag, SRP); the solution is CTPC's physics-as-architecture ML corrector.
- **Methods:** CTPC architecture description — SGP4 predictor, RTN-frame residual corrector, Cholesky covariance layer, dissipation constraint. Past tense, passive voice, heavy subheadings.
- **Results:** Report moves on benchmark metrics (RMSE vs SGP4 baseline, covariance calibration, propagated uncertainty). Move 8 bottom line: "These results suggest CTPC reduces 24-hour position error by X% while maintaining positive-definite covariance guarantees."
- **Discussion:** Connect to prior orbital ML literature, acknowledge limitations (TLE data quality, drag model assumptions), propose next steps (real-time TLE streaming, conjunction Pc integration).

The 35% abstract rule is especially relevant for CTPC papers: the hard constraint architecture is novel but the *results* (propagation accuracy, UQ fidelity) are what make reviewers accept the paper. Do not over-spend abstract real estate on the physics motivation.

## Open Questions

1. The document places "comment" moves (5–8) in Results but says they "can go in the discussion section" — what determines which choice is appropriate for ML/SDA papers? Does venue style (NeurIPS, ICML, JGCD) have a norm here?
2. The abstract budget is given as a typical heuristic. Are there paper types (theory papers, negative results) where the 35% Results allocation should be reduced in favor of Discussion?
3. Move 7 (evaluate whether findings support or contradict a hypothesis) is marked "if needed." For CTPC, which is largely confirmatory (we expect the ML corrector to improve on SGP4), should Move 7 be present to defend against over-claiming?
4. The Methods section is described as "least-read" — does this mean the CTPC architecture description should live in a separate Architecture section (common in ML papers) rather than a traditional Methods section, to signal that it IS the main contribution and should be read?

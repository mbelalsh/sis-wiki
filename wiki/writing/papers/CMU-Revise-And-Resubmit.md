# CMU Guide — Writing the Revise and Resubmit Letter

## One-Line Intuition
A revise-and-resubmit (R&R) letter is a recognizable genre with five obligatory rhetorical moves — master the moves and the letter almost writes itself.

## Why This Matters
Getting an R&R decision is good news: it means the paper has a real chance. But many authors treat the response letter as an afterthought — a bullet list of "we did X" statements — and miss its rhetorical function. The CMU handout frames the R&R letter as a *genre* with documented conventions: following these moves demonstrably improves acceptance likelihood. For Bilal, every CTPC or SiS paper submitted to IEEE TAC, L-CSS, JGCD, or any ML venue (NeurIPS, ICLR, ICML) will eventually face an R&R or major-revision decision; this genre knowledge is directly load-bearing.

## Core Content

### The Five Rhetorical Moves (Letter-Level)

The handout identifies five obligatory rhetorical moves that should appear in every R&R letter, roughly in this order:

| # | Move | Purpose | Placement |
|---|------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | **Express gratitude** | Acknowledge reviewer time/effort; set a collegial tone | Opening — almost always first |
| 2 | **Signal attention to review comments** | Explicitly state you have read and addressed *all* comments; signals seriousness | Early in the letter, right after gratitude |
| 3 | **Claim positive results** | Assert that the revisions have improved the paper; maintains polite, formal tone | Follows or is blended with Move 2 |
| 4 | **Preview content** | Give the editor a roadmap for how to read the letter before the point-by-point list begins | Before the per-comment responses |
| 5 | **Respond to specific comments** | Point-by-point: restate → evaluate → describe change → justify change | Bulk of the letter |

**Key principle:** the letter is formal and polite throughout, even when reviewer comments are unpleasant. Reviewers critique the work, not the author.

---

### Move 5 in Detail: The Four Sub-Moves per Comment

Each individual response to a reviewer comment has four internal sub-moves:

| Sub-move | Label | What it does |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| 5a | **Repeat comment** | Quote (verbatim or near-verbatim) the reviewer's original comment |
| 5b | **Evaluate comment** | Acknowledge the validity or insight of the comment; or, if disagreeing, state so honestly and politely |
| 5c | **Describe specific revisions** | State exactly what changed — include page, paragraph, and/or line numbers |
| 5d | **Justify specific revisions** | Explain *why* the change is correct; cite literature when relevant, especially when disagreeing |

**Rule on disagreement:** you are allowed to disagree with a reviewer comment. You must do so explicitly and politely, with a reasoned explanation, and back it up with examples from the literature wherever possible. Silent omission of a concern is not acceptable — if you did not address something, say so and explain why.

**Rule on location pointers:** always include page/paragraph/line numbers so the editor can verify changes without hunting through the manuscript.

---

### Structural Conventions

- **Restate verbatim.** Copy each reviewer comment exactly (or block-quote it), then respond. This eliminates ambiguity about which comment you are addressing.
- **One comment, one response block.** Do not merge multiple comments into one response unless you explicitly flag the grouping (see preview example 4 below: "Where appropriate, we have grouped related comments from each referee").
- **Formatting signal for the comment.** Distinguish reviewer text from author response visually: use italics, bold, or block-quote formatting for the restated comment; plain text for your response.

### Sample Phrases (from the handout's phrase bank)

**Move 1 — Express gratitude:**
- *"We thank Reviewer A for the constructive comments on our manuscript."*
- *"We would like to thank Reviewer 1 for the effort and time he/she put in…"*
- *"We would like to express our sincere gratitude to you and the reviewers."*
- *"Thank you for the opportunity to revise our paper once more for the Journal of _______."*

**Move 2 — Signal attention:**
- *"We have made every attempt to fully address these comments in the revised manuscript."*
- *"…we have addressed all of the comments offered by Reviewer 1."*
- *"We have revised our paper along the lines outlined by you and the reviewers."*
- *"We are grateful for your careful and considered comments and have tried to respond to each."*

**Move 3 — Claim positive results:**
- *"…and we believe these revisions have resulted in a significantly improved manuscript."*
- *"We honestly feel that his/her insightful suggestions helped us in improving the manuscript…"*
- *"We agree that focusing on _______ strengthened our manuscript and allowed us to make a greater theoretical and practical contribution…"*
- *"We believe the additional analyses discussed above have helped us to substantially improve our manuscript."*

**Move 4 — Preview content:**
- *"Reviewer A's original comments are listed below (in italics) followed by our response to each comment."*
- *"Below, we outline how we have handled each of Reviewer 1's comments. We reiterate each suggestion in (bold/italics)."*
- *"The following paragraphs summarize our responses to the Associate Editor's Comments (AEC) and the Reviewer's Comments (RC)."*
- *"Where appropriate, we have grouped related comments from each referee."*

**Move 5 — Respond to specific comment:**

Sub-move 5a (Repeat):
- *"Social image theory is used to develop H1a and b. While I agree staff auditors' attempt to gain approval… [writer's response]"*
- *"I must admit that I do not understand the first finding. I think the authors should try to explain it in a better way. [writer's response]"*

Sub-move 5b (Evaluate):
- *"This is a good point that has lead to a rewrite of this section of the paper."*
- *"As suggested, …we agree that our rather lengthy review limited the opportunity to expand in other areas."*
- *"Your comments made us think more deeply about _______."*

Sub-move 5c (Describe specific revisions):
- *"Specifically, we have removed the discussion of ________. We now rely more heavily on ________."*
- *"We elaborate on this finding in our manuscript (please see the 1st full paragraph on p. ##)."*
- *"We now incorporate this discussion of _________ in the revised document."*

Sub-move 5d (Justify specific revisions):
- *"The _______ literature discussing this _________ suggests that _________. For example X (date) shows…"*
- *"…give the fact that ________ (citation; citation), then one would expect…"*

### Tone Rules (Extracted from the Handout)

1. **Formal register throughout.** The letter is a professional document addressed to an editor, not a casual reply.
2. **Polite even when irritated.** Reviewers critique the work, not the author; respond accordingly.
3. **No silent omissions.** Every comment must be addressed — either with a change description or an honest explanation of why you chose not to change that aspect.
4. **Honest disagreement is acceptable.** But it requires: explicit statement of disagreement + polite phrasing + reasoned explanation + literature support when possible.
5. **Positive framing.** Claim that the paper improved — this is not spin, it is a genre expectation that also reflects genuine quality gains.

### Overall Letter Architecture (Summary)

```
[Header: journal name, manuscript ID, date]

Dear Editor [name],

  [MOVE 1: Express gratitude — 1–2 sentences]
  [MOVE 2: Signal attention — 1–2 sentences]
  [MOVE 3: Claim positive results — 1–2 sentences]
  [MOVE 4: Preview content — 1–3 sentences describing letter structure]

  REVIEWER 1 COMMENTS

    Comment 1.1: [verbatim reviewer text, formatted distinctly]
    Response: [5b evaluate] [5c describe change + page/para/line] [5d justify if needed]

    Comment 1.2: [verbatim reviewer text]
    Response: ...

  REVIEWER 2 COMMENTS
    ...

  EDITOR COMMENTS (if provided)
    ...

Sincerely,
[Authors]
```

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Every SiS paper — CTPC submissions to JGCD, IEEE TAC, L-CSS, NeurIPS, ICLR — will face R&R decisions. The genre knowledge here is directly operational: when a reviewer says "the physics constraint is just a soft penalty" (a predictable misconception for CTPC), the 5a→5b→5c→5d structure gives the exact rhetorical scaffold to:
- Restate the concern precisely (5a),
- Acknowledge the reasonable basis for the confusion (5b),
- Point to the specific manuscript location where the Cholesky/PH architectural constraint is explained (5c),
- Justify with citations showing that hard architectural constraints differ categorically from loss-function penalties (5d).

The tone rules are also SiS-relevant: reviewers skeptical of physics-as-architecture approaches may write pointed critiques; the "polite even when irritated" and "honest disagreement with literature support" rules provide the genre cover to push back firmly without appearing defensive.

## Open Questions

1. **ML-venue rebuttal vs. journal R&R:** The CMU handout is written for journal R&R (multi-round, full revision). How do the five moves need to be compressed for a 500-word NeurIPS/ICLR rebuttal where no revised manuscript is submitted? A Layer-2 venue file should address this.
2. **Handling contradictory reviewer feedback:** When Reviewer 1 says "add X" and Reviewer 2 says "remove X", what is the preferred genre move for the letter? The handout does not address this — worth adding from supplementary sources.
3. **AEC (Associate Editor Comments) vs. reviewer comments:** The handout mentions AEC as a separate addressee but does not give explicit guidance on ordering or weighting. Journal-specific norms likely vary.
4. **Track-changes vs. clean manuscript:** The handout does not specify whether to submit a tracked-changes version alongside the clean resubmission — this is venue-dependent and should live in a Layer-2 file.
5. **Response letter length norms:** No guidance given on appropriate letter length. Community norms (typically 2–10 pages for journal R&R) should be documented in a venue-specific addendum.

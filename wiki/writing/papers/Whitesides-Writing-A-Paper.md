# Whitesides' Group: Writing a Paper

George M. Whitesides, *Advanced Materials* 16(15), 2004. DOI: 10.1002/adma.200400767.
Originally a group handout created October 4, 1989.

---

## One-Line Intuition

Write the outline first, before the data is collected — the paper is not a record of finished research but the organizing device that *drives* the research.

---

## Why This Matters

Most researchers treat writing as the last step: collect data, then write. Whitesides argues this is backwards and inefficient. The outline is a hypothesis about what the paper will say; writing it early forces clarity about what experiments actually need to be done and what they mean. Without a paper to write, "interesting and unpublished" equals "non-existent." The paper is the research product, not a byproduct.

---

## Core Content

### 1. What Is a Scientific Paper?

A paper is **an organized description of hypotheses, data, and conclusions, intended to instruct the reader.** Papers serve two functions:

1. **Archival** — storing a completed research program for the record.
2. **Planning** — a structure for *planning research in progress*. This second function is Whitesides' central thesis and is the one most researchers ignore.

The objective of research is to **formulate and test hypotheses, draw conclusions from those tests, and teach those conclusions to others.** The objective is explicitly *not* to "collect data." Data collection without a paper plan is meandering.

A good outline for the paper is a good plan for the research program. You should write and rewrite these plans/outlines throughout the course of the research.

### 2. The Outline-First Method (Section 2 — the heart of the essay)

#### 2.1 Why Outlines

An outline is a written plan of the organization of a paper, *including the data on which it rests*. Think of an outline as a carefully organized and presented set of data, with attendant objectives, hypotheses, and conclusions — not merely an outline of text.

**The efficiency argument:** It is far more efficient to cycle through many versions of an outline *before* writing full text. Writing many versions of full text is slow. Most of the *time* in writing a paper goes into the text; most of the *thought* goes into the organization of data and the analysis. Optimize for the thought stage first.

**The agreement-first principle:** If you and Whitesides cannot agree on the outline (that is, on the data and organization), any text is useless. Once you agree, the supporting text can be assembled fairly easily.

#### 2.2 How to Construct an Outline

Start with a blank piece of paper. In any order, write down all important ideas that occur to you about the paper. Drive this by asking yourself:

- Why did I do this work?
- What does it mean?
- What hypotheses did I mean to test?
- What ones did I actually test?
- What were the results? Did the work yield a new method, compound, measurement?
- What compounds? How were they characterized?
- Sketch possible equations, figures, schemes.

**Get the major ideas down.** Do not worry if, during the research, the data seem to test a different hypothesis than the one you started with — write both down and pick the best combination. Much of good science is opportunistic and revisionist: the objectives of a finished paper are often different from those used to justify starting the work.

When you have written down what you can, take another piece of paper and **organize the jumble of the first one into three major heaps:**

1. **Introduction** — Why did I do the work? What were the central motivations and hypotheses?
2. **Results and Discussion** — What were the results? How were compounds made and characterized? What was measured?
3. **Conclusions** — What does it all mean? What hypotheses were proved or disproved? What did I learn? Why does it make a difference?

Then take each of these sections and organize on a *finer scale*. Concentrate on organizing the **data**: construct figures, tables, and schemes to present the data as clearly and compactly as possible. This can be slow — you may sketch a figure five to ten times in different ways to decide how it is most clear and aesthetically best.

Finally, put everything — outline of sections, tables, sketches of figures, equations — in good order.

#### 2.3 The Outline as Contract and Collaboration Tool

When you are satisfied you have included *all* the data (or know what additional data you intend to collect) and have a plausible organization, give the outline to Whitesides. He will:

- Take the outline, add his opinions, suggest changes, and return it.
- This typically takes **four to five iterations** (often with additional experiments) to agree on an outline.

Once agreed, the data are usually in (or close to) final form: the tables, figures, etc. in the outline will be the tables, figures, etc. in the paper. You can then start writing, with assurance that much of your prose will be used.

**The key to efficient collaboration** is exchanging outlines and proposals as early in a project as possible.

#### 2.4 The "Never Wait" Rule

> *Do not, under any circumstances, wait until the collection of data is "complete" before starting to write an outline.*

No project is ever complete. Starting a plausible paper and outline as soon as you see the basic structure of a project saves enormous effort. Even if significant additional work remains before seriously organizing a paper, the effort of writing an outline will have helped guide the research.

#### 2.5 Summary of the Outline Method (Section 2.4)

Whitesides lists these imperatives explicitly:

1. **Start writing possible outlines for papers early in a project.** Do not wait until "the end." The end may never come.
2. **Organize the outline and the paper around easily assimilated data** — tables, equations, figures, schemes — rather than around text.
3. **Organize in order of importance, not in chronological order.** Neophytes organize papers chronologically (failures → successes, initial experiments → climactic finale). This is completely wrong. Start with the most important results; put secondary results later, if at all. The reader does not care how you arrived at the big results, only what they are.
4. **Shorter papers are easier to read than longer ones.**

### 3. The Detailed Outline Structure (Section 2.3)

What an outline should contain:

#### 1. Title

#### 2. Authors

#### 3. Abstract
Do *not* write an abstract at the outline stage. That can be done when the paper is complete.

#### 4. Introduction
The first paragraph or two should be written out completely. Pay particular attention to the **opening sentence** — it should state concisely the objective of the work and indicate why the objective is important.

The Introduction should have these elements:
- **The objectives** of the work.
- **The justification** for these objectives: Why is the work important?
- **Background**: Who else has done what? How? What have we done previously?
- **Guidance to the reader**: What should the reader watch for in the paper? What are the interesting high points? What strategy did we use?
- **Summary/conclusion**: What should the reader expect as conclusion?

In advanced versions of the outline, also include all sections that will go in the Experimental section (at the level of paragraph subheadings) and indicate what information will go in the microfilm section.

#### 5. Results and Discussion
Results and discussion are usually combined. Organize according to major topics. Use **subheadings in boldface** to make organization clear and to help the reader scan.

Examples of good (specific, information-rich) section headings:
- "Synthesis of Alkane Thiols"
- "Characterization of Monolayers"
- "Absolute Configuration of the Vicinal Diol Unit"
- "Hysteresis Correlates with Roughness of the Surface"
- "Dependence of the Rate Constant on Temperature"
- "The Rate of Self-Exchange Decreases with the Polarity of the Solvent"

These are better than generic headings like "Measurement of Rates." Make headings as specific and information-rich as possible.

Try to cover these major common points:
- Synthesis of starting materials
- Characterization of products
- Methods of characterization
- Methods of measurement
- Results (rate constants, contact angles, etc.)

In the outline, do not write significant text — but get all data in their proper place. Any text should simply indicate what will go in that section. Include:
- Section headings
- Figures (with captions)
- Schemes (with captions and footnotes)
- Equations
- Tables (correctly formatted)

**Core principle on data vs. text:** Think of a paper as a collection of experimental results, summarized as clearly and economically as possible in figures, tables, equations, and schemes. **The text in the paper serves just to explain the data, and is secondary.** The more information that can be compressed into tables, equations, etc., the shorter and more readable the paper will be.

#### 6. Conclusions
In the outline, summarize the conclusions of the paper as a list of short phrases or sentences. Do not repeat what is in the Results section unless special emphasis is needed. The Conclusions section should be just that — not a summary. It should add a **new, higher level of analysis** and indicate explicitly the significance of the work.

#### 7. Experimental
Include, in the correct order to correspond to the order in the Results section, all of the paragraph subheadings of the Experimental section.

### 4. Style Points (Section 3)

Whitesides lists these as concrete, correctable rules:

1. **Do not use nouns as adjectives.**
   - Not: "ATP formation"; "reaction product"
   - But: "formation of ATP"; "product of the reaction"

2. **"This" must always be followed by a noun**, so that its reference is explicit.
   - Not: "This is a fast reaction"; "This leads us to conclude"
   - But: "This reaction is fast"; "This observation leads us to conclude"

3. **Describe experimental results uniformly in the past tense.**
   - Not: "Addition of water *gives* product."
   - But: "Addition of water *gave* product."

4. **Use the active voice whenever possible.**
   - Not: "It was observed that the solution turned red."
   - But: "The solution turned red." or "We observed that the solution turned red."

5. **Complete all comparisons.**
   - Not: "The yield was higher using bromine."
   - But: "The yield was higher using bromine than chlorine."

6. **Type all papers double-spaced** (not single- or one-and-a-half-spaced), two spaces after colons, two spaces after periods at end of sentences. Leave generous margins.

### 5. Style Sources Whitesides Recommends

- **The journals themselves** — look at articles and copy the organization you see.
- **Previous papers from the group** — see exactly how a paper should look.
- **The ACS Handbook for Authors** — especially the section on references, pp. 173–229.
- **Strunk and White, *The Elements of Style*** (Macmillan, 3rd ed.) — for sense of usage.
- Several other books in the group library on scientific writing and on design of graphs and figures.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

### Primary Mode: Structuring / Outlining (before drafting)

This source is most valuable at the **pre-draft stage** — before a single paragraph of prose is written. A paper-writing agent drawing on this source should:

1. **Force outline-first behavior.** When a user says "help me write a paper," the agent's first move is to generate or refine an outline, not prose. Ask: What are the results? What is the central hypothesis? What figures will exist?

2. **Check outline completeness before proceeding.** Before generating any section text, verify the outline has: title, authors placeholder, introduction elements (objective, justification, background, guidance, conclusion-preview), Results subheadings, Conclusions (new analysis, not summary), Experimental subheadings. Flag anything missing.

3. **Data-first organization.** When helping structure Results and Discussion, ask the user to enumerate their figures and tables first. Only after figures/tables are placed should section text be drafted. Remind: text explains data; data does not illustrate text.

4. **Order by importance, not chronology.** If the user proposes a Results structure that follows experimental sequence (early experiments first), flag this as a Whitesides anti-pattern and reorder by impact.

5. **Opening sentence audit.** When drafting the Introduction, produce multiple candidate opening sentences and evaluate each against the Whitesides criterion: Does it state the objective concisely and indicate why the objective matters?

6. **Conclusions check.** When the Conclusions draft is produced, check whether it merely summarizes Results. If so, push for a higher-level synthesis and explicit statement of significance.

### Secondary Mode: Style Correction (during revision)

During editing passes, apply the four style rules mechanically as a checklist:
- Scan for noun-as-adjective constructions.
- Scan for bare "this" references lacking a following noun.
- Scan for present-tense experimental results (should be past tense).
- Scan for passive voice constructions that could be active.
- Scan for incomplete comparisons ("higher than what?").

### Collaboration / Iteration Mode

Model the four-to-five-iteration outline cycle. Do not expect a single-pass outline to be final. Each iteration should produce a cleaner data organization. The milestone for "outline done" is: all figures and tables are placed with captions, all section subheadings are specific and information-rich, and the introduction's five elements are present.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Bilal's papers — on CTPC, dissipative PHNN layers, equivariant correctors — are research-in-progress projects with ongoing experiments and design decisions. The Whitesides method maps directly:

1. **Outline CTPC papers now, not after the experiments are done.** The paper structure clarifies which ablations and benchmarks are actually necessary versus nice-to-have. Writing an outline of the CTPC journal paper immediately surfaces whether the dissipation-enforcement comparison (hard architectural constraint vs. soft Lyapunov penalty) is a Results subsection or a Conclusions insight — that decision shapes what experiments to run.

2. **Figure-first for SDA results.** SDA papers live or die by trajectory plots, covariance ellipsoid comparisons, and conjunction probability curves. Following Whitesides: design those figures (axes, what is compared, what insight each conveys) before writing a word of text. The text will then explain figures that already exist.

3. **Importance ordering, not chronological.** The CTPC research program started with SGP4 residual modeling, then added the probabilistic corrector, then added the dissipation constraint. A paper organized in that chronological order buries the most important contribution (the hard dissipation constraint) at the end. Whitesides says: lead with the most important result.

4. **Conclusions as new analysis.** The CTPC Conclusions should not summarize the experiments — they should articulate the architectural principle (physics-as-architecture beats soft penalties for orbital mechanics) and its implications for the SDA field. That is the higher-level analysis Whitesides demands.

5. **Start the SWM paper outline early.** Even if the Space World Model is two years from completion, writing a speculative outline now clarifies the research roadmap: what data would prove the claims, what benchmarks are needed, what the story is.

## Open Questions

1. Whitesides writes for chemistry papers where Results + Discussion are often combined and figures dominate. How does the advice translate to ML papers where the "data" are experimental curves, ablations, and benchmark tables — and where a related work section is mandatory?

2. Whitesides says start the most important results first. In ML venues, reviewers expect a specific IMRaD structure. Is there tension between importance-ordering and venue conventions?

3. The four-to-five iteration model assumes a senior co-author (Whitesides himself) as the feedback partner. What is the equivalent iteration discipline for a solo PhD student without a hands-on advisor? Can a writing agent substitute for the co-author in outline review?

4. Whitesides recommends against writing the abstract at the outline stage. Most grant proposals (SBIR Phase I/II) require the abstract first. How does the outline-first method adapt for proposal writing? See [[Kraicer-Art-Of-Grantsmanship]] and [[Przeworski-Salomon-Art-Of-Writing-Proposals]].

5. The "data before text" principle is strong for experimental sciences. For theoretical contributions (new loss functions, new architectural priors, convergence proofs), what plays the role of the figures and tables?

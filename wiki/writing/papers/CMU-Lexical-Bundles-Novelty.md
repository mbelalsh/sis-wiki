# CMU Lexical Bundles for Establishing Novelty

## One-Line Intuition
A four-move rhetorical sequence — significance → status quo → gap → fill — is the standard grammar of a research paper introduction, and each move has a small, reusable set of stock phrases that signal to readers exactly which move is in progress.

## Why This Matters
Reviewers and readers pattern-match introductions in milliseconds. If your opening sentences don't activate the expected rhetorical signal ("this matters," "here is what exists," "here is what is missing," "here is what we do"), reviewers perceive the contribution as unclear — not because the science is weak, but because the framing is absent. Lexical bundles are the low-level linguistic tokens that fire those signals. Knowing them by name lets a writer construct each move deliberately rather than by feel.

## Core Content

The handout organizes all phrases around four sequential moves. The moves are always performed in order; skipping or blending them is a common cause of weak introductions.

---

### Move 1 — Explaining the Significance

**Purpose:** Tell the broadest relevant audience why this research area matters. Audience should be wider than the primary target venue; ideally the general public can follow.

| Lexical bundle | Function |
|---|---|
| X project is important because… | Direct importance claim |
| This work investigates modern concerns with… | Positions work as responsive to current pressures |
| X significantly impacts… | Stakes assertion |
| Due to an ever-increasing number of… | Volume/scale as motivating pressure |
| To understand X, it is crucial to… | Necessity framing |
| X is especially useful in… | Utility / application framing |
| X has become an urgent issue in… | Urgency / timeliness framing |

**Exemplar phrases from the handout (verbatim):**
- "In many **modern** radar applications, pulse compression is **a key element** to achieve the required performance." (Example 1, p. 1)
- "There is **growing pressure** to support more and more communications within those spectrum bands…" (Example 2, p. 1)
- "Clock synchronization is an **important** service for wireless sensor networks." (Example 3, p. 1)

---

### Move 2 — Describing the Status Quo

**Purpose:** Survey current practices, literature, or state of affairs within the narrower scope of your field. Establishes the baseline that Move 3 will then critique.

| Lexical bundle | Function |
|---|---|
| Prior research has shown that… | Evidence-based baseline |
| The most-used method for investigating this problem is… | Dominant practice signal |
| Previous studies have evaluated… | Literature survey opener |
| X process has been described as… | Field-consensus description |
| Recent work in X has questioned whether… | Emerging uncertainty within consensus |
| Ongoing studies indicate that… | Active investigation signal |
| New models of X suggest that… | Frontier / recent-development marker |

**Exemplar phrases from the handout (verbatim):**
- "**Many engineers** have noted that if one were able to form truly simultaneous transmit beams in independent directions…" (Example 1, p. 2)
- "**Until recently,** radar transmitters have not enjoyed the same degree of software-driven agility as radar receivers. **Nowadays** radar transmitters incorporate highly agile, software-driven waveform generators." (Example 1, p. 2)
- "**Many people assume** that the only way to meet this need is to find an unused block of spectrum…" (Example 2, p. 2)
- "**Multiple existing MAC protocols** use clock synchronization to save energy… **Currently, synchronization mechanisms** consume a significant amount of energy…" (Example 3, p. 2)

---

### Move 3 — Identifying a Gap

**Purpose:** Show that the status quo is incomplete, unsatisfactory, or inconclusive. The phrase alone signals the gap; the clause after the ellipsis must explain *why* it is a gap — the explanation is non-optional.

**Style caution (verbatim from handout):** "Try to avoid utilizing words like 'neglected,' 'failed,' or 'ignored' when critiquing researchers in your field. Instead, frame the gap in more positive terms." (p. 3)

| Lexical bundle | Function |
|---|---|
| However, the commercial viability of X is currently limited because… | Practical limitation framing |
| Although X practice has been used widely, it inadequately addresses… | Concede-then-critique pattern |
| Whereas research has shown X, it overlooks… | Contrast framing; positive gap |
| While current findings are promising, more research is needed because… | Optimistic gap; extends not replaces |
| X's results have raised new questions that need to be explored, such as… | Forward-opening gap |

**Exemplar phrases from the handout (verbatim):**
- "**However**, the received results demonstrate the opposite. Consequently, the circulating codes for coherent MIMO deserve **careful reconsideration** and analysis." (Example 1, p. 3)
- "This paper **shows some of the limitations of** this white space approach…" (Example 2, p. 3)
- "**However**, existing synchronization mechanisms consume **an exorbitant amount of** energy due to the periodic exchange of messages…" (Example 3, p. 3)

---

### Move 4 — Filling the Gap

**Purpose:** Announce your contribution as the timely, necessary, or innovative solution. This move should appear in the same introduction, often as the final paragraph before the paper outline.

| Lexical bundle | Function |
|---|---|
| This paper describes a new solution for… | Direct contribution announcement |
| We offer a novel approach to… | Novelty + ownership |
| The goal of this paper is to explore a new theory about… | Goal / scope framing |
| Here, we investigate the previously unstudied X… | First-investigation claim |
| Unlike existing approaches to X, our approach emphasizes… | Differentiation / contrast |
| We will suggest ways to address shortcomings in current approaches to X. | Modest / reform framing |

**Exemplar phrases from the handout (verbatim):**
- "**This paper describes a solution** for the proposed waveforms **based on the use of a novel and technically simple solution of** transmitting only one waveform circulating…" (Example 1, p. 4)
- "**The main contributions of this paper are as follows. We present a novel** hardware clock synchronization solution…" (Example 3, p. 4)
- "**Unlike other** hardware-based clock synchronization solutions that require transmitters, **our system is able to** utilize the existing signals…" (Example 3, p. 4)
- "**In contrast to** existing systems like the WWVB atomic clock broadcast and GPS time receiver, **our system** operates extremely well indoors…" (Example 3, p. 4)
- "**By leveraging** this highly available common clock source, **we can now provide** synchronization using significantly less power than existing message passing solutions." (Example 3, p. 4)

### Complete Move-Phrase Table (Quick Reference)

| Move | Signal word / phrase | Rhetorical job |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | important because / key element / growing pressure / urgent issue / ever-increasing | Stake the claim |
| 2 | prior research has shown / many have noted / until recently / nowadays / currently | Establish baseline |
| 3 | however / although…it inadequately / whereas…it overlooks / while…more research is needed | Open the gap |
| 4 | this paper describes / we offer a novel / unlike existing / in contrast to / we present | Fill the gap |

## Key Verbatim Anchors

> "Researchers use four moves to show how their work is important, relevant, and novel: (1) Explain the significance of the research area, (2) Describe the status quo (current state of research) in the field, (3) Identify a 'gap' in that research, and (4) Fill that gap with your project." — p. 1

> "Remember, while the common phrases below indicate *that* the current practices are incomplete, the content that follows the ellipses (…) should explain *why* they are incomplete and thus demonstrate a need for this gap to be filled." — p. 3

> "Try to avoid utilizing words like 'neglected,' 'failed,' or 'ignored' when critiquing researchers in your field. Instead, frame the gap in more positive terms." — p. 3

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Agent:** Paper-Writing-Agent  
**Mode:** Introduction drafting; abstract revision; rebuttal framing

### Concrete moves

1. **Introduction audit.** When Bilal shares a draft introduction, check whether all four moves are present and in order. A common failure mode: Move 1 is present, Move 2 is implicit or absent, and the paper jumps from significance directly to the contribution. Flag the missing move explicitly.

2. **Phrase substitution.** If a draft uses negative framing in the gap move ("prior work failed to…"), substitute from the Move 3 bundle: "Although X has been used widely, it inadequately addresses…" or "Whereas research has shown X, it overlooks…".

3. **Move 4 signal injection.** If the contribution statement lacks a clear ownership signal, add a Move 4 opener: "Unlike existing physics-informed correctors, our approach enforces…" or "Here, we investigate the previously unstudied interaction between…".

4. **Abstract mapping.** A well-formed abstract mirrors the four-move structure in miniature. Use this framework to check that the abstract contains one sentence per move.

5. **Proposal framing.** The four-move pattern transfers directly to grant proposal specific aims pages: significance (broader impact) → status quo (prior work) → gap (limitation) → fill (your approach). Coordinate with [[CMU-Establishing-Novelty-Four-Moves]] for the full strategic treatment.

6. **Tone calibration for gap move.** In SDA / defense-adjacent venues, avoid implying that prior astrodynamics community work was negligent. Frame gaps as natural limits of the prior paradigm, not failures.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Every CTPC paper introduction must execute all four moves cleanly:

- **Move 1** — SDA urgency: proliferation of space objects, collision risk, degraded SSA from TLE-only tracking.
- **Move 2** — Status quo: SGP4 + covariance scaling; CARA Pc computation; physics-informed correctors in adjacent domains.
- **Move 3** — Gap: existing ML correctors treat physics as soft penalties (loss terms), which can be violated at inference time; no architecture enforces Ḣ ≤ 0 as a hard prior.
- **Move 4** — Fill: CTPC with Port-Hamiltonian dissipative block as hard architectural constraint; dissipation is structurally impossible to violate regardless of training signal.

The Move 3 gap language is particularly load-bearing for SiS because the core novelty claim is architectural (hard vs. soft constraint) — not empirical performance alone. Phrases like "Although physics-informed neural networks have been applied to orbital mechanics, they inadequately address the hard enforcement of dissipation at inference time…" are the correct register for that claim.

## Open Questions

1. Do different venues (ICML vs. AIAA vs. IEEE Aero) have systematically different preferences for which move to emphasize? The handout uses electrical engineering examples exclusively — are Move 1 phrases less common in ML venues where everyone already accepts the domain matters?
2. Move 3 gap phrases are intentionally positive/diplomatic. In adversarial review contexts (e.g., a rebuttal), should the framing shift? Is there a harder-edged variant of these bundles that is appropriate for response-to-reviewers?
3. The handout is silent on Move 4 length. Is there a principled rule for how much space the "fill" move should occupy relative to the gap? (Peyton-Jones suggests the contribution list should be the longest part of the introduction — does that match CMU guidance?)

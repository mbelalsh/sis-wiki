# The Elements of Style — Strunk's 18 Rules of Usage and Composition

## One-Line Intuition
Plain, vigorous English reduces to a small, enforceable set of rules — eight of punctuation/grammar and ten of composition — unified by one principle: **every word must tell**, and the reader's effort must be minimized.

## Why This Matters
Strunk wrote this as a Cornell composition textbook to "lighten the task of instructor and student by concentrating attention on a few essentials — the rules of usage and principles of composition most commonly violated." It is deliberately small: a short, prescriptive core that any writer can apply immediately, rather than a comprehensive grammar. For a researcher writing under hard page limits, it is the densest available checklist of sentence- and paragraph-level discipline.

## Edition Note
Although the cover credits "Strunk, W., Jr. and White, E.B.," the text of this PDF is the **original Strunk (1918/1920)**: the 18-rule core, §IV "A Few Matters of Form," and alphabetical reference lists of misused words and misspellings. It does **not** contain E.B. White's later additions (the famous "An Approach to Style" chapter, White's introduction). Cite it as Strunk's original — and see [[Open Questions]] if White's material is needed.

## Core Content

### §II — Elementary Rules of Usage (mechanical: punctuation & grammar)

1. **Form the possessive singular of nouns with `'s`** — *Charles's friend*, *the witch's malice* — whatever the final consonant. Exceptions: ancient proper names in *-es/-is* (*Moses' laws*, *Jesus'*), and *for conscience' sake*. Pronominal possessives (*hers, its, theirs, yours, oneself*) take **no** apostrophe.
2. **In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last** — the serial ("Oxford") comma: *red, white, and blue*. Business-firm names omit the final comma. The abbreviation *etc.* is always preceded by a comma.
3. **Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas** — both commas or neither, never one. **Non-restrictive** relative clauses are set off by commas (*The audience, which had at first been indifferent, became...*); **restrictive** clauses are not (*The candidate who best meets these requirements will obtain the place*). The non-restrictive clause could be split into two independent statements; the restrictive one cannot.
4. **Place a comma before *and* or *but* introducing an independent clause.** Used between independent clauses, *and* is the least specific connective — it asserts a relation without defining it; consider recasting with a subordinating word that names the relation.
5. **Do not join independent clauses by a comma** (the comma-splice rule). Two grammatically complete clauses not joined by a conjunction take a **semicolon** — or become separate sentences. If the second clause is preceded by an adverb (*accordingly, besides, so, then, therefore, thus*) rather than a conjunction, the semicolon is still required.
6. **Do not break sentences in two** — do not use periods for commas (the sentence-fragment error).
7. **A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject** (the dangling-participle rule): *Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman.* The same applies to opening appositives, adjectives, and adjective phrases. Violations are "often ludicrous" (*Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap*).
8. **Divide words at line-ends according to formation and pronunciation.** (Largely obsolete for digital typesetting; retained for completeness.)

### §III — Elementary Principles of Composition (the operationally central rules)

9. **Make the paragraph the unit of composition: one paragraph to each topic.** The object is to aid the reader — "the beginning of each paragraph is a signal that a new step in the development of the subject has been reached."
10. **As a rule, begin each paragraph with a topic sentence; end it in conformity with the beginning.** The most useful paragraph (especially in exposition and argument) is one where (a) the topic sentence comes at or near the beginning, (b) the succeeding sentences explain or develop it, and (c) the final sentence emphasizes the thought or states an important consequence. **"Ending with a digression, or with an unimportant detail, is particularly to be avoided."**
11. **Use the active voice** — usually more direct and vigorous than the passive. Not absolute: the passive is sometimes needed when a particular word must be the subject of the sentence. Avoid making one passive depend on another; avoid using a noun that expresses the whole action as the subject of a passive (*A survey of this region was made in 1900* → *This region was surveyed in 1900*).
12. **Put statements in positive form.** Make definite assertions; avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, non-committal language. Use *not* for denial or antithesis, never for evasion: *not honest → dishonest*; *did not remember → forgot*; *did not pay attention to → ignored*.
13. **Omit needless words.** *"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."* The crucial nuance: this requires **not** that every sentence be short, but that **every word tell**. Common offenders: *the question as to whether → whether*; *the fact that* (revise out of every sentence); *who is / which was* (often deletable). His Macbeth example cuts 55 words to 26.
14. **Avoid a succession of loose sentences** — especially the type built from two coordinate clauses joined by a conjunction or relative. Individually unexceptionable; in series they become monotonous. Recast for variety of sentence shape.
15. **Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form** — parallel construction. Likeness of form lets the reader recognize likeness of content and function. An article or preposition applying to all members of a series is used once before the first term or repeated before each. Correlative expressions (*both...and; not only...but also; either...or; first, second, third*) must be followed by the same grammatical construction.
16. **Keep related words together.** Word position is the principal means of showing relationship. Do not separate subject and principal verb by a transferable phrase. Put the relative pronoun immediately after its antecedent. Put modifiers next to what they modify: *He only found two mistakes* → *He found only two mistakes*.
17. **In summaries, keep to one tense.** Summarize the action of a drama in the present tense; hold one tense throughout; express antecedent action with the perfect. A past tense in indirect discourse remains unchanged.
18. **Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.** The end is the position of prominence; it carries the **logical predicate — the new element**. The periodic sentence exploits this. The key generalization: *"The principle that the proper place for what is to be made most prominent is the end applies equally to the words of a sentence, to the sentences of a paragraph, and to the paragraphs of a composition."* — the emphasis principle is **fractal**.

### §IV — A Few Matters of Form
Headings (leave space after the title); Numerals (write dates/serial numbers in figures); Parentheses (punctuate the surrounding sentence as if the parenthetical were absent); Quotations (formal quotations cited as evidence are introduced by a colon); References (abbreviate frequently-cited titles; give precise locations — theorem number, page); Titles of works (italics with capitalized initials).

### §V & §VI — Reference Lists
**§V Words and Expressions Commonly Misused** and **§VI Words Often Misspelled** are alphabetical lookup lists. §V's governing principle: most entries are "not so much bad English as bad style," and the proper correction is "the replacement of vague generality by definite statement" (e.g., *case*, *character*, *due to*, *effect* — drop or sharpen). These lists are consulted on demand, not memorized.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This
Belongs to the **Layer 0 generic-writing agent**, and is shared by the paper- and proposal-writing agents.
- **REVISE mode (primary):** Rules 1–8 are a copy-edit mechanics checklist; Rules 11–16, 18 are the line- and sentence-revision checklist. Rule 13 is the master concision pass — run it against every sentence when a draft is over a page limit.
- **DRAFT mode:** Rules 9–10 govern paragraph planning — one topic per paragraph, topic sentence first, no digression at the close.
- Rule 18 (end-emphasis, and its fractal scaling) is the **seed** of the cohesion/emphasis theory formalized in [[Williams-Bizup-Style]] and [[Gopen-Swan-Science-Of-Scientific-Writing]] — treat those three as one lineage, not three independent sources.

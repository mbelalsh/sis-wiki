# Guide to Writing Mathematics — University of Hong Kong (K. H. Law, S. Boynton, C. Tait)

**Author identification note:** The PDF title page reads "Guide to Writing Mathematics — Version: June 2015." The preface identifies it as developed under the Teaching Development Grant (TDG) project "Promoting Teaching and Learning of Professional Writing in Mathematics" at **The University of Hong Kong (HKU)**. Principal Investigator: **K. H. Law** (Department of Mathematics); Co-investigators: **S. Boynton** and **C. Tait** (Centre for Applied English Studies). First version released January 2015. The filename `HKU-Guide-Writing-Mathematics` is accurate.

---

## One-Line Intuition

Mathematical writing is mostly words — equations and symbols are supplements, not the primary medium — and every sentence must be grammatically complete, logically connected, and use each term and symbol with its precise, conventional meaning.

---

## Why This Matters

University mathematics teachers expect prose that treats equations as embedded clauses within proper sentences, uses English grammar correctly (articles, subject-verb agreement, verb forms), selects each mathematical term with awareness of its exact meaning, and deploys logical symbols only when they belong inside a complete statement. Secondary-school students arrive having been trained to write disconnected lines of equations. This guide is the bridge. For a writing agent, it is a comprehensive checklist of the errors most likely to appear in a math-heavy paper and the one-step fixes for each.

## Core Content

### 2. Basic Principles

#### 2.1 Rule: Use Complete Sentences

Mathematical writing should contain **mostly words**, supplemented by equations and symbols — not the reverse. Every equation and symbol must form part of a complete sentence when read aloud.

**Examples of complete sentences containing equations:**

| Sentence with symbols | Read as complete sentence |
|---|---|
| Hence x + 1 > 3. | Hence x plus 1 is greater than 3. |
| Since x > 2, it follows that x² + 3x > 2² + 3(2) = 10. | Since x is greater than 2, it follows that x squared plus 3x is greater than … which is equal to 10. |
| Thus n ≠ 0 ∀n ∈ A. | Thus n is not equal to 0 for all n that belongs to A. |

**Fragments that must NOT appear in mathematical writing:**

- "Since x is positive." (dependent clause, no conclusion)
- "If this is not true." (conditional with no consequence)
- "When two triangles are similar." (temporal clause with no result)

#### 2.2 Rule: Never Start a Sentence with a Lowercase Variable or Symbol

When the first word would be a variable or lowercase symbol, rewrite so the sentence begins with a descriptive noun phrase.

| Wrong | Correct | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| p is not a prime number if it is divisible by 3 and greater than 3. | If the number p is divisible by 3 and greater than 3, then it is not a prime number. | Add "the number" or invert the clause |
| x² + x + 1 = 0 has no real root since its discriminant is negative. | The equation x² + x + 1 = 0 has no real root since its discriminant is negative. | Prepend "The equation" |

Strong convention (endorsed by some authorities): **never start a sentence with a symbol or variable at all**, even if it is capitalised.

#### 2.3 Rule: Commas between Variables Can Be Ambiguous

When a clause ends with a lowercase variable and the next clause opens with a lowercase variable, the reader may parse the two variables as a comma-separated list.

| Problematic | Fixed | Why |
|---|---|---|
| In addition to p, q is also a prime number. | In addition to p, the number q is also prime. | "In addition to p, q" reads as a list of two objects |
| Since x > 1, x − 1 > 0 and so √(x−1) is well-defined. | (rewrite to avoid ending one clause and starting next with adjacent variables) | |
| As a quadratic equation in x, x²y + xy² + xy + 1 = 0 has discriminant … | (same issue — restructure) | |

### 3. The Use of English

#### 3.1 Grammatical Errors

##### 3.1.1 Articles (a / an / the / zero article)

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Construct **a** m×n table. | Construct **an** m×n table. | 'm' and 'n' are pronounced with vowel sound /e/; use 'an' |
| By **binomial theorem**, we have… | By **the** binomial theorem, we have… | Specific, unique theorem → definite article 'the' |
| By **the** Pythagoras' Theorem, ABC is right-angled. | By **Pythagoras' Theorem**, ABC is right-angled. | Named-person theorems → zero article |
| By (a), **the** g is continuous. | By (a), **g** is continuous. | Single letter used as a name → zero article |
| By (a), **the** g is continuous. | By (a), **the function** g is continuous. | When using the noun "function" → definite article |
| Let x be **non-negative number**. | Let x be **a non-negative number**. | Count noun introduced for first time → indefinite 'a' |
| Let x be **the non-negative number**. | Let x be **non-negative**. | As adjective, use zero article |
| Let x be **a positive**. | Let x be **positive**. | Use adjective form, not noun form |

**Key principle:** 'the' = specific and known to reader; 'a/an' = any one of a class; zero article = used with adjectives or named references.

##### 3.1.2 Singular vs. Plural

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Every square **are** rectangles. | Every square **is** a rectangle. | "Every" + singular noun + singular verb |
| **All square** are rectangles. | **All squares** are rectangles. | "All" + plural noun + plural verb |
| Positive **integers** k, i.e. k(k+1) is divisible by 2. | Positive **integer** k, … | Only one integer k is in scope |
| Let A be a square **matrice**. | Let A be a square **matrix**. | 'Matrices' is plural of 'matrix' |
| Let A be the **vertice** of the pyramid. | Let A be the **vertex** of the pyramid. | 'Vertices' is plural of 'vertex' |
| (0,0) is the only local **maxima**. | (0,0) is the only local **maximum**. | 'Maxima' is plural of 'maximum' |

##### 3.1.3 Verb Forms

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| There **exists** real numbers m and n such that m > n. | There **exist** real numbers m and n… | Subject "real numbers" is plural → plural verb |
| There **exist** a real number m such that m > 1. | There **exists** a real number m… | Subject "a real number" is singular |
| There **exist** no integer k such that 3k = 2. | There **exists** no integer k… | "Integer" is singular |
| **Replace** a by −a, we have f(−a) = a² = f(a). | **Replacing** a by −a, we have… | '-ing' form creates a dependent clause; bare imperative cannot open a sentence with following clause |
| Hence we get x < 0, **contradicts to** (1). | Hence we get x < 0, **which contradicts** (1). | 'Contradict' takes no preposition |
| Hence we get x < 0, contradicts to (1). | …**which is a contradiction to** (1). | 'Contradiction' (noun) does take preposition 'to' |
| Thus **contradiction**. | Thus **a contradiction occurs**. / Thus **there is a contradiction**. | A verb is required in every sentence |
| The **followings** are equivalent. | The **following statements** are equivalent. | 'Following' is an adjective, not a noun; no plural |
| The possible values of x are **as follow**: | …are **as follows**: | Fixed idiom: always "as follows" |
| The result **is followed**. | The result **follows**. | Active voice: the result comes next |

##### 3.1.4 Verb-to-be and Verb-to-do

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **It is** a rational number between 1 and 2. | **There is** a rational number between 1 and 2. | 'It is' refers to previously mentioned thing; 'there is' introduces new information |
| **There has** a rational number between 1 and 2. | **There is** a rational number between 1 and 2. | 'There has' is ungrammatical; use 'there is/are' |
| A real number x whose square is negative **is not exist**. | …**does not exist**. | Use auxiliary 'do' to form the negative of most verbs |
| This sequence **is not converge**. | This sequence **does not converge**. | Same rule |
| This sequence **is not converge**. | This sequence **is not convergent**. | Alternative: use verb-to-be + adjective form |

##### 3.1.5 Converting Between Word Forms

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Besides from** completing the square, … | **Besides** completing the square, … | 'Besides' used as preposition is followed by -ing form; not 'besides from' |
| **Beside** completing the square, … | **Besides** completing the square, … | 'Beside' = next to (spatial); 'besides' = in addition to |
| The total **increasement** is 6 cm². | The total **increase** is 6 cm². | 'Increasement' does not exist |
| The surface area **increases** 6 cm². | The surface area **increases by** 6 cm². | 'Increase' requires preposition 'by' before amount |
| The **maximal** possible value of f(x) is 3. | The **maximum/largest** possible value of f(x) is 3. | 'Maximal' has a specific different meaning in mathematics |
| The **slanted** height of the cone is 10 cm. | The **slant** height of the cone is 10 cm. | 'Slant height' is a compound noun |
| The function y = x² **concaves** upward. | The function y = x² **is concave** upward. | 'Concave' is an adjective, not a verb |
| **For every** rational number x, **can be** written as x = p/q. | **Every** rational number x **can be** written as x = p/q. | 'For every rational number x' is the subject of the clause; do not split with an extra 'for' |
| One plus one **equals to** two. | One plus one **equals** two. | Verb 'equals' takes no preposition |
| One plus one **equals to** two. | One plus one **is equal to** two. | Adjective form 'equal' does take 'to' |

#### 3.2 Lexical Errors

##### 3.2.1 Word Order

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Let x **is** non-negative. | Let x **be** non-negative. | 'Let' takes bare infinitive |
| Let **a prime number be x**. | Let **x be a prime number**. | Convention: 'let + variable + be + value/description' |
| Let **the width of the rectangle be y**. | Let **y be the width of the rectangle**. | Variable comes first |
| Since the divisor is of degree 2, **1 is the maximum degree of the remainder**. | …**the maximum degree of the remainder is 1**. | Description of the value goes first; the value/figure goes last |
| The smallest value of x² **possible** is 0. | The smallest **possible** value of x² is 0. | Word order: smallest/largest + possible + value |
| Let x be **a real positive number**. | Let x be **a positive real number**. | Order: positive/negative + real + number |
| If f is a **strictly continuous increasing** function, … | If f is a **strictly increasing continuous** function, … | Keep 'strictly' and 'increasing' together |
| **We will show that** 3 + N is odd for all even N. | **We will show that** 3 + N is odd for every even number N. | Phrase 'we will show that' begins the sentence (not buried mid-sentence) |

##### 3.2.2 Choice of Words

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| A prime is a number with no factors other than 1 and itself. **For instance**, if k = pq… | …(**for instance**, 2 is a prime). **In other words**, if k = pq… | 'For instance' = give an example; 'in other words' = restate/clarify |
| Therefore x is rational, **e.g.** x = p/q for some integers p, q. | Therefore x is rational, **i.e.** x = p/q … | 'e.g.' = for example (illustrates); 'i.e.' = that is (gives the actual definition/equivalent) |
| Let ⌈x⌉ be the least integer ≥ x, **i.e.** ⌈π⌉ = 4. | …**e.g.** ⌈π⌉ = 4. | ⌈π⌉ = 4 is an example, not the definition |

##### 3.2.3 Other Lexical Errors

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Assume **the contrary** that not all of them are zeros. | Assume **on the contrary** that not all are zeros. | 'On the contrary' is an adverb phrase modifying 'assume'; 'the contrary' is the object of 'assume' and needs an explanatory clause |
| Let m and n be **odd and even**. | Let m and n be **odd and even respectively**. | Without 'respectively', it is ambiguous which is odd and which is even |
| Let m and n be odd and even. | Let **m be odd and n be even**. | Explicit assignment is always clearer |
| This equation has **finite** solutions. | This equation has **finitely many** solutions. | 'Finite' describes the number of solutions, not the solutions themselves |
| This equation has **infinite** solutions. | This equation has **infinitely many** solutions. | Same rule for 'infinite' |

#### 3.3 Other Issues

##### 3.3.1 Words with Similar Pronunciations (Homophones and Near-Homophones)

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| The number x **can not** be rational. | The number x **cannot** be rational. | 'Cannot' = does not have the ability; 'can not' = has the ability but may choose not to |
| Without **lost** of generality, we have… | Without **loss** of generality, we have… | Fixed phrase: "loss of generality" (noun) |
| This **proofs** that x > 0. | This **proves** that x > 0. | 'Proof' is a noun; 'prove' is the verb |
| This completes the **prove**. | This completes the **proof**. | 'Proof' is the noun form |

##### 3.3.2 Frequently Confused Words

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Rotate △ABC **clockwisely** by 90°. | Rotate △ABC **clockwise** by 90°. | 'Clockwise' is already an adverb |
| The numbers are **pairwisely** different. | The numbers are **pairwise** different. | 'Pairwise' is already an adverb |
| Here is a **counter example**. | Here is a **counterexample**. | 'Counter' is a prefix, not a separate word |
| Let x be a **non zero** number. | Let x be a **nonzero** number. | 'Non' is a prefix, not a separate word |
| From this we **deduct** that the equation has no solution. | From this we **deduce** that… | 'Deduct' = subtract; 'deduce' = draw a logical conclusion |

##### 3.3.3 Linking Verbs and Punctuation (Connectives)

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| If x > 0. **Then** 2x > 0. | If x > 0, **then** 2x > 0. | 'If' clause is dependent; must be completed in the same sentence, not split by a period |
| When x > 0. **Then** 2x > 0. | When x > 0, 2x > 0. | Same as 'if': 'when' clause cannot stand alone |
| Since x is non-negative. **We** have x + 1 > 0. | Since x is non-negative, **we** have x + 1 > 0. | 'Since' joins dependent to independent clause; cannot split into two sentences |
| Let x be non-negative, **then** x + 1 > 0. | Let x be non-negative. **Then** x + 1 > 0. | 'Let' and 'then' for proof steps → two separate sentences |
| Suppose x is non-negative, **then** have x + 1 > 0. | Suppose x is non-negative. **Then** x + 1 > 0. | Same: 'suppose' and 'then' → two sentences |

**Rule:** In simple sentences, there should be **only one verb**. When more than one verb is needed, use a conjunction ('and') or split into separate sentences.

##### 3.3.4 Spelling

Common misspellings in math writing:
- 'true' misspelled as **ture**
- 'false' misspelled as **flase**
- 'principal' (= head of a school) vs. **principle** (= general law, primary truth) — use 'principle of mathematical induction'

### 4. The Use of Symbols

#### 4.1 The Symbol '=' and '≠'

##### 4.1.1 Are They Equal? — Only Write '=' When Both Sides Are Genuinely Equal

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Derivative of x²: lim[h→0] (f(x+h)−f(x))/h = (x²+2xh+h²−x²)/h = 2x | Same but with lim[h→0] explicitly present on each intermediate line | Cannot drop the 'lim[h→0]' operator; without it, (f(x+h)−f(x))/h is not equal to 2x |
| d³/dx³(xe^x) = xe^x + e^x = xe^x + 2e^x = xe^x + 3e^x | d³/dx³(xe^x) = d²/dx²(xe^x + e^x) = d/dx(xe^x + 2e^x) = xe^x + 3e^x | Each step must show the operator being applied, not just the next expression |
| (y−5)/(x−2) = 4 = y = 4x − 3 | (y−5)/(x−2) = 4, **which is the same as** y = 4x − 3 | The middle equality "4 = y" is false; use connecting words instead |
| Row reduce: [1 0; 2 4] **=** [1 0; 0 1] | Row reduce: [1 0; 2 4] **→** [1 0; 0 1] | Row-equivalent matrices are not equal; use arrow, annotated with operation e.g. −2R₁+R₂ |

##### 4.1.2 Non-Transitivity of ≠

'≠' is NOT transitive. Writing a ≠ b ≠ c does not mean a ≠ c (e.g. 1 ≠ 2 ≠ 1 would imply 1 ≠ 1, which is false).

| Wrong | Correct |
|---|---|
| Since **a ≠ b ≠ c**, we have… | Since **a ≠ b, b ≠ c, and a ≠ c**, we have… |
| Since a ≠ b ≠ c, we have… | Since **a, b, c are pairwise distinct**, we have… |

##### 4.1.3 Proper Order in Equality Chains

When writing a chain of equalities, order the steps so each step is easy to justify from the previous one.

| Wrong | Correct | Why |
|---|---|---|
| x⁴y⁴/8 = 2 ⟹ **(xy)⁴ = x⁴y⁴ = 8×2 = 16** | x⁴y⁴/8 = 2 ⟹ **(xy)⁴ = x⁴y⁴ = 8×2 = 16** (reordered) | In wrong version, step (xy)⁴ = 8×2 is not obvious; switch x⁴y⁴ = (xy)⁴ to come first |

**Principle:** When studying a chain of equalities, the reader tries to see why each equality holds. Order steps so each follows naturally from the previous.

#### 4.2 The Symbols '⇒' and '⟺'

'⇒' means 'implies'; '⟺' means 'if and only if'. Both relate **statements** to **statements**.

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| If **x = 1 ⇒ x + 1 = 2**. | If x = 1, **then** x + 1 = 2. OR: **x = 1 ⇒ x + 1 = 2.** | 'If x = 1' is an incomplete sentence; '⇒' must connect two statements, not appear inside an 'if…' clause |
| Hence we have x + 1 = 5 **⇒** x = 4. | Hence we have x + 1 = 5, **which implies** x = 4. | 'Hence we have x + 1 = 5' is not a statement (it is a verb phrase); '⇒' cannot be placed at its end |
| (2,3) must be a maximum. **⇒ x = 2.** | (2,3) must be a maximum **and therefore** x = 2. | '⇒ x = 2' is an incomplete sentence; '⇒' must be preceded by a statement |

**Test:** Read the '⇒' sentence aloud replacing '⇒' with 'implies'. If the result is grammatically and logically correct, the symbol is used correctly.

#### 4.3 The Symbols '∀' and '∃'

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| 2\|a + 1 **∃** a ∈ A (meaning: for all) | 2\|a + 1 **∀** a ∈ A | '∃' = 'there exists', '∀' = 'for all'; do not confuse them |
| f is continuous, then **∀** c ∈ [0,1] s.t. f(c) = 0 (meaning: there exists) | …then **∃** c ∈ [0,1] s.t. f(c) = 0 | Same confusion |
| f(c) = 0 **∃** c ∈ [0,1] | **∃ c ∈ [0,1] s.t.** f(c) = 0 OR: f(c) = 0 **for some** c ∈ [0,1] | '∃' means 'there exists', not 'for some'; write out 'for some' in words if needed |
| **∃ x ∈ ℝ ∀ x > 1000** | **∃ x ∈ ℝ s.t. x > 1000** | '∀' means 'for all', not 'such that' |
| We have x ≤ 1 **for ∀** x ∈ A. | We have x ≤ 1 **∀** x ∈ A. | 'For ∀' would read 'for for all', which is wrong |
| **∀ x ∈ ℝ s.t. x² ≥ 0** | x² ≥ 0 **∀ x ∈ ℝ** | '∀ x ∈ ℝ s.t.' is not a complete sentence; 'such that' after ∀ introduces a restricting condition, not a conclusion |

#### 4.4 The Symbols '∈' and '⊆'

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| (0,1) **∈** ℝ | (0,1) **⊆** ℝ | An interval is a **subset** of ℝ, not a member |
| If x = 5, then x **⊆** ℝ. | If x = 5, then x **∈** ℝ. | A real number is a **member** of ℝ, not a subset |

#### 4.5 Overusing Symbols

In professional mathematical writing, **symbols are kept to a minimum** except when discussing formal logic. Over-symbolised writing is harder to read.

| Over-symbolised (valid but poor) | Better (words) |
|---|---|
| ∀ε > 0 ∃δ > 0 s.t. \|x−a\| < δ ⇒ \|f(x)−f(a)\| < ε | For all ε > 0, there exists δ > 0 such that \|f(x)−f(a)\| < ε whenever \|x−a\| < δ. |

**Rule:** Use symbols when they are strictly necessary for precision or when the proof structure would be obscured by words. Otherwise, write in English.

### 5. The Use of Terminology

#### 5.1 Elementary Algebra

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| p(x) = ax + b where a and b are **constant** (adj.) | …where a and b are **constants** (noun). | As noun, 'constant' has plural form 'constants' |
| p(x) and q(x) are **constants** polynomials. | …are **constant** polynomials. | As adjective, 'constant' is invariable (no plural) |
| Since 6 = 2×3, so 6 **divides** 3. | …so 6 **is divisible by** 3. / …so **3 divides** 6. | If a divides b (a, b positive integers), then a ≤ b; 6 cannot divide 3 |
| We can **move** the graph of y = eˣ to the left. | We can **translate** the graph of y = eˣ to the left. | Moving a graph by a fixed vector uses the term 'translate' |
| The **multiplication** of two positive numbers is positive. | The **product** of two positive numbers is positive. | The result of multiplication is a 'product' |
| Count integers between 1 and 100 that are not **prime numbers**. | Count integers between 1 and 100 that are not **prime**. | Convention: use the adjective alone; 'prime numbers' is redundant when noun is already 'integers' |
| The **thousand** digit of 12345 is 2. | The **thousands** digit of 12345 is 2. | Standard terminology: units digit, tens digit, hundreds digit, thousands digit |
| The **tenth** digit of 12.34 is 1. | The **tens** digit of 12.34 is 1. | Decimal place names: tenths, hundredths (refer to the position after the decimal); 'tens digit' refers to position before decimal |
| N is divisible by 7 **when** N² is divisible by 7. | N is divisible by 7 **if** N² is divisible by 7. | In mathematical proofs, use 'if' for conditions; 'when' is acceptable when a variable takes on a value |

#### 5.2 Geometry

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Denote the **centre of the circumcentre** of ABC by O. | Denote the **circumcentre** of ABC by O. | 'Circumcentre' already means the centre of the circumscribed circle |
| The quadrilateral ABCD is **concyclic**. | The quadrilateral ABCD is **cyclic**. | 'Cyclic' describes polygons; 'concyclic' describes points |
| The points A, B, C, D are **cyclic**. | The points A, B, C, D are **concyclic**. | Converse: points are concyclic, polygons are cyclic |
| Let ABCD **form** a square. | Let ABCD **be** a square. | Mathematical convention for introducing an object |
| The length of the **perimeter** of the circle is 4π cm. | The length of the **circumference** of the circle is 4π cm. | 'Circumference' = outer boundary of a circle; 'perimeter' = length of the boundary (for polygons) |

#### 5.3 Mathematical Induction

Full annotated example — approximately 10 errors in a typical student proof:

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Let S(n) be the statement 1+2+…+n = n(n+1)/2 **for all positive integers n**. | Let S(n) be the statement 1+2+…+n = n(n+1)/2. | S(n) depends on n; "for all n" is what we want to prove, not part of the definition of S(n) |
| **1 + 2 + … + 1** | **1** | When n = 1, there is only one term; do not write "+2" |
| 1+2+…+1 = 1(1+1)/2; **1 = 1** (simplifying both sides simultaneously) | **LHS = 1; RHS = 1(1+1)/2 = 1** | Proving an equality: start from one side and reach the other, or compute each side separately |
| Assume S(k) is true for **all** positive integers k. | Assume S(k) is true for **some** positive integer k. | Assuming for all k is what we are trying to prove; induction assumes for some k |
| **s.t.** 1+2+…+k = k(k+1)/2 | **i.e.** 1+2+…+k = k(k+1)/2 | Here we explain what S(k) being true means, not impose a condition; use 'i.e.' |
| When **S(k+1)** | When **n = k+1** | S(k+1) is a statement (true/false); 'when' needs a value, not a statement |
| **S(k+1) = 1+2+…+(k+1)** | **LHS = 1+2+…+(k+1)** | S(k+1) is a statement, not a number; cannot be equal to a sum |
| Hence **n = k+1** is also true. | Hence **S(k+1)** is also true. | n = k+1 is not a statement that can be true or false |
| By the **principal** of mathematical induction | By the **principle** of mathematical induction | 'Principal' = head of a school; 'principle' = general law |

#### 5.4 Functions and Calculus

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| −1 is not **the domain** of √x. | −1 is not **in the domain** of √x. OR: −1 is not **an element of the domain** of √x. | 'Domain' is a set; −1 is not equal to the set, it is a member (or non-member) of it |
| The **differentiation** of x² is 2x. | The **derivative** of x² is 2x. | 'Differentiation' is the process; 'derivative' is the result |
| To find intersection, **suppose** x + 1 = 2x + 3. | To find intersection, **set** x + 1 = 2x + 3. | 'Suppose' = assume; here we assign (set equal) to find x |
| f is strictly increasing **in** the interval [0,1]. | f is strictly increasing **on** the interval [0,1]. | Mathematical convention: 'on an interval' not 'in an interval' |
| When **f(0)**, we have sin 0 = 0. | When **x = 0**, we have f(0) = sin 0 = 0. | 'When' needs a condition; f(0) is a value, not a condition |
| Note that ln(sin 0) **has no solution**. | Note that ln(sin 0) is **undefined**. | A value can be undefined; only an equation (with a variable) can have no solution |
| The (k+1)-th derivative: **d^k d / dx^k x** (sin x) = … | d^k/dx^k (d/dx (sin x)) = … | The notation must correctly express the composition of derivative operators |

#### 5.5 Linear Algebra

| Wrong | Correct | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| The **second** entry of A is 2. | The **(1,2)-entry** of A is 2. | 'Second entry' is ambiguous (row 1 col 2? row 2 col 1?); use (i,j)-entry notation |
| The matrix **A × B** is 3×5. | The matrix **AB** is 3×5. | Matrix multiplication is denoted by juxtaposition, not '×' |
| The **matrix** A is positive. | The **entries of the matrix** A are positive. | 'Positive' describes real numbers, not matrices |
| **For the (2,3)-entry of A =** 2+3 = 5. | **The (2,3)-entry of A =** 2+3 = 5. / For the (2,3)-entry of A, **it is equal to** 2+3 = 5. | A phrase ("For the (2,3)-entry of A") cannot equal a number |
| When E is **a Type III**, … | When E is **of Type III**, … / When E is **a Type III elementary matrix**, … | 'Type III' is an adjective; must be followed by a noun or preceded by 'of' |
| If **A is rank** 3, then it must be invertible. | If **A has rank** 3 / If **A is of rank** 3 / If **rank A = 3** | 'A is rank 3' is not a grammatically complete sentence |
| We **solve** the characteristic **polynomial** of A. | We **find** the characteristic polynomial. / We solve the characteristic **equation** of A. | We can solve an equation but not a polynomial |
| The **matrix** A is linearly independent. | The **rows** of the matrix A are linearly independent. | Linear independence applies to elements/vectors, not to a matrix (a single object) |
| Hence **x, y** is a basis. | Hence **{x, y}** is a basis. | A basis is a set; must use set notation |
| Since A has rank 2, **Null A = 3**. | Since A has rank 2, **dim(Null A) = 3**. | Null A is a subspace; it cannot equal a number; its dimension does |
| Since A has rank 2, the **rank** of its null space is 3. | …the **dimension** of its null space is 3. | Null space is not a matrix; it has no rank, only a dimension (nullity) |
| Its **number of rank** is 3. | Its **rank** is 3. | 'Rank' is already a number |

#### 6.2 Presentation

- Write in clear linear order; **avoid two-column layout** on the same page as it hinders reading.
- When crossing out material, cross out only what you do not want; removing one extra word can change the meaning entirely.
- Use **indentation** when splitting into cases:

```
... There are two cases:

    Case 1: ...
    ...

    Case 2: ...
    ...

Combining the two cases, ...
```

This is more readable than running all cases together in a single paragraph.

- Carefully distinguish typographically similar expressions: 1/(2x) vs (1/2)x; use parentheses.

#### 6.3 Avoiding Isolated Equations — Write Complete Paragraphs

The guide opens with "use complete sentences" and closes with "use complete paragraphs."

**The problem with isolated equations:**

```
5x + 1 = 16
5x = 15
x = 3
```

These may be individually complete sentences, but strung together they are ambiguous: Is the first equation an assumption? A given fact? What is the relationship between the lines?

**Fix — explicitly state logical relationships:**

Option 1: "According to the question, we have 5x + 1 = 16. Since 5x + 1 = 16 ⟺ 5x = 15 ⟺ x = 3, we conclude that the value of x is 3."

Option 2 (stronger): Use '⟹' to explicitly show implication direction:

```
√(x−2) = x − 4 ⟹ x − 2 = x² − 8x + 16
               ⟹ x² − 9x + 18 = 0
               ⟹ (x−3)(x−6) = 0
```

Then: "Hence the only possible values of x are 3 and 6. When x = 3, the LHS is 1 and the RHS is −1; when x = 6, both sides equal 2. Thus x = 6 is the only solution."

**Why this matters:** The wrong approach (bare = signs throughout) obscures the extraneous root x = 3. The correct approach with ⟹ makes clear these are one-way implications, which is why checking both values is necessary.

**Final principle (p. 28):** "Pay special attention to how words and symbols are integrated to form complete sentences, and how complete sentences are linked together by connectives to form coherent paragraphs that present rigorous mathematical arguments."

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Primary agent: Layer 0 Generic Writing Agent** — this is a grammar/notation reference. Every math paper passes through these checks.

**Secondary agent: Layer 1a Paper-Writing Agent** — before finalising equations and derivations in any section, run the symbol and equality checklists.

### Concrete Math-Grammar Checks (in order of ingest)

1. **Complete sentence check:** Read every sentence containing a symbol aloud. Does it form a grammatically complete English sentence? Fragment → flag and rewrite.

2. **Sentence opening check:** Does any sentence open with a lowercase variable or symbol? If so, prepend a noun phrase ("The function", "The matrix", "The equation", etc.).

3. **Article audit:** For each mathematical object introduced: is it specific and already known (→ 'the')? Introduced for first time as any member of a class (→ 'a/an')? Used as adjective or with a named person (→ zero article)? Check m × n tables ('an m×n table'), theorems ('the binomial theorem' but 'Pythagoras' Theorem').

4. **Subject-verb agreement:** Every 'there exists/exist' and 'every/all' pairing must agree. Singular antecedent → 'exists'. Plural → 'exist'.

5. **Equality sign correctness:** For every '=' sign: are both sides genuinely equal at that step? Are limits/operators retained throughout? Is a row-operation written with '→' not '='?

6. **'⇒' correctness:** Replace '⇒' with 'implies' and read aloud. Left side must be a statement. Right side must be a statement. Neither side may be a bare equation appended to a verb phrase.

7. **'∀'/'∃'/'∈'/'⊆' correctness:** Read each symbol in English. Is '∀' used for 'for all' (not 'such that')? Is '∃' for 'there exists' (not 'for some')? Are intervals written with '⊆' and elements with '∈'?

8. **Non-transitivity of ≠:** Flag any chain a ≠ b ≠ c. Replace with explicit pairwise statements or 'pairwise distinct'.

9. **Terminology audit (domain-specific):** Run the Section 5 tables for any linear algebra, calculus, or geometry sentence: derivative vs. differentiation, on vs. in an interval, dim(Null A) vs. Null A = number, cyclic vs. concyclic, etc.

10. **Symbol minimisation check:** If a sentence contains three or more symbols (∀, ∃, ⇒, s.t.), consider whether word equivalents would be clearer.

11. **Isolated equation check:** Are there three or more consecutive display equations with no connecting words or logical symbols? If so, add connectives (⟺, ⟹, "which gives", "it follows that") and concluding prose.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

CTPC papers are mathematics-dense: derivations involve Hamiltonian flows, Cholesky parameterizations for positive-semidefinite covariance blocks, Port-Hamiltonian dissipative structures, and state-space equations in RTN/RSW frames. Every rule in this guide directly applies:

- **Equality sign correctness (§4.1):** When deriving the CTPC corrector update H(x) ≤ 0 via Lyapunov arguments, each step in the equality/inequality chain must preserve the operator (e.g., the time derivative d/dt cannot be silently dropped mid-derivation, just as lim[h→0] cannot be dropped).
- **'⇒' vs. prose (§4.2):** In proofs that dissipation conditions enforce Ḣ ≤ 0, the implication must connect two statements. "The Cholesky factor L is constructed ⇒ LL^T is PSD" is well-formed; "Cholesky parameterization ⇒ PSD" is not (the left side is a noun phrase, not a statement).
- **Terminology precision (§5.5 linear algebra):** CTPC uses matrices extensively. 'The entries of the covariance matrix are positive' not 'the covariance matrix is positive'. 'The rank of A is n' not 'A is rank n'. 'dim(Null A) = 0' not 'Null A = 0'.
- **∀/∃ correctness (§4.3):** Orbital perturbation arguments often invoke universal and existential quantifiers ('for all t ≥ 0 the energy satisfies…', 'there exists a dissipation rate…'); these must be written as full sentences.
- **Isolated equation avoidance (§6.3):** The classic error in ML-physics papers is presenting four lines of derivation with no words. CTPC derivations must connect each step with 'which implies', 'it follows that', or explicit logical symbols with a concluding sentence.
- **Complete paragraphs:** Any theorem or proposition about CTPC's properties (stability, covariance realism) must be embedded in a paragraph that states what is being proved, carries the argument, and explicitly concludes.

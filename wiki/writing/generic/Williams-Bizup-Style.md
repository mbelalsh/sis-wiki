# Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace (Williams & Bizup, 11th ed.)

## One-Line Intuition

Readers judge sentences by a small set of predictable principles — characters as subjects, actions as verbs, old information before new, emphasis at the end — and once you know those principles you can diagnose and revise any unclear sentence systematically.

---

## Why This Matters

Most writing advice consists of vague imperatives: "be clear," "be concise," "think of your audience." Williams's contribution is to replace vague advice with *diagnostic principles* grounded in how readers actually process sentences. The book answers three questions that most style guides ignore:

1. What is it in a sentence that makes readers judge it as unclear?
2. How do you diagnose your own prose to anticipate their judgment?
3. How do you revise so readers will think better of it?

The answer is not rules about grammar but a reader-centered model of how sentences create or frustrate understanding. Style is treated as *choice*, not correctness — every syntactic decision is a rhetorical one with predictable reader effects.

1. Distinguish real grammatical rules from folklore (pp. 10–21)
2. Use subjects to name the characters in your story (pp. 46–52)
3. Use verbs to name their important actions (pp. 32–39)
4. Open sentences with familiar units of information (pp. 68–70)
5. Get to the main verb quickly: avoid long intro phrases, long subjects, interrupted subject–verb connections (pp. 145–148)
6. Push new, complex information to the end (pp. 80–83)
7. Begin sentences that form a unit with consistent subjects/topics (pp. 72–74)
8. Be concise: cut meaningless words, compress phrases to words, prefer affirmatives (pp. 127–131)
9. Control sprawl: don't stack subordinate clauses; use resumptive, summative, and free modifiers instead (pp. 151–155)
10. Write to others as you would have others write to you (pp. 189–190)

## Core Content

### Lesson 1 — Understanding Style

**Style as choice, not correctness.** Every sentence is the product of countless small decisions. Style fails not because a writer breaks rules but because readers experience a mismatch between how the writer chose to arrange words and what readers need to process information.

**Three causes of unclear writing:**
- Writers plump up prose hoping it signals depth
- Writers freeze, worrying about grammatical correctness rather than reader comprehension
- Writers simply don't know what readers experience, because our own writing always seems clearer to us than to our readers — we read into it what we want them to get out of it

**Clarity vs. academese.** Williams traces unclear writing from 16th-century English prose imitating Latin, through 19th-century turgidity, to modern *bureaucratese* (government), *legalese* (law), and *academese* (academic writing that inflates small ideas into gassy abstractions). He quotes C. Wright Mills: "A turgid and polysyllabic prose does seem to prevail in the social sciences. Such a lack of ready intelligibility usually has little to do with the complexity of thought." (p. 5)

**Principles vs. prescriptions.** The book's principles are meant to help you *predict* how readers will judge your prose and then decide whether and how to revise. During drafting, ignore all of it — these principles apply to *revision*, not composition.

### Lesson 2 — Correctness

Williams's most liberating lesson: most of the "rules" taught in schools are **grammatical folklore** — invented by 18th-century grammarians who tried to make English conform to Latin, or by 19th-century teachers who simply made them up. None are based on how language actually works.

**Rules that are folklore (you can safely ignore them):**

| "Rule" | Reality |
|---|---|
| Never end a sentence with a preposition | Latin rule, never applied in English; Churchill mocked it: "That is the sort of pedantry up with which I will not put." |
| Never split an infinitive | Latin has no split infinitives because infinitives are single words; English infinitives are two words and split freely |
| Never begin a sentence with *And*, *But*, or *Because* | All fine; strong writers do it constantly |
| Never use *which* for a restrictive relative clause | Both *which* and *that* are used restrictively by careful writers |
| Use *I* only sparingly | No basis; avoiding it produces awkward circumlocutions |
| Never use the passive | The passive is a valuable tool; the problem is the *thoughtless* passive |

**Real rules** do exist — rules that, if violated, will cause readers to judge writing as uneducated or careless (e.g., *they was*, dangling modifiers that create unintended meanings). The agent's task is to distinguish real rules from folklore so writers stop worrying about the wrong things.

**The grammar you do need:**
To apply Williams's diagnostic principles, you need a small working vocabulary: SUBJECT, VERB, NOUN, ACTIVE/PASSIVE, CLAUSE, PREPOSITION, COORDINATION. You also need new meanings for two familiar words — TOPIC (what a sentence is about, often but not always its grammatical subject) and STRESS (the information a sentence emphasizes, typically at its end). New terms: NOMINALIZATION, METADISCOURSE, RESUMPTIVE MODIFIER, SUMMATIVE MODIFIER, FREE MODIFIER.

### Lesson 3 — Actions

**The core principle: verbs should name real actions.**

The most common cause of unclear prose is burying a key action inside a noun (a *nominalization*) and replacing it with a weak, semantically empty verb.

**Nominalization defined:** A nominalization is a noun derived from a verb or adjective. Examples:
- *decision* (from *decide*)
- *failure* (from *fail*)
- *investigation* (from *investigate*)
- *analysis* (from *analyze*)
- *assumption* (from *assume*)

**Diagnostic test:** Circle every nominalization. Ask: is the action it names better expressed as the main verb?

**Before/after examples (from pp. 29–36):**

Nominalizations masking real actions:
> "Our failure to **provide** adequate instruction was the **result** of our **assumption** that students would request help."

Revised with actions as verbs:
> "We failed to provide adequate instruction because we assumed students would request help."

Another:
> "There was a **decision** on the part of the executive committee to undertake a **review** of **qualification** requirements."

Revised:
> "The executive committee decided to review what it requires of applicants."

**Common nominalization patterns to hunt for:**

| Nominalization + weak verb | Better |
|---|---|
| make an *assumption* | assume |
| conduct an *investigation* | investigate |
| come to a *decision* | decide |
| give *consideration* to | consider |
| provide a *description* | describe |
| be in *agreement* | agree |
| have a *tendency* | tend |
| make an *improvement* | improve |
| take *action* | act |
| offer a *suggestion* | suggest |

**When nominalizations are acceptable:**
1. As the subject of a sentence when it refers to a previous clause: "These *decisions* troubled us." (The nominalization carries old information.)
2. When the action is the topic of the sentence: "The *analysis* of orbital residuals is the CTPC corrector's core function."
3. When no good verb exists: *our *approach*, *the *problem**
4. When a nominalization at the end of a sentence creates appropriate weight/emphasis

**Metadiscourse** — a related problem. Metadiscourse is writing *about* your writing rather than just writing. Excessive metadiscourse is throat-clearing:
> "It is important to note that the analysis revealed that..."

Better: "The analysis revealed that..."

Common metadiscourse to cut: *it is important to note that*, *I would argue that*, *the fact that*, *it seems that*.

### Lesson 4 — Characters

**The core principle: subjects should name the characters in your story.**

A sentence tells a story. It has characters doing things. When the grammatical subject is an abstraction rather than a real agent — a person, institution, or concrete entity that can *do* something — readers lose the sense of who is acting on whom, and the sentence feels impersonal, bureaucratic, and dense.

**The "story" principle:** Every piece of writing is a story with characters performing actions. Your job is to put the right character in the subject position.

**Diagnosis:** Read each sentence and ask: who is doing something here? If the character doing the action is buried in a prepositional phrase ("by X") or absent entirely, bring it to the subject.

**Before/after examples:**

Impersonal subject (abstraction):
> "**Inflation** has affected the ability of citizens to purchase homes."

Human character as subject:
> "**Citizens** can no longer afford homes because of inflation."

Or with passive hiding the agent:
> "**Policies** were enacted to reduce carbon emissions."

With real character:
> "**Congress** enacted policies to reduce carbon emissions."

**The passive voice — tool, not error.** The passive is valuable when:
- The agent is unknown, unimportant, or obvious: "The experiment was conducted at 25°C."
- You want to keep the same subject/topic across a sequence of sentences
- You want to move a long, complex agent to the end of the sentence (end-weight)

The problem is the *unthinking* passive that hides the real character for no rhetorical reason — especially when it allows writers to evade responsibility (see Lesson 12).

**Abstractions as characters (sometimes fine):** When you write about concepts, the concepts themselves become the characters. Williams acknowledges that abstract subjects are sometimes appropriate — the test is whether the abstraction *can* perform the action the verb names.

### Lesson 5 — Cohesion and Coherence

This lesson and Lesson 7 together form the core of Williams's paragraph-level theory. Two related but distinct concepts:

**Cohesion** = how individual sentences connect to each other (local, sentence-to-sentence flow)
**Coherence** = how the whole passage hangs together around a consistent set of topics (global, paragraph-level unity)

#### Cohesion: The Old–New Contract

**Principle:** Open every sentence with information readers already know (old/familiar), and close it with information that is new and complex.

Why: Readers process a sentence by using its opening to connect to what they already know, then absorbing the new material at the end. When new information appears first, readers have no context for it; they must hold it in working memory until they find the connection, which is cognitively expensive.

**The Topic Position (sentence opening):** The first five to eight words determine what the sentence is "about." They set up the context for everything that follows.

**Before (new before old — hard to read):**
> "Bacterial plasmids, or small circular forms of DNA that are separate from the chromosomal DNA, are the means by which rapid spread of resistance to antibiotics happens."

**After (old before new):**
> "Bacteria spread resistance to antibiotics rapidly by means of plasmids — small circular forms of DNA separate from chromosomal DNA."

**The Stress Position (sentence ending):** The last few words of a sentence receive the heaviest emphasis. Readers unconsciously expect the most important new information to fall at the end. When a sentence ends with a preposition, a qualifier, or a weak phrase, it feels anticlimactic.

**Principle:** Push the information you most want readers to remember to the end of the sentence. Use end-weight deliberately.

**Before (weak ending):**
> "The data were collected **in June**."

If what matters is the data, not when:
> "In June, we collected **the data**."

**Before/after (end-weight):**
> "Ohm's law is **V = IR**" vs. "The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance is expressed by **Ohm's law**, which states that **V = IR**."

#### Coherence: Topic Strings

**Principle:** A coherent passage has sentences that open with a consistent set of subjects/topics. Readers scan the left edge of sentences to track what a passage is "about." If topics hop randomly — even if each individual sentence is clear — the passage feels incoherent.

**Diagnosis: the topic string test.** List the grammatical subjects of every sentence in a passage. If they form a consistent set (all referring to the same character, concept, or entity), the passage is likely coherent. If they scatter randomly, the passage likely lacks coherence even if every sentence is locally correct.

**Example of inconsistent topic strings (incoherent):**
> "**The study** examined orbital decay rates. **SGP4 errors** were measured over 30-day windows. **Our approach** uses a neural corrector. **Residuals in RTN** coordinates informed the training signal."

Four different topics in four sentences — hard to track.

**Revised with consistent topics:**
> "**We** examined orbital decay rates by measuring SGP4 errors over 30-day windows. **We** trained a neural corrector on residuals in RTN coordinates."

**Metadiscourse as framing.** A small amount of metadiscourse can signal topic shifts explicitly ("In this section, we examine...") — but should be used sparingly and deliberately, not as throat-clearing.

### Lesson 6 — Emphasis

**The stress position = the position of greatest rhetorical emphasis in a sentence.**

Emphasis is not created by italics, boldface, or exclamation marks. It is created architecturally, by placing important information at the end of a sentence — where readers' attention naturally falls.

**Four principles for controlling emphasis:**

#### 1. End Weight
Long, complex information naturally belongs at the end. Short, simple information belongs at the beginning. When a long subject precedes a short verb phrase, the sentence feels top-heavy and readers labor through it to reach a weak payoff.

**Before (top-heavy):**
> "**That increasing the frequency of training runs on the GPU cluster shortens total wall-clock time by 40%** was our main finding."

**After (end-weight):**
> "Our main finding was **that increasing the frequency of training runs on the GPU cluster shortens total wall-clock time by 40%**."

#### 2. End Focus
Move the word or phrase you want to emphasize to the very end of the sentence. English readers expect the end to carry the point.

**Before:**
> "We propose **a Hamiltonian corrector** to handle the dissipation problem."

If dissipation is the key contribution:
> "To model dissipation, we propose **a Hamiltonian corrector**."

#### 3. Light Before Heavy
In coordinated elements, put the shorter before the longer. This is a general design principle that applies at every scale — subject-verb sequences, coordinated phrases, paragraph-level organization.

#### 4. Avoid Anticlimactic Endings
A sentence that ends with a preposition, a qualifier, a trivial phrase, or a word that repeats the topic feels anticlimactic.

**Before:**
> "This is the problem we must now address."

**After:**
> "We must now address **this problem**."

**Cleft sentences for emphasis.** Two constructions that move information into the stress position:

*It-cleft:* "It was **the corrector architecture** that enabled the improvement."
*What-cleft:* "What enabled the improvement was **the corrector architecture**."

### Lesson 7 — Motivation (Framing Paragraphs and Sections)

Readers must be *motivated* to read. They will not invest attention in a text unless they understand why reading it is worth their effort. This lesson addresses document-level framing.

**The problem of unmotivated information:** Writers often front-load context, background, and qualification before stating their point. Readers who don't already care about the topic have no reason to read through the context. They need to know the *problem* before they can appreciate the *solution*.

**The issue–discussion–point structure:**
A well-motivated passage has three moves:
1. **Issue** — establish what problem or question the passage addresses
2. **Discussion** — present information, evidence, analysis
3. **Point** — the key claim or takeaway

Most academic writing buries the point at the end of a section. Readers can tolerate this if the issue is clearly stated upfront. They cannot tolerate it if neither issue nor point is visible until they have read everything.

**Introductions:** An introduction must do two things: (1) establish a problem or question whose answer matters to readers, and (2) promise that the document will answer it. A document without a clearly stated problem has no motivating force.

**Short introductions:** For short documents (papers, memos), readers need only a sentence or two establishing the problem before the discussion.

### Lesson 8 — Global Coherence

Global coherence is the coherence of the document as a whole, achieved through the structural organization of sections and their relationship to a controlling argument.

**The point of paragraphs:** A paragraph should have a point — a sentence (or two) that states what the paragraph claims. Williams calls this the paragraph's *issue sentence* or *point sentence*. It should appear at or near the beginning of the paragraph so readers know what to expect and can frame the information that follows.

**Where to put the point sentence:**
- **First** (most common for technical/scientific writing): state the claim, then support it
- **Last** (for building to a conclusion): accumulate evidence, then state the claim
- **First and last** (for complex paragraphs): preview the claim, develop it, restate it

**Paragraph structure as argument.** Each paragraph should have one controlling claim that is supported by everything in that paragraph. If a paragraph has two claims, split it.

**Section introductions.** Each major section of a document needs a short introduction — typically one paragraph — that states the section's issue, previews its structure, and connects it to the preceding section.

**Document-level coherence.** A document is coherent when its sections form a logical sequence in which each section responds to a problem set up by the previous one. The reader should be able to predict what comes next because each section motivates the next.

### Lesson 9 — Concision

**Principle:** Every word should do work. Cut anything that does not.

Concision is not about writing short sentences. It is about removing words that add no meaning. A 50-word sentence can be more concise than a 10-word sentence if all 50 words are necessary and the 10-word sentence has redundancies.

#### Category 1: Redundant Pairs

Many phrases repeat the same idea twice. One word is enough:

| Redundant pair | Better |
|---|---|
| *basic and fundamental* | *fundamental* |
| *full and complete* | *complete* |
| *each and every* | *every* |
| *various and sundry* | *various* |
| *hopes and desires* | *hopes* |
| *true and accurate* | *accurate* |

#### Category 2: Redundant Modifiers (Implied Meaning)

When a modifier repeats what the noun already implies, cut it:

| Redundant | Better |
|---|---|
| *advance planning* | *planning* |
| *past history* | *history* |
| *future plans* | *plans* |
| *close proximity* | *proximity* |
| *free gift* | *gift* |
| *end result* | *result* |
| *completely surrounded* | *surrounded* |

#### Category 3: Meaningless Modifiers

These add length but no meaning: *kind of*, *really*, *basically*, *actually*, *virtually*, *practically*, *essentially*. Cut them.

#### Category 4: Pompous Diction

Latinate words used to inflate simple ideas:

| Pompous | Plain |
|---|---|
| *utilize* | *use* |
| *facilitate* | *help* |
| *implement* | *carry out* |
| *demonstrate* | *show* |
| *endeavor* | *try* |
| *commence* | *begin* |
| *is indicative of* | *indicates* |
| *is suggestive of* | *suggests* |
| *at this point in time* | *now* |
| *in the event that* | *if* |
| *in order to* | *to* |
| *despite the fact that* | *although* |
| *due to the fact that* | *because* |

#### Category 5: Throat-Clearing Phrases to Delete

These add words but no information:
- *It is important to note that...*
- *It should be pointed out that...*
- *The fact that X is Y...*
- *What this means is that...*
- *It is interesting to note that...*

Simply delete them and start with the substance.

#### Category 6: Negative → Affirmative

Affirmative phrasing is shorter and clearer:

| Negative | Affirmative |
|---|---|
| *not different* | *similar* |
| *not the same* | *different* |
| *not include* | *exclude* |
| *not consider* | *ignore* |
| *not accept* | *reject* |

#### Diagnosis procedure for concision

1. Circle every nominalization. Is the action better expressed as a verb?
2. Circle every weak verb (*is*, *are*, *have*, *make*, *provide*, *give*, *conduct*, *perform*). Is there a single, specific verb that replaces [weak verb + nominalization]?
3. Underline every *the fact that*, *despite the fact that*, *in order to*, *at this point in time*. Cut or replace.
4. Underline redundant pairs and modifiers. Cut one word of each pair.
5. Count the ratio of abstract Latinate nouns to concrete short-syllable verbs. If nominalizations dominate, nominalization-hunt.

### Lesson 10 — Shape

A sentence has shape. Shape determines how easily readers move through it. The key principles of shape complement the clarity principles in Part Two.

#### Principle 1: Get to Subject and Verb Quickly

Readers want to reach the subject and verb of the main clause in the first 7–8 words. Long introductory phrases and clauses delay the subject–verb core and make readers work harder.

**Before (delayed subject–verb):**
> "In light of the fact that the committee had reviewed all the available evidence, a decision was reached."

**After:**
> "Having reviewed all the evidence, the committee decided."

Diagnostic: underline the first 7–8 words of every sentence. Do they contain the subject and verb of the main clause?

**Remedy for long subjects:** Convert the long subject into an introductory subordinate clause.
> "**A company that focuses on hiring the best personnel and then trains them not just for the work they are hired to do but for higher-level jobs** is likely to earn the loyalty of its employees."

→ "**When a company focuses on hiring the best personnel and trains them for higher-level jobs,** it is likely to earn the loyalty of its employees."

Or split into two sentences.

#### Principle 2: Avoid Interrupting Subject–Verb and Verb–Object

Inserting a long clause between subject and verb, or between verb and object, forces readers to hold mental breath.

**Interrupted subject–verb (problematic):**
> "Some scientists, **because they write in a style that is impersonal and objective,** do not easily communicate with laypeople."

**Fixed (interruption moved):**
> "**Because some scientists write in an impersonal and objective style,** they do not easily communicate with laypeople."

Short interruptions (a single adverb) are fine.

#### Principle 3: Open with Your Point (for long sentences)

A long sentence should begin with a short main clause stating its point, then extend with supporting information. If the point is buried at the end or in the middle, readers cannot frame the complex information that precedes it.

**Backward (point at end):**
> "[High-deductible health plans and Health Saving Accounts into which workers and their employers make tax-deductible deposits] result in workers taking more responsibility for their health care."

**Forward (point first):**
> "[Workers take more responsibility for their health care] [when they adopt high-deductible insurance plans and Health Saving Accounts into which they and their employers deposit tax-deductible contributions.]"

#### Principle 4: Control Sprawl — Resumptive, Summative, and Free Modifiers

When a sentence needs extension, don't just tack on one subordinate clause after another. Use three named devices instead:

**Resumptive modifier:** End the grammatical sentence at a natural boundary, then resume with a key noun repeated, followed by a restrictive *that* clause:
> "We discovered a problem with the covariance estimator, **a problem that affects every training run longer than 48 hours**."

**Summative modifier:** End the grammatical sentence at a natural boundary, then add a noun phrase that *sums up* the preceding clause, followed by a *that* clause:
> "Economic changes have reduced Russian population growth to less than zero, **a demographic event that will have serious social implications**."

**Free modifier:** End the grammatical sentence at a natural boundary, then add a present or past participial phrase (*-ing* or *-ed*) or an adjective that comments on the subject of the closest verb:
> "The model converged in twelve epochs, **reducing wall-clock time by 40%**."
> "Leonardo da Vinci was a man of powerful intellect, **driven by an insatiable curiosity and a vision of artistic perfection**."

Free modifiers are "free" because they can also appear at the beginning:
> "**Driven by an insatiable curiosity**, Leonardo da Vinci was a man of powerful intellect."

#### Principle 5: Short-to-Long as a Unifying Principle

The short-before-long ordering principle applies at every scale:
- Subject–verb sequences: short subject, longer predicate
- Old–new: old (psychologically shorter) before new (psychologically longer)
- Long sentences: short point clause before long supporting clause
- Coordinated elements: put shorter elements before longer ones

#### Principle 6: Coordinate Gracefully

COORDINATION is the foundation of graceful sentence extension. When coordinating elements:
- Coordinate elements parallel in grammar *and* in thought
- Use correlative conjunctions (*both X and Y*, *not only X but Y*, *neither X nor Y*) to emphasize coordinations
- Order from shorter to longer
- Avoid faulty grammatical coordination (coordinating a gerund phrase with a *that* clause)
- Avoid faulty rhetorical coordination (joining elements with *and* when the logical relationship is *because*, *but*, or *therefore*)

#### Troubleshooting Long Sentences

**Faulty grammatical coordination** — elements coordinated that are not parallel in structure:
> "The committee recommends **revising the curriculum** and **that the division be reorganized**."
Fix: make both elements the same grammatical type.

**Faulty rhetorical coordination** — using *and* when the logical relationship is not additive:
> "Grade inflation is a problem at many universities, **and** it leads to a devaluation of good grades."
Fix: "Grade inflation is a problem at many universities, **because** it devalues good grades."

**Ambiguous modifiers** — move to unambiguous position.

**Dangling modifiers** — a modifier dangles when its implied subject differs from the explicit subject of the main clause. Make the implicit subject explicit.

### Lesson 11 — Elegance

Elegance is beyond clarity — it is the quality that makes a sentence not just clear but *memorable*. Williams is explicit that elegance cannot be taught directly; it must be absorbed through reading. But he identifies several devices that *can* produce elegant effects when used deliberately.

**Three characteristics of elegant prose:**
1. The simplicity of characters as subjects and actions as verbs (clarity principles from Lessons 3–4)
2. The complexity of balanced syntax, meaning, sound, and rhythm
3. The emphasis of artfully stressed endings

#### Device 1: Balanced Coordination

Balance one part of a sentence against another in word order, sound, rhythm, and meaning. The most common form is balanced coordination using *and*, *or*, *but*, *nor*, *yet*. The effect is symmetry.

Walter Lippmann (p. 169): "The national unity of a free people depends upon a sufficiently even balance of political power to make it impracticable **for the administration to be arbitrary** and **for the opposition to be revolutionary and irreconcilable**."

The balance echoes in topics (*administration / opposition*) and stressed meanings (*arbitrary / revolutionary / irreconcilable*).

**Uncoordinated balance** is also possible — balancing structures that are not grammatically coordinated:
> "A government [that is unwilling to *listen* to the *moderate hopes* of its citizenry] must eventually *answer* to the *harsh justice* of its revolutionaries."

#### Device 2: Climactic Emphasis — Five Ways to End a Sentence Strongly

How a sentence ends determines its rhythm and grace. Five methods:

1. **Weighty words:** End with a noun or nominalization that carries semantic weight. Adjectives and adverbs are lighter than nouns; prepositions are lightest of all. Churchill: "...the New World, with all its power and might, steps forth to the rescue and the **liberation** of the old." (p. 174)

2. **Of + weighty word:** A prepositional phrase headed by *of* followed by a single strong monosyllable or nominalization creates a satisfying climactic beat: "...the emptiness **of our spirits** and the shallowness **of our minds**." (p. 181, Exercise 11.2 model)

3. **Echoing salience:** End with a word that echoes in sound or meaning an important word earlier in the sentence. The echo amplifies emphasis: "...manner is indissolubly linked to matter; style shapes, and in turn is shaped by, **substance**." (p. 175)

4. **Chiasmus** (kye-AZZ-muss): The second part of a sentence reverses the element order of the first. Pattern AB → BA: "You reveal your own highest rhetorical skill by the way you respect **the beliefs most deeply held by your reader**." (p. 176 — ABCDE : ABEDC pattern)

5. **Suspension:** Delay the main verb and its object until the end by opening with multiple parallel subordinate clauses or phrases. The long build-up heightens the climactic landing. Effective only when used sparingly — its impact is inversely proportional to its frequency.

#### Nuances of Length and Rhythm

- Short sentences create urgency, directness, even staccato energy (Trotsky, Pound quoted at pp. 182–183)
- Long sentences can mirror complex thought or create the sense of accumulating experience (Mailer quoted at p. 183)
- Most writers should worry about sentence length only if *all* sentences are longer than ~30 words or all shorter than ~15 words
- Natural variety emerges when you revise for the principles already covered

**Lesson 11 summary (p. 185):** "Of the many graces of style, compression is still, I think, the first."

### Lesson 12 — The Ethics of Style

**Core claim:** Choosing subjects and verbs is not just a matter of clarity — it is a moral act. Every syntactic decision implies a philosophy of action and carries ethical weight.

#### The First Principle of Ethical Writing (p. 189–190)

> "Write to others as you would have others write to you."

This is the fundamental ethical test: would you willingly put yourself in the place of your readers and experience what they do as they read what you've written?

Clear prose is an ethical obligation, not just an aesthetic preference. When readers struggle to understand writing, they face a choice: invest more effort, or conclude the writer is careless, lazy, or self-indulgent. Either way, the writer's *ethos* — the character readers infer from the writing — suffers. Ethos hardens into reputation. Reputation is pragmatically valuable, not just altruistically nice.

#### Unintended Obscurity

Many writers who produce dense, impenetrable prose do not intend to. They simply don't know what readers experience. The ethical obligation in that case falls partly on *readers* — to respond candidly and helpfully when they have the standing to do so.

#### Intended Misdirection — Three Examples

Williams shows how stylistic choices can be used deliberately to mislead:

**Example 1: Sears recall (p. 191).** Sears was accused of overcharging. Their response: "With over two million automotive customers serviced last year in California alone, mistakes may have occurred." The verb *occurred* makes mistakes sound spontaneous rather than caused by Sears. The real agent (Sears) disappears. Had they written "When we serviced over two million customers, we made mistakes," the accountability would have been clear.

**Example 2: Peoples Gas rate increase (pp. 191–192).** A utility company notified customers of a rate increase. In the letter, no human character appears as subject/topic/agent — only "The Illinois Commerce Commission," "This," "The restructuring of rates," and "The new rates." The customer — the person whose interests are most at stake — appears only twice, in third person, only in prepositional phrases ("for various classes of utility *customers*"). The writer deliberately avoided making the customer the subject of sentences. Had the notice read "We can now make you pay more for your gas service after November 12, 1990," the ethical situation would have been transparent.

**Example 3: Automobile recall letter (pp. 192–193).** A recall letter about a potentially deadly defect. The subjects of seven sentences: "a defect," "this plate," "its failure," "your vehicle," "the secondary catch," "sudden hood fly-up," "occurrence of either condition." The real character — *you, the driver* — appears only as a possessive. The actions that should alarm readers — failure, crash, death — are all nominalized or passivized. The writers used the grammar of technical description to evade the grammar of danger.

**Lesson:** The choice of subjects is crucial not only for clarity but also for honesty or deception. Nominalizations and passives are the primary tools of stylistic misdirection.

#### Rationalizing Opacity

Some writers claim they must write in a complex style because they are breaking new intellectual ground. Williams's position:

> "In the final analysis... when writers claim their prose style must be difficult because their ideas are new, they are, as a matter of simple fact, more often wrong than right."

Wittgenstein: "Whatever can be thought can be thought clearly; whatever can be written can be written clearly." Williams adds: "...and with just a bit more effort, more clearly still." (p. 195)

#### Salutary Complexity / Subversive Clarity

Two further defenses of opacity, both rejected by Williams:
1. *Complexity is good for you* — "the harder we have to work, the more deeply we think." No evidence supports this.
2. *Clarity is a tool of power* — "those in power use deceptively simple language to dumb down complex issues." True, but the misuse of clarity does not indict clarity itself.

Williams's conclusion: clarity is an ideological value — it has to be created, maintained, and fought for. But those who attack clarity as a conspiracy are as wrong as those who attack science because some use it for malign ends.

#### Extended Analysis: Jefferson's Declaration of Independence (pp. 197–204)

Williams's most sophisticated example. Jefferson wrote the Declaration in three distinct stylistic registers:

**Parts 2 and 3** (the list of grievances and the colonists' attempts to negotiate): maximum clarity. King George is the explicit subject/topic/agent of every sentence in Part 2 ("He has refused ... He has forbidden ... He has refused ... He has called together..."). The colonists are the explicit subjects in Part 3 ("We have petitioned ... We have warned ... We have reminded..."). Jefferson's choice of subjects makes agency clear and unmistakable.

**Part 1** (the philosophical preamble, the famous "We hold these truths..."): almost no human character is a subject of an active verb. Abstractions perform the actions: "Prudence dictates," "experience has shown," "necessity constrains," "causes impel." Even "all men are created equal" is passive — the agent (God) is implicit. Jefferson's impersonal, abstract style strips the colonists of free will and invests agency in higher forces, making the revolution seem not a choice but an inevitability.

Jefferson's stylistic manipulation was not unethical if his ends justified his means — but Williams leaves this question open: "If his end did not justify his means, we might argue that Jefferson was being deceptive here, using language instead of logic to establish the crucial premise of his argument: the colonists were not free to do other than what they did." (p. 203)

**The lesson:** Subject choice is always a rhetorical and ethical choice, not just a grammatical one.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

### Which agent

**Layer 0 — Generic Writing Agent**: This is the primary corpus document for this agent. All twelve lessons apply directly, with no domain-specific modification needed.

**Layer 1a — Paper Writing Agent**: Lessons 3–8 and 12 are the most directly applicable. The clarity principles (characters/actions/cohesion/emphasis) govern every sentence in a research paper. Lesson 12 (ethics) governs how to frame claims about what a system does and doesn't do — especially important in an adversarial peer-review environment.

**Layer 1b — Proposal Writing Agent**: Lessons 7 and 8 (Motivation, Global Coherence) are especially critical here. Grant reviewers must be motivated to read; they must see the problem before the solution; sections must have explicit issue sentences. Lesson 4 (Characters) matters for framing who benefits from the proposed work.

### Mode: Drafting vs. Revising

**During drafting:** Ignore all of Williams. The principles gum up composition. Draft fast.

**During revising:** Apply the diagnostic cascade in order:
1. Clarity pass: characters and actions (Lessons 3–4)
2. Flow pass: old–new, topic strings (Lesson 5)
3. Emphasis pass: stress positions (Lesson 6)
4. Structure pass: paragraph points and section introductions (Lessons 7–8)
5. Concision pass: nominalizations, redundancy, throat-clearing (Lesson 9)
6. Shape pass: sentence length, interruptions, sprawl, modifiers (Lesson 10)

### Concrete Checks and Moves

**Check 1 — The Nominalization Hunt.** Circle every noun ending in *-tion*, *-ment*, *-ance*, *-ence*, *-ity*, *-ness*, *-ism*. Ask: is this the main action of the clause? If yes, convert to verb.

**Check 2 — The Subject Test.** Read each sentence and ask: "Who is doing this?" If the answer is not the grammatical subject, revise.

**Check 3 — The Topic String Scan.** List the subjects of every sentence in a paragraph. Do they form a consistent set? If they scatter, revise for consistent topics.

**Check 4 — The Stress Position Check.** Read the last 3–5 words of every sentence aloud. Does the most important information fall there? If the sentence ends weakly (preposition, qualifier, repeated topic), revise.

**Check 5 — The 7-Word Test.** Underline the first 7 words of every sentence. Do they contain the subject and main verb? If not, is there a good rhetorical reason for the delay?

**Check 6 — The Redundancy Scan.** Look for *due to the fact that*, *in order to*, *at this point in time*, *despite the fact that*, redundant pairs, meaningless modifiers. Cut mechanically.

**Check 7 — The Ethics Check (for claims about system behavior).** For every claim about what the system does or doesn't do, ask: who is the subject? Is the agent explicit? Could a reader be misled by a passive or nominalized framing?

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Williams's framework connects to Bilal's research writing in at least four concrete ways:

**1. The nominalization problem is endemic in ML/SDA writing.** The natural language of the field is abstract: *optimization*, *representation*, *estimation*, *propagation*, *correction*, *verification*. Every paper draft will be nominalization-heavy. Applying Lesson 3 systematically — converting nominalizations to verbs — will make CTPC papers significantly clearer to reviewers who are not specialists in astrodynamics.

**2. The character problem for technical claims.** CTPC papers need to state clearly who does what: "We train a corrector that models residuals in the RTN frame" is clearer than "Residual modeling in the RTN frame is performed by the corrector component of the CTPC framework." The first sentence has human researchers as characters; the second has an abstraction.

**3. Emphasis and the stress position for contribution claims.** Every reviewer skims sentences looking for what is new. The contribution of a paper should appear in the stress position of key sentences. "This paper presents a corrector that **enforces Ḣ ≤ 0 as a hard architectural constraint**" lands the key claim correctly. "A corrector with a hard Ḣ ≤ 0 architectural constraint **is presented in this paper**" buries it.

**4. Lesson 12 — Ethics of writing for safety-critical claims.** SiS operates in Space Domain Awareness, where wrong predictions can have life-safety consequences. The ethics lesson is directly relevant to how Bilal writes about system limitations, failure modes, and uncertainty bounds. Nominalizing and passivizing failures ("degraded performance may occur under sparse observation conditions") evades the ethical obligation to be clear about when the system fails and what happens when it does.

**5. Document motivation for grant proposals.** Lesson 7 (Motivation) is critical for AFRL/NSF proposals. A program officer who has reviewed fifty proposals will not invest attention in a proposal that does not immediately establish a compelling problem. The issue must precede the solution; the gap in knowledge must be stated before the proposed method for closing it.

## Open Questions

1. **Williams vs. the passive in ML papers.** The field convention is to use passive voice heavily ("the model was trained," "experiments were conducted"). Williams allows the passive when the agent is irrelevant or obvious. An agent reading this page should decide for each passive instance whether the agent is genuinely irrelevant or whether the active voice would be clearer. Where does convention end and obscurity begin?

2. **Nominalization in mathematical exposition.** Mathematical writing often *requires* nominalization — "the Lipschitz constant of the mapping," "the convergence rate of the optimizer." Williams's test (is this the main action?) distinguishes acceptable from problematic nominalizations, but in math-heavy passages, nearly every noun is a nominalized concept. A refined diagnostic for math-heavy writing is needed.

3. **Topic strings in cross-disciplinary papers.** CTPC papers address both ML and astrodynamics readers. The topic strings that are consistent for an ML reader (model, corrector, architecture) may be opaque to an astrodynamics reader, and vice versa. How do you maintain topic-string coherence for a mixed audience?

4. **Elegance vs. the 8-page NeurIPS constraint.** Williams's elegance devices (suspension, chiasmus, balanced coordination) lengthen sentences. In a venue with strict page limits, there is tension between graceful prose and maximal information density. Where should Bilal prioritize elegance vs. concision in paper writing?

5. **Lesson 12 applied to safety claims.** What exactly is the ethical obligation when writing about system uncertainty in SDA? If a CTPC paper understates failure modes to avoid discouraging reviewers, is that intentional misdirection? What is the minimum candor required?

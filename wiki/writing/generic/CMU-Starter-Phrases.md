# CMU Starter Phrases for Academic Writing

## One-Line Intuition
A concise rhetorical phrase bank — organized by communicative function — that gives you ready-made sentence openers for signaling organization, expressing agreement, registering disagreement or limitations, and drawing connections in academic prose.

## Why This Matters
Academic writing requires not just ideas but *moves* — recognizable rhetorical gestures that tell readers what you are doing at any given moment. Non-native English writers and even fluent writers under deadline routinely stall at the sentence level because they lack the stock phrases that signal these moves. This CMU Student Academic Success Center handout distills those phrases into four functional categories drawn directly from Graff & Birkenstein's *They Say / I Say* framework. Having these phrases internalized (or retrievable) means a writing agent or human author can immediately slot the right opener onto any claim without reinventing the wheel.

## Core Content

The handout groups phrases into four rhetorical functions. Every phrase uses placeholder variables (X, Y, Z) that you replace with your specific content — concepts, authors, findings, or theories.

---

### 1. Meta-language that signals a text's organization

Use these to orient the reader — announce structure, scope, or the paper's specific contribution.

| Phrase template | When to use |
|---|---|
| Arguments against phenomenon X depend on four assumptions: | Framing a critique you are about to unpack |
| The perspective that informs most research on X is… | Situating the dominant paradigm before you challenge it |
| I would like to discuss two alternatives and their implications… | Signaling a comparative structure |
| This paper will investigate X and propose four solutions/explanations… | Classic thesis-announcement opener |
| This paper addresses the limitations of Y… | Positioning paper as a gap-filler or corrective |

---

### 2. Language that shows agreement

Use these when your work corroborates, extends, or aligns with prior work. They show you are building on a foundation rather than contradicting it.

| Phrase template | When to use |
|---|---|
| There is strong convergent evidence for…. | Summarizing a settled empirical consensus |
| Research seems to agree that…. | Softer consensus; hedged agreement |
| X supports *or* extends Y's finding…. | Positioning your result as additive to prior work |
| We find support for this hypothesis/theory in…. | Linking your data to a theoretical prediction |
| Our data support *or* confirm *or* verify the work of X by showing that X… | Replication / corroboration framing |

---

### 3. Language that shows disagreement or limitations

Use these when your findings diverge from prior work, when you are critiquing methodology, or when you want to hedge the scope of your own claims. The handout includes a key note: **never use words like "failed" or "ignored"** when critiquing other researchers — frame your contribution positively.

| Phrase template | When to use |
|---|---|
| The research on X is mixed…. | Flagging genuine empirical tension in the literature |
| There is debate over…. | Acknowledging an unresolved controversy |
| Despite X, our findings suggest Y… | Acknowledging prior work while asserting a contrasting result |
| X's findings call into question *or* refute Y… | Strong rebuttal of a prior claim |
| While X found Y, these findings may be limited on the grounds that X's experimental design was… | Methodological critique — scoped as a limitation, not a failure |
| While X found Y, these findings cannot be extended to Z… | Generalizability critique — denying applicability to a new domain |

> **Handout note (verbatim):** "Avoid using words like 'failed' or 'ignored' when critiquing other researchers. Instead, frame your contribution in positive terms: 'While X pioneered the field of Y, my work contributes X…'"

---

### 4. Language that suggests a connection

Use these to signal causal reasoning, conditional relationships, or a logical leap from prior evidence to your proposed contribution.

| Phrase template | When to use |
|---|---|
| As a consequence of X, Y… | Causal chain — X caused or enabled Y |
| In the absence *or* presence of X, Y… | Conditional relationship — Y holds only when X is/isn't present |
| To see whether these findings apply to X, we propose Y… | Motivating a new experiment or method by extending prior results |
| Research demonstrating X has led scientists to propose Y… | Tracing an intellectual lineage — X as motivation for Y |

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Which agent:** Layer-0 Generic-Writing-Agent. Every upper-layer agent (paper-writing, proposal-writing) inherits from Layer 0, so this phrase bank is universally applicable.

**Concrete moves:**

1. **Organization announcement (Intro / Abstract):** When drafting an introduction or abstract, pull from Category 1 to construct the thesis-announcement sentence. For a CTPC paper: *"This paper investigates X and proposes four solutions/explanations…"* becomes *"This paper investigates physics-as-architecture constraints for orbital propagation and proposes a dissipation-enforcing Port-Hamiltonian corrector."*

2. **Related-work positioning:** When writing a related-work or background section, alternate between Category 2 (agreement) and Category 3 (disagreement/limitation) templates. This prevents a paragraph that only praises prior work or only critiques it — the rhetorical alternation is what reviewers read as scholarly balance.

3. **Transition sentences between sections:** Category 4 (connection) templates are ideal transition sentences. *"Research demonstrating X has led scientists to propose Y"* bridges a background paragraph to a methods paragraph without a jarring jump.

4. **Limitation sections:** Category 3 templates scaffold the Limitations section of any paper. The handout's explicit warning about "failed / ignored" should be treated as a hard style rule by the agent: never output a limitation statement that implies negligence by prior authors.

5. **Hedging calibration:** Categories 2 and 3 both contain hedged variants ("seems to agree," "may be limited"). The agent should calibrate hedge strength to the strength of the evidence: strong convergence → Category 2 unhedged; mixed evidence → Category 2 hedged or Category 3 opening.

**Draft-time checklist the agent can apply:**
- Every paragraph that cites prior work: does it open with a Category 2 or Category 3 template?
- Every new section: does the first sentence use a Category 1 signpost or Category 4 connection?
- Any sentence that critiques a prior author: does it pass the "not failed/ignored" test?

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

CTPC papers must navigate a specific rhetorical challenge: they disagree with the dominant SGP4 paradigm (soft-penalty physics-informed ML) while remaining collegial toward a large body of PIML work that Bilal builds on. The phrase bank maps directly onto this challenge:

- **Category 3 disagreement templates** are the primary tool for positioning CTPC against soft-constraint PINN approaches: *"While [prior PHNN work] found [improved energy conservation], these findings may be limited on the grounds that [no hard dissipation constraint was enforced architecturally]."*
- **Category 4 connection templates** link SGP4's well-understood error signature to CTPC's corrector motivation: *"Research demonstrating [SGP4 drag-epoch errors] has led scientists to propose [ML residual correctors in the RTN frame]."*
- **Category 1 organization templates** are the backbone of any CTPC paper's introduction: *"This paper addresses the limitations of [soft-penalty physics-informed ML for SDA] and proposes [a Port-Hamiltonian corrector with hard dissipation enforcement]."*
- **Category 2 agreement templates** are used when acknowledging the parts of PIML/Neural-ODE literature that CTPC does build on: *"There is strong convergent evidence for [the utility of continuous-time neural dynamics in trajectory learning]."*

The "never say failed/ignored" rule is especially important for SiS papers: reviewers from the PIML and SDA communities will be familiar with SGP4 and prior PHNN work. Framing CTPC as a contribution that *extends* the field, not one that exposes negligence, is the tone that passes review.

## Open Questions

1. The handout has only four categories. Graff & Birkenstein's full *They Say / I Say* book has more (e.g., phrases for introducing "they say," phrases for objections, phrases for conclusions). Should the Layer-0 corpus include the full book, or is the CMU distillation sufficient for a writing agent?
2. Are there ML-specific agreement/disagreement phrases that should supplement this general academic bank? (e.g., "Our method achieves state-of-the-art on X while reducing computational cost by Y" — a form not covered here.)
3. Category 4 (connection) phrases presuppose a linear causal chain. For SDA/orbital mechanics papers where the connection is probabilistic or statistical, what is the correct hedged variant?

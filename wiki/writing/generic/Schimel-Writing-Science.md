# Writing Science: Schimel's Storytelling Framework for Papers and Proposals

## One-Line Intuition

Science writing is storytelling — not metaphorically but structurally — and every paper and proposal must be built on a coherent narrative arc with an opening, a conflict, an action, and a resolution if it is to be read, remembered, and cited.

---

## Why This Matters

Most science writing fails not because the science is bad, but because the writing treats the paper as a data-delivery vehicle rather than a story. Readers are humans who evolved to process narrative. When writing abandons story structure, readers disengage, reviewers reject, program officers defund. Schimel's central thesis (p. 1–8) is that the same cognitive machinery that makes novels compelling also determines which papers get cited and which proposals get funded. The implications are concrete and structural, not stylistic.

This book is the shared backbone for both the paper-writing agent (Layer 1a) and the proposal-writing agent (Layer 1b): it applies to every scientific document that must persuade a reader, regardless of format.

---

## Core Content

### Part I: Story Structure

#### Chapter 1–2: Science as Story

Schimel opens by asserting that scientists are professional storytellers. The product is not data but knowledge — the advance in understanding that follows from data plus interpretation. Stories are how humans organize, transmit, and remember knowledge. A paper that lacks story structure will not be cited, because readers will not remember it, will not understand its significance, and may never finish reading it.

The key principle: **make the reader's job easy**. This is Principle 1 of the book. Every structural decision — what goes where, how long a section runs, what to emphasize — should be evaluated by this standard.

#### Chapter 3: SUCCES — What Makes a Story Stick

Schimel adapts Heath and Heath's SUCCES formula (from *Made to Stick*) as a diagnostic for scientific writing:

| Letter | Quality | What it means in science writing |
|--------|---------|----------------------------------|
| **S** | Simple | Find the simple core story. What is the ONE thing this paper is about? |
| **U** | Unexpected | Create knowledge gaps — what don't we know, and why does it matter? |
| **C** | Concrete | Use concrete, specific language. Not "little is known" but "the rate constant has never been measured below 298 K." |
| **C** | Credible | Make your science verifiable. Readers need to evaluate your claims. |
| **E** | Emotional | Make readers care about your characters (organisms, molecules, phenomena). |
| **S** | Stories | Build a narrative arc. OCAR is the formal instantiation. |

SUCCES is a **diagnostic checklist**: when a paper feels weak, identify which element is missing. Most weak science writing fails on S (no simple story), U (no surprise), or C (not concrete). Proposals additionally fail on E (no emotional hook for the program officer).

The last S ("Stories") is treated as the master element — it encompasses all others when done well.

#### Chapter 4: Story Structures — OCAR, ABDCE, LDR

Schimel introduces three named story structures applicable to scientific documents:

---

**OCAR: Opening — Challenge — Action — Resolution**

This is the master structure for most scientific papers (specialist journals, IMRaD format):

```
O — Opening:    Frame the large problem. Introduce characters and stakes.
C — Challenge:  Define the specific knowledge gap. State the question.
A — Action:     Methods + Results + most of Discussion.
R — Resolution: Conclusions. Answer the challenge; widen back to the opening.
```

OCAR maps cleanly onto IMRaD:
- Introduction = O + C (Opening through the knowledge-gap Challenge)
- Materials and Methods + Results + Discussion = A (Action)
- Conclusions = R (Resolution)

The shape of OCAR is an hourglass (Schimel uses this metaphor explicitly, pp. 35–37):
- The Opening is wide (large problem, broad audience).
- The Funnel narrows to the specific Challenge.
- The Action is the narrow middle (detailed, technical).
- The Resolution widens back out to significance.

**Critical rule**: the width of the Opening must match the width of the Resolution. If you open broadly (global climate) but resolve narrowly (one bacterial enzyme kinetic constant), readers feel cheated. If you open narrowly but resolve broadly, you undersell your work (pp. 45–46).

---

**ABDCE: Action — Background — Development — Climax — Ending**

Used in high-impact general-audience venues (*Nature*, *Science*, *PNAS* broad-audience papers) and grant proposals:

```
A — Action:      Start with the most exciting result or claim. Hook immediately.
B — Background:  Pull back and fill in context (after the hook).
D — Development: Develop the evidence and argument.
C — Climax:      The key finding.
E — Ending:      Implications and resolution.
```

ABDCE inverts the patient OCAR approach — it **leads with the punchline**. The opening action must be strong enough to grab any interested reader. This requires a "queen launch" rather than a "pawn push" (see below). Most grant proposals use ABDCE or LDR structure because reviewers are impatient.

---

**LDR: Lead — Development — Resolution**

The journalistic structure, common in *Science* reports, some *Nature* papers, and high-impact Letters. Extremely front-loaded:

```
L — Lead:        The most important conclusion, stated immediately.
D — Development: Evidence and context supporting the lead.
R — Resolution:  Brief synthesis tying back to the lead.
```

LDR is unforgiving — the lead must carry the entire paper's weight. It is effective when the finding is sufficiently striking to stand alone without build-up.

---

**Summary table: when to use which structure**

| Structure | Venue / Use case | Audience patience | Opening style |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| OCAR | Specialist journals, dissertations, book chapters | High | Pawn push or queen launch |
| ABDCE | Nature/Science papers, NSF/NIH proposals, broad audience | Low | Queen launch required |
| LDR | Science reports, Letters, high-impact short papers | Very low | Lead = conclusion |

#### Chapter 5: The Opening

The Opening has two jobs: (1) identify the large problem that motivates the work, and (2) frame the audience — tell readers this paper is for them.

**Two opening styles** (pp. 46–47):

- **Pawn push**: A calm, incremental opening — one or two sentences that set up the problem step by step before diving into specifics. Safe, works for specialist journals. Common and acceptable, but uninspiring when done lazily.
- **Queen launch**: A bold, immediate statement that seizes control of the story from the first sentence. Grabs the broadest possible audience and signals confidence. Required for *Nature*, *Science*, and grant proposals. A queen launch dives straight into the central tension or finding.

**Schimel's analogy** (pp. 47–48): Opening a paper is like an opening chess move. Beginners push a pawn because they've seen others do it. Masters push a pawn *strategically* as the start of a plan. Or they launch the queen — a move impossible in chess but available in writing. The best science writers know which move serves their story.

**Opening width rule**: the Opening should be exactly as wide as the story you will deliver. Frame too wide → readers expect a broad paper and are disappointed by narrow conclusions. Frame too narrow → you lose readers who would have been interested in the broader implications.

**The Pawn-Push / Queen-Launch diagnostic**: For every paper, ask: did I write a pawn push or a queen launch? For specialist journals, a pawn push is acceptable if it is well-crafted. For *Nature*, NSF proposals, or CTPC papers targeting interdisciplinary audiences, a queen launch is required.

---

#### Chapter 6: The Funnel — Connecting O and C

The body of the Introduction (between Opening and Challenge) is called the **Funnel** (pp. 50–57). Its function:
- Narrow from the large problem (O) to the specific question (C)
- Frame the **knowledge gap**: what do we know? What is missing? Why does that matter?
- Build an argument that answering the specific question is necessary to making progress on the large problem

**Key principle**: An Introduction is NOT a literature review. A literature review builds a wall of knowledge. An Introduction identifies the hole in that wall. The two tell different stories and move differently:

| Mode | Focus | Citation style |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Introduction | What we do NOT know (the gap) | X occurs (Smith 2003) — finding first |
| Literature review | What we DO know | Smith (2003) found X — author first |

**The Bizzwidget Problem** (pp. 54–55): Many authors write proposals and papers that jump straight to their solution before defining the problem. Schimel uses the analogy of a door-to-door salesman: "Hi, I'm selling the Bizzwidget — it's the most amazing tool you've ever seen!" The customer closes the door because she doesn't know she has a problem. The fix: first convince readers they have problem X, then show your solution.

**Bad Introduction subtypes**:
1. *Failing to identify the problem*: using "little is known about X" (scientifically inaccurate, logically weak, linguistically fuzzy — see p. 54).
2. *Offering a solution before defining the problem*: the Bizzwidget problem.
3. *Literature dump*: presenting what is known, not what is unknown.

**Stepwise narrowing**: Good funnel writing narrows step by step, with each step justified. Each narrowing move must explain *why* we need to know the next-smaller thing. The methane/OH atmospheric chemistry example in pp. 51–53 (the Vaghjiani/Ravishankara *Nature* paper) is Schimel's canonical illustration: the authors moved from global warming → methane sources → the rate constant for OH+CH₄, justifying each step.

---

#### Chapter 7: The Challenge

The Challenge is the pivot point of the paper — the specific question or hypothesis that the work addresses (pp. 58–65):

**Question vs. Hypothesis vs. Objective**:
- The **question** is mandatory. "What is the rate constant for the reaction between OH and CH₄ at arctic temperatures?" Every paper must have a clear question.
- A **hypothesis** is a falsifiable version of the question. Some fields require it; others don't. The question always comes first.
- **Objectives** describe what information you will collect. Many authors write only objectives and leave the question implicit — this is almost always wrong. Objectives without a question invite the criticism "why are you wasting my time?"

**Canonical challenge form** (p. 60): "To learn X, we did Y." The X is the knowledge you hope to gain. The Y is the approach. The most common weak form is "We did Y" — stating only what was done, not what was learned.

**Challenge order matters** (pp. 65–66): When a challenge has multiple goals, lead with the most important (intellectual) one. The most common mistake is leading with the methodological goal and burying the scientific question — this frames the paper as being primarily about methods comparison rather than scientific advance.

**After the challenge**: In some papers, especially telegraphed-OCAR structures (common in biomedical literature), authors add a brief preview of conclusions: "In this paper we show..." This helps impatient readers and is more front-loaded than standard OCAR but less extreme than ABDCE.

---

#### Chapter 8: Action — Methods, Results, Discussion

The Action covers everything between Challenge and Resolution: Materials and Methods, Results, and most of Discussion (pp. 67–82).

**Core principle**: You are not presenting results; you are telling a story. Each action section is a mini-story with its own OCAR arc.

**Methods** (pp. 68–69):
- Use LD (Lead/Development) structure: provide an overview first, then details. Most readers don't need the details on first reading, but specialists need them.
- Tap into established schemas (named methods, standard approaches). "We measured protein by the Lowry approach" communicates instantly to any biochemist. Use the schema, then note deviations.
- Never describe a method without saying why you used it. The method serves the question.

**Results and Discussion** (pp. 70–82):
- Three types of material in results: (1) Data — what you measured. (2) Inference — the clear interpretation any practitioner would draw. (3) Interpretation — your specific thoughts on what it means for the larger problem.
- It is essential to *distinguish* data from interpretation, but not necessarily to *separate* them physically. Many field-specific conventions govern this; the author must decide.
- **Data presentation** (LD structure for results): lead with the main point or pattern, then provide details. "Soil bacterial diversity varied across ecosystem types. Soil pH was, by far, the best predictor." — the first sentence is the lead; everything else develops it.
- **Statistics and Stories** (pp. 76–79): Statistics establish credibility; they do not carry the story. The story is in the data, not the p-value. "Warming significantly increased methane emissions (p < 0.05)" buries the story. "Warming increased methane emissions by a factor of 3.4 (p < 0.05)" tells a story. Report the magnitude, not just the significance.
- **"Murder your darlings"** (p. 74): Collect all the data you need; present only what the story requires. Archive the rest. This is painful but essential.
- **Discussion structure**: can use OCAR (open with the challenge, work through evidence, end with resolution) or LDR (open with conclusion, develop evidence, close with synthesis). Both work; LDR is more common for Discussion sections.

---

#### Chapter 9: The Resolution

The Resolution is the power position — the last thing readers remember (pp. 83–94).

**The hourglass closes**: A good Resolution steps backward through OCAR, widening back out to the opening problem. It must:
1. Synopsize the key results (what did you find?)
2. Synthesize those results (what do they mean, as new knowledge?)
3. Demonstrate contribution to the larger problem identified in the Opening

**Three types of bad resolution** (pp. 87–93):

| Type | Description | Fix |
|------|-------------|-----|
| Weak | Summarizes results but doesn't synthesize into new knowledge | Identify the actual new insight and state it explicitly |
| Distracting | Introduces new ideas not in the Introduction | Weave them back into the Opening where they belong |
| Undermining | "More research is needed" / "X remains to be established" | State what you DID establish; end positive and specific |

**The worst resolution failure**: actively undermining conclusions. Ending with "more research is needed" throws away the power position and makes readers remember that the work is incomplete. Instead, conclude with a question that *grows from* the work (not a doubt *about* the work). Posing a concrete new question is powerful; admitting you didn't finish is fatal.

**Resolutions in Proposals** (pp. 92–94): Proposals using ABDCE structure often lack a resolution paragraph — they end after describing experiments. This is a mistake. The resolution paragraph in a proposal reiterates the big problem, explains how all proposed components work together, and makes the final case for funding. Program officers often form their final opinion from the resolution. End strong.

---

#### Chapter 10: Internal Structure — Nested Arcs

Every section of a paper must have its own internal story arc (pp. 95–103). The paper is a hierarchical structure of nested arcs (Figure 10.2, p. 96):

```
Paper arc
  Section arcs
    Paragraph arcs
      Sentence arcs (clauses)
```

**Arc properties**:
- A complete arc: has an opening, development, and resolution.
- A broken arc: opens a topic, disappears mid-paper, reappears elsewhere. This is "stream-of-consciousness" writing — each sentence flows from the previous but the paragraph has no coherent goal.
- Each arc must be resolved before opening a new one. When you introduce a topic, develop it fully and resolve it before moving to the next.

**Self-audit checklist** (p. 102–103):
- Does each unit make a single, clear point?
- When several paragraphs together form a section, are the linkages clear?
- Has every extraneous thought that breaks the serial arc structure been removed?
- When a new topic is introduced, is it resolved before a newer topic appears?
- Is every major unit defined by either a subhead or clear opening text?

### Part II: Language — Sentences, Paragraphs, Flow, Words

#### Chapter 11: Paragraphs

A paragraph is a story arc at the smallest coherent scale. It should tell one complete story (pp. 104–112).

**Two paragraph structures**:

1. **TS-D (Topic Sentence — Development)**: The classical "point-first" structure. The first sentence makes the point; the rest develops it. Simple, clean, the dominant structure in science writing. If you can read only the first sentence of every paragraph and still follow the paper's argument, the TS-D structure is working.

2. **OCAR/LDR paragraph**: "Point-last" structure. Opens with an issue or question, develops evidence, ends with the conclusion. More sophisticated; useful when the point emerges from the data rather than being declared upfront.

**The paragraph as unit of composition**: Strunk and White's second principle of composition. A paragraph gains its coherence from developing one complete idea. A paragraph that tries to do two things usually does both poorly.

---

#### Chapter 12: Sentences — Topic and Stress Positions

Schimel draws on Clark and Williams's sentence mechanics (pp. 113–130). Every sentence has two power positions:

| Position | Name | Function |
|----------|------|----------|
| Beginning | **Topic** | What this sentence is about. Anchor to what reader already knows (old information). |
| End | **Stress** | The new information — what readers should remember and emphasize. |

**The 2-3-1 rule** (Clark): The most emphatic position in a sentence is the end (1). The second most emphatic is the beginning (2). The middle (3) is the least emphatic. Put your most important word at the end.

**Old-new contract**: Each sentence should begin with information the reader already knows (from the previous sentence or general knowledge), then end with new information. This creates smooth flow and makes each new piece of information feel earned.

**Passive vs. active voice**: Not a simple "always use active" rule. Passive voice is useful to control topic and stress — it allows the grammatical subject (topic position) to be what the story is about, even if it is not the agent of the action. Use active when the agent is the story. Use passive when the result or object is the story.

**Weak syntax patterns to avoid**:
- Nominalization: turning verbs into nouns ("we performed an evaluation of" vs. "we evaluated"). Nominalizations drain energy and often force passive constructions.
- Beginning sentences with "It is" or "There are" — this pushes the real topic out of the topic position and buries it in the middle.
- Long, rambling opening clauses that delay the subject.

---

#### Chapter 13: Flow

Flow is what happens when readers move through a text without friction. It operates at three levels (pp. 131–145):

**1. Sentence-to-sentence flow**: governed by the old-new contract. The stress of sentence N should be picked up by the topic of sentence N+1.

**2. Paragraph-to-paragraph flow**: governed by arc linkages. The resolution of one paragraph's arc should connect to the opening of the next.

**3. Section-to-section flow**: governed by the larger arc structure. Transitions must explain *why* we are moving from one topic to the next, not just signal the move.

**Transitions**: Poor transitions are either (a) absent (abrupt jumps) or (b) mechanical ("In addition," "Furthermore," "Moreover") without logical content. Good transitions carry a logical argument: "Because the rate constant depends on temperature, the next question is how temperature varies in the Arctic."

**Flow paradox**: Adding linking words can *improve* flow while *increasing* word count — a trade-off worth making. But good arc structure reduces the need for transitions because the narrative logic is already visible.

---

#### Chapter 14: Paragraphs (extended — topic and stress within paragraphs)

Chapter 14 extends topic/stress principles to the paragraph level. The first and last sentences of a paragraph are its power positions — use them for the main point and the synthesis respectively. The middle sentences develop the argument. A paragraph that buries its main point in the third sentence is squandering the topic power position.

---

#### Chapter 15: Words

The fundamental word-choice principles (pp. 144–156):

**Jargon vs. technical terms** (pp. 146–149):
- *Jargon*: a term that refers to a schema the reader does NOT hold, or for which a plain-language equivalent exists. Jargon excludes.
- *Technical term*: a term that refers to a schema the reader DOES hold, or for which no adequate plain substitute exists. Technical terms are necessary.
- The boundary is audience-dependent. "NPP" (net primary production) is a technical term to ecologists; jargon to anyone else.
- Where to introduce a term in the sentence controls how readers experience it: topic position → assumed to be known; stress position → defined for everyone; middle position → reminder, soft definition.

**Anglo-Saxon vs. French/Latin** (pp. 151–153): English has two vocabularies. Anglo-Saxon words are short, concrete, energetic. French and Latin words are long, formal, abstract. Academic English instinctively reaches for French/Latin — this makes writing feel heavy and distant.

| Long French/Latin | Short Anglo-Saxon |
|------------------|------------------|
| Duration | Length, time |
| Consume | Eat |
| Demonstrate | Show |
| Initiate | Start |
| Utilize | Use |
| Mortality | Death |

Rule of thumb: if a word ends in *-ate*, it is Latin (heavy). If it ends in *-ion*, it is French (heavy). Prefer the short Anglo-Saxon alternative unless there is a genuine distinction.

**Compound nouns vs. prepositional phrases** (pp. 153–155): Prefer compound nouns ("reaction rate," "nitrogen supply," "fossil fuel burning") over prepositional phrases ("rate of reaction," "supply of nitrogen," "burning of fossil fuels"). Compound nouns are shorter and tighter. Exception: when stacking three or more nouns creates an unreadable "noun train" — break it up with a preposition.

**Nominalization** (pp. 153–154): Converting verbs to nouns ("initiation of," "demonstration of," "application of") is the single most pervasive flaw in academic science writing. It produces passive constructions, prepositional phrases, and abstract language. Replace nominalizations with the underlying verb: "we initiated" not "initiation was performed by."

#### Chapter 16: Condensing

Condensing is not optional — it is a professional obligation (pp. 158–165). NSF proposals have strict page limits. Journals increasingly enforce length limits. Dense writing signals poor thinking; reviewers notice and penalize.

**The two-step condensing strategy** (p. 160):
1. **"Prune the big limbs"** (Roy Peter Clark / Stephen King): identify what the story does NOT need and eliminate it. This is structural — eliminate entire sections, redundant results, distracting tangents.
2. **"Shake out the dead leaves"**: cut words within the pieces that survive. Target:

| Target | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Redundancies | Using several words where one suffices. "Most, but not all" → "Most." |
| Obvious | Statements any reader would assume. "There is evidence that" → delete. |
| Empty amplifiers | Adverbs/adjectives that intensify without adding content: certainly, dramatically, highly, quite, very, rather, entirely, simply. |
| Metadiscourse | Sentences about the text rather than about science: "In this section we will discuss..." |
| Verbosity | Using five words where two work. |

**Stephen King's rule** (p. 160): "Formula: second draft = first draft − 10%." Schimel applies this systematically to proposals: first drafts run 18 pages; final drafts must be 15.

**NSF-specific advice**: A densely packed proposal (figure 16.1, p. 159 — literally blurred to illustrate the problem) signals to reviewers that the PI cannot prioritize. A clear, well-structured 15-page proposal will outperform an 18-page draft squeezed into 15 pages with formatting tricks (smaller font, reduced margins).

### Part III: Titles and Abstracts

#### Chapter 17: Titles

The title has one job: attract the right readers while not excluding potential readers (pp. 181–195, inferred from chapter structure).

**Title strategies**:
- **Declarative titles**: State the main conclusion. "Warming increases soil carbon loss by a factor of 3." Highly effective — immediately communicates the finding and is more memorable and searchable.
- **Question titles**: Frame the challenge. "Does warming increase soil carbon loss?" Engages curiosity but does not communicate the answer.
- **Descriptive titles**: "Effects of warming on soil carbon loss." Neutral, common in many fields, but the weakest option — does not communicate findings or engage curiosity.

**Schimel's preference**: declarative titles for papers, because they communicate the resolution and are more likely to be cited (readers know what they are citing). Question titles are appropriate when the answer is genuinely uncertain or when the question itself is the contribution.

**Title pitfalls**:
- Too long (embedding multiple findings).
- Acronym-heavy (excludes readers outside the subfield).
- Too narrow (fails to engage the broad audience the opening promises).

---

#### Chapter 18: Abstracts

The Abstract is the most-read section of any paper or proposal. It is a standalone mini-paper with its own complete OCAR story arc (pp. 196–220, inferred from chapter structure and from Schimel's consistent framework):

**Abstract structure (OCAR)**:
1. **Opening** (1–2 sentences): The large problem. Wide enough to engage broad audience.
2. **Challenge** (1–2 sentences): The specific question or gap. What is unknown?
3. **Action** (2–4 sentences): What you did and what you found. The key results, not the methods detail.
4. **Resolution** (1–2 sentences): The main conclusion and its significance. Back to the opening problem.

**Common abstract failures**:
- Leading with methods rather than the problem.
- Describing objectives without stating the question.
- Omitting the resolution (ending with results but no conclusion).
- Using the abstract as a table of contents ("In this paper we describe... In section 3 we present...") — this fails SUCCES on nearly every dimension.

**Proposal abstracts**: Even more critical — the program officer often makes a preliminary funding decision from the abstract alone. The abstract must be a complete ABDCE or OCAR story, not a list of aims. It must answer: what is the problem? What is unknown? What will you do? What will this knowledge enable?

When the writing is good, we notice the ideas and the data, and those are what make the science."

12. **p. 160**: "To write concisely, Roy Peter Clark gives the advice of 'Prune the big limbs, then shake out the dead leaves.' He elaborates by saying that 'brevity comes from selection, not compression, a lesson that requires lifting blocks from the work.'"

13. **p. 83** (Resolution chapter): "A good resolution shows us how our understanding of nature has advanced, and by offering new insights into the problem identified in the opening, it wraps up the story. This creates the story's spiral, closing back to the original topic, but drawing it out by showing how the starting point has moved."

This source is the **shared backbone** for both the paper-writing agent (Layer 1a) and the proposal-writing agent (Layer 1b). It is the single most important craft source in the corpus.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

### For CTPC papers

The CTPC paper structure should follow OCAR with a queen launch:
- **Opening (queen launch)**: Satellite collision avoidance is a safety-critical problem. Current orbit propagators (SGP4) accumulate errors that exceed safe conjunction thresholds within days. [Wide enough to engage aerospace + ML + safety communities.]
- **Funnel**: Space debris environment is growing; conjunction data messages have high false-positive rates; SGP4 error is systematic and learnable; existing ML correctors ignore physical constraints, producing unphysical covariances.
- **Challenge**: "To learn whether physics-constrained ML correction can improve conjunction probability estimates, we developed CTPC — a framework that encodes dissipative dynamics as hard architectural constraints rather than soft penalties."
- **Action**: CTPC architecture (Ph predictor + ML corrector + Cholesky covariance); evaluation on TLE data; comparison to SGP4 baseline and unconstrained ML.
- **Resolution**: CTPC achieves X% improvement in covariance realism while guaranteeing energy-non-increasing dynamics. This closes the circle to the opening: better orbit prediction enables safer conjunction screening.

**Knowledge gap framing**: avoid "little is known about ML for SDA." Say instead: "Existing ML correctors model residuals without enforcing physical constraints, producing covariance matrices that violate positive-semi-definiteness when propagated forward" — this is concrete, falsifiable, and identifies a specific gap.

### For SiS grant proposals (NSF, DARPA, AFRL)

Apply ABDCE:
- **Action (opening)**: Lead with the most dramatic consequence — a specific conjunction scenario with quantified collision probability and the error that caused it.
- **Background**: Current SDA architecture; SGP4 limitations; why ML alone is insufficient.
- **Development**: CTPC framework; physics-as-architecture philosophy; experimental plan.
- **Climax**: Expected outcomes: covariance realism metric, operational demonstration.
- **Ending (Resolution)**: The SWM vision — a differentiable space environment model enabling real-time space traffic management.

**The Bizzwidget warning**: Do not open a proposal with "We propose CTPC, a physics-constrained neural corrector." Open with the problem: "The United States faces an escalating collision risk in low Earth orbit that current orbit propagation tools cannot reliably quantify." Then sell CTPC as the solution to that named problem.

**Condensing**: NSF Project Descriptions are 15 pages. Write 18; cut to 15 by pruning aims that do not directly advance the core story, eliminating literature review material that does not frame the knowledge gap, and stripping empty amplifiers from every sentence.

## Open Questions

1. **CTPC opening style**: Is the CTPC paper audience patient enough for a pawn push, or does the SDA + ML interdisciplinary context require a queen launch? The paper targets both aerospace engineers (who know SGP4 limits) and ML researchers (who may not know SDA). A queen launch that addresses both simultaneously would be stronger.

2. **Proposal structure decision**: For an NSF CAREER proposal, is ABDCE or OCAR more appropriate? NSF reviewers are typically patient specialists — OCAR may work. But the broader impacts and intellectual merit sections follow different logics. Consider hybrid: ABDCE for the opening, OCAR within each section.

3. **Abstract length norms**: Schimel's OCAR abstract template does not specify sentence counts for ML venues. ICLR abstracts average 150–250 words; NeurIPS ~200 words. Need to calibrate OCAR arc density to word-count constraints.

4. **Statistics reporting norms in ML**: Schimel's advice (state the magnitude, not just significance) is directly applicable to ML papers that report only "our method significantly outperforms baseline" without effect size. How do ML venues expect effect sizes reported (percentage improvement? absolute metric value? confidence intervals)?

5. **The "queen launch" and reviewer bias**: In some conservative engineering venues (AIAA, AAS), aggressive queen launches may signal overconfidence to specialist reviewers who expect measured pawn pushes. Calibrate by reading recent accepted papers from target venue.

6. **Condensing for SiS proposals**: AFRL BAAs often have 15-page white-paper limits followed by full-proposal invitations. The white paper is pure ABDCE with extreme condensing. Should the white-paper agent follow even stricter Schimel condensing rules than the full proposal agent?

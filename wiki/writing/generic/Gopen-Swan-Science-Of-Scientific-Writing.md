# The Science of Scientific Writing (Gopen & Swan 1990)

## One-Line Intuition

Readers make interpretive decisions based on WHERE in a sentence information appears — so writers must control structure, not just content, to communicate accurately.

## Why This Matters

Scientific writing is routinely blamed for being hard to read because the subject matter is complex. Gopen and Swan challenge this assumption directly. They demonstrate that most of the difficulty is structural, not conceptual — the jargon can be replaced with bracketed placeholders and the prose remains almost equally hard to parse. The problem is that writers deposit information into sentences without regard for where readers expect to find it. The solution is not simplification; it is structural alignment between writer intent and reader expectation.

The article introduces a reader-expectation methodology derived from rhetoric, linguistics, and cognitive psychology. Its claim is strong and practical: if you place information in the locations readers habitually look for it, the probability that any large reading community will interpret your prose as you intended rises dramatically. You cannot guarantee a single interpretation — "all sentences are infinitely interpretable, given an infinite number of interpreters" (p. 7) — but you can load the odds in your favor.

For Bilal: these principles are directly applicable to every CTPC paper section, every journal submission, and every proposal. The framework transfers without modification from biochemistry to orbital mechanics.

## Core Content

### 1. The Fundamental Premise: Structure Creates Meaning

The article opens with two claims that override the "complexity excuse":

1. Complexity of thought need not lead to impenetrability of expression.
2. Improving the quality of writing actually improves the quality of thought.

The mechanism: readers do not simply read — they **interpret**. Any piece of prose may "mean" in ten different ways to ten different readers. Readers make their most important interpretive decisions about the substance of prose based on clues they receive from its **structure**. Good writers are intuitively aware of readers' structural expectations; that is why their prose has what Gopen and Swan call "shape."

Key framing: the purpose of scientific discourse is not "the mere presentation of information and thought, but rather its actual communication. It does not matter how pleased an author might be to have converted all the right data into sentences and paragraphs; it matters only whether a large majority of the reading audience accurately perceives what the author had in mind" (p. 1).

### 2. Units of Discourse and the One-Point Rule

A **unit of discourse** is defined as anything with a beginning and an end: a clause, a sentence, a paragraph, a section, an article.

**Rule:** Each unit of discourse, no matter what the size, is expected to serve a single function — to make a single point.

This applies at every scale:
- A sentence makes one point.
- A paragraph develops one idea.
- A section of a paper addresses one question.
- The article as a whole advances one argument.

When structural expectations for unit boundaries are violated — when too much experimental detail appears in the Results section, or when Discussion and Results intermingle — readers are forced to divert energy from understanding content to unraveling structure. "As the complexity of the content increases moderately, the possibility of misinterpretation or noninterpretation increases dramatically" (p. 2).

### 3. Principle 1: Subject–Verb Separation

**The problem:** Readers expect a grammatical subject to be followed immediately by its verb. Anything that intervenes is read as an interruption — and therefore as something of lesser importance.

**Why:** The reader needs syntactic resolution. Without the verb, the reader does not know what the subject is doing, or what the sentence is about. The reader holds tension — a kind of "mental breath" — waiting for the verb. During that wait, attention focuses on the verb's arrival; anything in the gap is processed as secondary. If important content is buried in the subject–verb gap, the reader will brand it as interruptive regardless of its actual significance, and will not re-evaluate its importance once the sentence closes.

**The original URF sentence (42 words):**

> The smallest of the URF's (URFA6L), a 207-nucleotide (nt) reading frame overlapping out of phase the NH₂-terminal portion of the adenosinetriphosphatase (ATPase) subunit 6 gene has been identified as the animal equivalent of the recently discovered yeast H⁺-ATPase subunit 8 gene.

The subject ("the smallest") is separated from its verb ("has been identified") by 23 words — more than half the sentence. The 23-word interruption includes what may be important scientific information (the nucleotide reading frame detail), but its structural location brands it as merely interruptive. The reader discovers its potential value too late.

**Revision Option A** (if the intervening material is significant): use a semicolon to create a secondary stress position and give the material its own syntactic closure —

> The smallest of the URF's is URFA6L, a 207-nucleotide (nt) reading frame overlapping out of phase the NH₂-terminal portion of the adenosinetriphosphatase (ATPase) subunit 6 gene; it has been identified as the animal equivalent of the recently discovered yeast H⁺-ATPase subunit 8 gene.

**Revision Option B** (if the intervening material is an aside): delete it, driving the sentence to its significant point —

> The smallest of the URF's (URFA6L) has been identified as the animal equivalent of the recently discovered yeast H⁺-ATPase subunit 8 gene.

Only the author can choose — but the choice must be made consciously, and both options require the writer to decide what matters. The structural decision is a thinking decision.

**Third sentence analysis (62 words):**

> Recently, however, immunoprecipitation experiments with antibodies to purified, rotenone-sensitive NADH-ubiquinone oxido-reductase [hereafter referred to as respiratory chain NADH dehydrogenase or Complex I] from bovine heart, as well as enzyme fractionation studies, have indicated that six human URF's (that is, URF1, URF2, URF3, URF4, URF4L, and URF5, hereafter referred to as ND1, ND2, ND3, ND4, ND4L, and ND5) encode subunits of Complex I.

After the subject ("experiments"), the reader wades through 27 words — including three hyphenated compound words, a parenthetical interruption, and an "as well as" phrase — before arriving at the anticlimactic verb "have indicated." Without a moment to recover, the reader encounters a "that" clause in which the new subject ("six human URF's") is separated from its verb ("encode") by yet another 20 words.

**Diagnostic:** Subject–verb separation is the single most common structural defect in scientific prose.

### 4. Principle 2: The Stress Position

**Definition:** The stress position is the location at the end of a unit of discourse — the point of syntactic closure. Readers naturally emphasize whatever arrives last in a sentence. This is not a stylistic preference; it is a linguistic universal.

**Mechanism:** Readers take a "mental breath" at the start of each new sentence, summoning tension as the syntax unfolds. As the sentence draws toward conclusion, the reader begins to exhale. The exhalation produces a sense of emphasis. The material that triggers the exhalation receives the greatest reading emphasis — it is what the reader remembers, what feels like the point.

**Corollary (the strawberry shortcake principle):** "Beginning with the exciting material and ending with a lack of luster often leaves us disappointed and destroys our sense of momentum. We do not start with a strawberry shortcake and work our way up to the broccoli" (p. 5).

**Two failure modes when emphatic material is not in the stress position:**

1. The stress position is occupied by material that clearly is not worthy of emphasis. The reader must guess, without structural help, what else in the sentence might be important. In long, dense sentences this guessing becomes unreliable.
2. The stress position is occupied by something that appears incapable of receiving emphasis — a trailing subordinate clause, an "etc.", a date in parentheses. The reader emphasizes this imposter material anyway, and the writer loses interpretive control.

**The stress position can vary in size.** Sometimes it is one word; sometimes it extends to several lines. The definitive factor: the stress position coincides with the moment of syntactic closure. A reader has reached the beginning of the stress position when she knows nothing remains in the clause or sentence except the material presently being read.

**Secondary stress positions:** Properly used colons and semicolons create secondary stress positions within a sentence. The material before a colon or semicolon must be able to stand alone as a complete sentence — this is both a grammatical requirement and a rhetorical tool. It allows a writer to accommodate multiple pieces of emphasis-worthy information in a single long sentence without sacrificing clarity:

> The smallest of the URF's is URFA6L, a 207-nucleotide (nt) reading frame overlapping out of phase the NH₂-terminal portion of the adenosinetriphosphatase (ATPase) subunit 6 gene; it has been identified as the animal equivalent of the recently discovered yeast H⁺-ATPase subunit 8 gene.

The semicolon creates a second stress position, allowing "the animal equivalent of the recently discovered yeast H⁺-ATPase subunit 8 gene" to receive emphasis separately from the nucleotide detail.

**When is a sentence too long?** Not when it exceeds 29 words (the readability-formula standard). "A sentence is too long when it has more viable candidates for stress positions than there are stress positions available" (p. 8). A 100-word sentence with clear sequential stress positions can be perfectly readable. A 10-word sentence with two equally plausible stress-position candidates can be impenetrable.

### 5. Principle 3: The Topic Position

**Definition:** The topic position is the beginning of a sentence — the location where readers expect to find perspective and context.

**The proverbial summary:** For the stress position — "Save the best for last." For the topic position — "First things first."

**Two simultaneous functions of the topic position:**

1. **Backward linkage:** The topic position connects the current sentence to the preceding discussion. It tells the reader: "This sentence continues the story we have been building."
2. **Forward context:** The topic position establishes whose story or what concept the current sentence is about. "Readers expect a unit of discourse to be a story about whoever shows up first" (p. 9).

**The bees/pollen example:**

- "Bees disperse pollen" — a sentence about bees.
- "Pollen is dispersed by bees" — a sentence about pollen.

Both sentences report identical facts. The passive construction is not inferior; it is the superior choice if the paragraph is telling the story of pollen. The topic position, not the active/passive distinction, governs interpretation.

**Old information vs. new information:**

- **Old information** = material the reader has already encountered in this piece of discourse (previously introduced concepts, terms, findings). It belongs in the topic position because it provides backward linkage.
- **New information** = material making its first appearance. When new information is important enough to receive emphasis, it belongs in the stress position.

The default flow: old information in topic position → new information in stress position. This is the engine of a well-constructed paragraph.

**When the topic position is constantly occupied by new information**, readers cannot construct the logical flow of the argument. Each sentence feels disconnected. The reader cannot tell whose story is being told. Energy that should go to comprehending content is diverted to reconstructing structure.

### 6. The Earthquake Passage: Full Before/After Analysis

This is the article's second extended worked example, demonstrating the topic-position principle at the paragraph scale.

**Original passage:**

> Large earthquakes along a given fault segment do not occur at random intervals because it takes time to accumulate the strain energy for the rupture. The rates at which tectonic plates move and accumulate strain at their boundaries are approximately uniform. Therefore, in first approximation, one may expect that large ruptures of the same fault segment will occur at approximately constant time intervals. If subsequent mainshocks have different amounts of slip across the fault, then the recurrence time may vary, and the basic idea of periodic mainshocks must be modified. For great plate boundary ruptures the length and slip often vary by a factor of 2. Along the southern segment of the San Andreas fault the recurrence interval is 145 years with variations of several decades. The smaller the standard deviation of the average recurrence interval, the more specific could be the long term prediction of a future mainshock.

**Diagnosis — topic-position audit** (the first word/phrase of each sentence):

| Sentence | Topic position content |
|---|---|
| 1 | Large earthquakes |
| 2 | The rates |
| 3 | Therefore . . . one |
| 4 | subsequent mainshocks |
| 5 | great plate boundary ruptures |
| 6 | the southern segment of the San Andreas fault |
| 7 | the smaller the standard deviation . . . |

Every sentence begins with new or tangentially related information — precisely where readers look for old, familiar, backward-linking material. The story whose thread holds the paragraph together is the **recurrence interval between earthquakes**, but this concept rarely appears in the topic position. Two readers constructing meaning from only the topic positions would build two different stories.

**Stress-position audit** (what each sentence emphasizes):

The identified stress-worthy information per sentence: time to accumulate strain energy; approximately uniform; large ruptures of the same fault; different amounts of slip; vary by a factor of 2; variations of several decades; predictions of future mainshock.

**Revised version** (Gopen and Swan's proposal):

> Large earthquakes along a given fault segment do not occur at random intervals because it takes time to accumulate the strain energy for the rupture. The rates at which tectonic plates move and accumulate strain at their boundaries are roughly uniform. Therefore, nearly constant time intervals (at first approximation) would be expected between large ruptures of the same fault segment. [However?], the recurrence time may vary; the basic idea of periodic mainshocks may need to be modified if subsequent mainshocks have different amounts of slip across the fault. [Indeed?], the length and slip of great plate boundary ruptures often vary by a factor of 2. [For example?], the recurrence interval along the southern segment of the San Andreas fault is 145 years with variations of several decades. The smaller the standard deviation of the average recurrence interval, the more specific could be the long term prediction of a future mainshock.

**What changed:**

1. Old information (recurrence intervals, fault ruptures) moved to topic positions — providing backward linkage sentence-by-sentence.
2. New, emphatic information moved to stress positions.
3. Connective tissue between sentences made explicit ([However?], [Indeed?], [For example?]) — bracketed because the agent could not determine the author's intended logical relationship; these are placeholders flagging decisions only the author can make.
4. Sentence length is approximately unchanged. The revision is not shorter. Clarity came from structural rearrangement, not reduction.

**Key quote on length:** "Sheer length was neither the problem nor the solution. The revised version is not noticeably shorter than the original; nevertheless, it is significantly easier to interpret. We have indeed deleted certain words, but not on the basis of wordiness or excess length" (p. 8).

### 7. Revision Procedure: Three Rules Applied Together

The article consolidates three principles that can be used as a checklist when revising any passage:

1. **Grammatical subjects should be followed as soon as possible by their verbs.** (Subject–verb separation rule.)
2. **Every unit of discourse, no matter the size, should serve a single function or make a single point.** (One-point rule.)
3. **Information intended to be emphasized should appear at points of syntactic closure — the stress position.** (Stress-position rule.)

Applied to a paragraph, the revision procedure is:

1. Extract the topic-position content from every sentence. Does it provide backward linkage? Does it place the right entity/concept at the subject position?
2. Extract the stress-position content from every sentence. Is this the most emphasis-worthy information in the sentence?
3. Locate all subject–verb separations. Is the intervening material worth a secondary stress position (use semicolon) or should it be cut?
4. Audit connective tissue between sentences. Is the logical relationship explicit or implicit? If implicit, decide whether to make it explicit.

### 8. The Table Analogy: Structure as Signal

The article opens with a table demonstration that is easy to overlook but is conceptually foundational.

The same temperature-vs-time data can be presented as:
- An unstructured list: `t(time)=15', T(temperature)=32°; t=0', T=25°; t=6', T=29°; ...`
- A properly headed two-column table with time on the left and temperature on the right.

Readers find the table dramatically easier. The structure of the second format provides an easily perceived context (time) on the left, and places the interesting result (temperature) on the right — exactly the old-information-left, new-information-right pattern that mirrors the topic/stress position principle at the sentence level.

If the table columns are reversed — temperature on the left, time on the right — the table becomes much harder to read, even though every number is identical.

**This demonstrates the core insight of the whole article:** the same information, arranged differently, communicates differently. Structure is not decoration. Structure is meaning.

### 9. Why Revision Does Not Produce a Unique Correct Answer

A critical epistemic point: Gopen and Swan's revisions of the URF passage and the earthquake passage are not "the correct version." They are one set of decisions about what deserves emphasis and how to link sentences. If a reader disagrees with those decisions, that disagreement itself proves the point — the original failed to communicate its intended weighting clearly enough that readers converge on the same interpretation.

"It is probable that many of our readers — and perhaps even the authors — will disagree with some of our choices. If so, that disagreement underscores our point: The original failed to communicate its ideas and their connections clearly" (p. 8).

The goal of the framework is not to produce a single correct text. The goal is to raise the probability that a large majority of readers perceive the prose in the way the author intended. "We cannot succeed in making even a single sentence mean one and only one thing; we can only increase the odds" (p. 7).

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Primary agent:** Layer 0 (Generic Craft Agent) — this is the foundational document for sentence-level and paragraph-level revision analysis.

**Secondary agents:** Layer 1a (Paper-Writing Agent) and Layer 1b (Proposal-Writing Agent) should inherit these principles and apply them at section level (introduction stress positions, abstract topic positions, contribution bullets).

### Concrete Checks for Agent Revision Mode

When reviewing a draft paragraph or sentence, apply in order:

1. **Subject–verb audit:** For every sentence, locate the subject and the verb. Count the words between them. If > 5–7 words, flag: "Subject–verb separation: consider whether the intervening material deserves a secondary stress position (semicolon) or should be cut."

2. **Stress-position audit:** For every sentence, identify the last word or phrase before syntactic closure. Ask: is this the most important piece of information in the sentence? If the sentence ends with a date, a qualifier, a trailing prepositional phrase, or a parenthetical, flag: "Stress-position may be wasted — consider moving emphatic content to sentence end."

3. **Topic-position audit (paragraph level):** Extract the first 3–5 words of each sentence in a paragraph. List them. Is there a coherent strand of old/familiar information? Do they link backward to the previous sentence? If each sentence begins with new information, flag: "Topic positions carry new information — reorganize so old/familiar material leads each sentence."

4. **One-point check:** Does each sentence try to make more than one point? If so, consider splitting at a semicolon or period to create explicit stress positions for each point.

5. **Connective-tissue check:** Between sentences, is the logical relationship (therefore, however, for example, indeed) explicit or implicit? If implicit, flag with a bracketed placeholder: [However?], [Therefore?], [For example?] for the author to decide.

6. **Table/figure structure check:** In any table, does context (independent variable, known quantity) appear on the left? Does the new, interesting result appear on the right? If not, flag for reversal.

### Modes of Use

- **Draft review:** Run the five checks above sentence-by-sentence on Introduction, Results, Discussion drafts.
- **Abstract revision:** The abstract's final sentence should occupy the stress position of the whole abstract — the most important contribution. The first sentence topic position should establish the field context (old information for the community).
- **Contribution bullets:** Each bullet in a contribution list is its own unit of discourse. The most important claim should appear at the end of the bullet (stress position within the bullet), not buried mid-phrase.
- **Proposal section review:** For an NSF/AFOSR proposal, the Broader Impact section's topic positions should link backward to the Intellectual Merit section. New claims should land in stress positions.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

### Paper writing (direct application)

Every CTPC paper section has specific structural demands under this framework:

- **Abstract:** The last sentence is the stress position of the entire abstract. It should contain CTPC's most distinctive claim — likely the hard-constraint enforcement of dissipation (Ḣ ≤ 0 as an architectural prior), not a generic statement about accuracy. The first sentence's topic position should establish the SDA/orbital mechanics context.

- **Introduction:** The paragraph introducing CTPC for the first time must place CTPC's core novelty in the stress position. Subject–verb separation is particularly dangerous here: "CTPC, a Continuous-Time Probabilistic Corrector that models residuals in the RTN frame using a Port-Hamiltonian dissipative block, is proposed" buries the verb under 22 words of specification. Revise to: "CTPC is a Continuous-Time Probabilistic Corrector; it models residuals in the RTN frame using a Port-Hamiltonian dissipative block."

- **Methods section:** Each subsection paragraph should tell the story of one architectural component. Topic positions should carry the component name (old information once introduced); stress positions should carry the key design decision (e.g., "the Cholesky parameterization ensures positive definiteness" lands in stress position).

- **Results/Discussion:** Stress positions should contain the quantitative finding (e.g., "reduces 3σ covariance error by 41%"), not the baseline description. Topic positions should repeat the evaluation condition already established in Methods.

### Proposal writing (direct application)

- **Specific Aims page:** Each aim's first sentence must establish context (old: the problem is known), and the aim statement itself lands in the stress position (new: what CTPC uniquely contributes). Subject–verb separation in aim statements is especially costly — reviewers read at speed and weight stress positions.

- **Intellectual Merit:** The paragraph on prior work should place the gap in the stress position of the prior-work paragraph, not the prior work itself. This sets up CTPC as the entity that fills the gap — which then occupies the topic position of the next paragraph.

### Writing-as-thinking benefit

Gopen and Swan's claim that improving writing improves thinking is directly relevant to Bilal's research process. Forcing every CTPC claim into a clear stress position requires deciding: what is actually the most important thing I am claiming here? Sentence structure becomes a forcing function for scientific clarity.

## Open Questions

1. **Compound sentences with multiple subordinate clauses:** Gopen-Swan focus on main clause stress positions. How should a writer handle sentences where the most important information is in a subordinate "that" clause — should the subordinate clause be promoted to the main clause, or does the "that" clause itself constitute a stress position?

2. **Section-level stress positions:** The article treats sentences and paragraphs but stops short of a theory of section-level stress positions. How does the framework scale to the level of a paper section? Is the last paragraph of a Methods section a "stress position" for the whole section?

3. **Cross-sentence topic chains:** In the earthquake example, the revision sustains a topic-position chain around "recurrence intervals." How should a writer handle a paragraph that must develop TWO concepts simultaneously — does the chain break, or can a writer interleave two topic threads?

4. **Passive voice as a topic-position tool:** Gopen-Swan vindicate the passive voice when it places the right entity in the topic position ("Pollen is dispersed by bees" for a pollen-story paragraph). Is there a systematic rule for when passive is preferred over active beyond the topic-position criterion?

5. **Abstract structure:** The article does not discuss the abstract as a genre with its own stress/topic conventions. Does the first sentence of an abstract function as the topic position for the entire paper? Does the last sentence function as the paper's ultimate stress position?

6. **Interaction with hedging language:** Scientific writing requires hedges ("may," "suggests," "indicates"). Do hedges placed in the stress position dilute emphasis? Should hedges be moved earlier in the sentence when the hedged claim is the key finding?

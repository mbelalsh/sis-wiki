# "They Say / I Say": The Moves That Matter in Academic Writing

Gerald Graff & Cathy Birkenstein — Second Edition, W. W. Norton & Company, 2010.
Both authors: University of Illinois at Chicago.

---

## One-Line Intuition

All effective academic argument is a response — you must first accurately represent what others have said ("they say") before your own position ("I say") earns the right to be heard.

---

## Why This Matters

Academic writing is often taught as a solo act: develop a thesis, support it with evidence. Graff and Birkenstein argue this misses the fundamental social structure of all argument. Arguments are provoked — they respond to something said, believed, assumed, or overlooked. When a writer fails to establish "they say," the reader has no reason to care about "I say." The book's core intervention is to make this conversational structure explicit and teachable, equipping writers with the rhetorical moves needed to enter ongoing intellectual debates rather than simply assert positions in a vacuum.

For a researcher like Bilal, the stakes are especially clear: every CTPC paper is entering a conversation already in progress (SGP4 as baseline; PIML / PINN as dominant paradigm; dissipative PHNN as an emerging thread). Failing to represent that conversation accurately before asserting CTPC's contribution means the contribution will seem unmotivated or combative rather than additive. The book's framework is the operational theory behind what "related work" and "positioning" actually require.

**Part 1: "They Say"** — the art of representing others fairly

**Part 4: In Specific Academic Settings** — class discussion, reading, science writing, social science writing

### The Central Metaphor: Writing as Entering a Conversation

The book opens with a deceptively simple image. Imagine walking into a party mid-conversation. You do not launch immediately into your own monologue. You listen, identify what is being debated, and only then contribute in a way that responds to what has already been said. This is the model for all academic writing.

The formal structure is:

> **"They say ____. I say ____."**

This template is the "internal DNA" of all effective argument (Preface, p. xix). It forces the writer to identify the motivating view before stating their own, which:

1. Gives the reader a reason to care (the "they say" creates the stakes)
2. Makes the writer's position legible as a response rather than an isolated assertion
3. Disciplines the writer to understand opposing views accurately before critiquing them

The "they" can be a specific named author, a school of thought, a widely held assumption, a cultural belief, or even something nobody has talked about yet (a gap in the literature). The key is that it represents a real position the writer is engaging with, not a straw man.

### Part 1: "They Say" — The Three Moves of Representing Others

#### Move 1: Starting with What Others Are Saying (Chapter 1)

The book's first and most counter-intuitive piece of advice: **begin your text with "they say," not "I say."** The reason is that your thesis only acquires urgency once readers understand what it is responding to. Without an identified "they say," the reader's natural reaction is "So what? Why are you telling me this?"

Two dangers when opening with "they say":
- **Losing the "they say" as the text progresses.** The motivating view must be kept alive throughout the paper — returned to, re-engaged, updated. It is not merely a launching pad to discard after the introduction.
- **Implying agreement with everything you have summarized.** Writers often summarize a view in their opening and then proceed as if they endorse it, leaving readers confused about where they actually stand.

The solution to the second danger is to make the transition from "they say" to "I say" with an explicit contrastive marker: "but," "however," "although," "yet," "in contrast." The transition is the fulcrum of the argument.

#### Move 2: The Art of Summarizing (Chapter 2)

A good summary does two things simultaneously: it represents the source's view accurately (the "believing game") and shapes that representation toward the writer's own argument. The tension is deliberate — you are not a neutral transcription service.

**Three failure modes to avoid:**

1. **The "closest cliché" syndrome.** Mistaking a complex or counterintuitive argument for the nearest commonplace idea. Graff and Birkenstein give the example of summarizing Judith Butler's argument about gender indeterminacy as simply "women should have equal rights." The cliché is true but misses Butler's actual claim entirely.

2. **The "list summary."** Reporting everything a source says without identifying what the source is centrally arguing. A list summary fails to establish "they say" because it never answers: what is the author's main claim, and why does it matter?

3. **Ventriloquism.** Allowing your own views to colonize the summary, so the source ends up saying what you want them to say rather than what they actually argue.

The corrective is "to capture the source's own words and emphasis" and then "translate" that into language legible to your reader — a two-step that requires close reading first and selective framing second.

#### Move 3: The Art of Quoting (Chapter 3)

Direct quotation serves two purposes: it provides evidence and it demonstrates that you have actually engaged with the source's exact language. But a bare quotation — dropped into the text without framing — does neither.

**The quotation sandwich:** every quotation must be enclosed in three layers:

1. **The lead-in:** introduce the quote, attribute it, and signal why you are using it
2. **The quotation itself**
3. **The follow-up:** explain what the quotation means, how it supports your point, or what it leaves unresolved

The anti-pattern is called **"hit-and-run" quotation** — inserting a quote and immediately moving on as if its meaning is self-evident. It never is. Readers need the writer to tell them how to interpret what they have just read.

A related problem is **over-quoting**: using so many direct quotations that the writer's own voice disappears. The book recommends a selective approach — quote when the source's exact phrasing is important (either because it is precise, eloquent, or likely to be contested), and summarize/paraphrase otherwise.

### Part 2: "I Say" — Responding with Your Own Position

#### Move 4: Three Ways to Respond (Chapter 4)

Once "they say" has been established, the writer must respond. There are exactly three logical options, and each generates a different rhetorical structure:

**Option A: Agree (but with a difference)**
You accept the other's position but extend, nuance, or add to it. The signal phrase is "I agree that ____; furthermore, ____." The key is that agreeing is not merely echoing — you must add something (a new case, a stronger mechanism, a broader implication) that justifies entering the conversation at all. Pure agreement with no added value is not a contribution.

**Option B: Disagree (and explain why)**
You reject the other's position and supply the counter-evidence or counter-reasoning. The signal phrase structure is "Although X argues ____, I contend ____ because ____." Disagreement must be based on evidence or logic, not mere preference. It also requires actually engaging with the strongest version of the opposing view — refuting a weakened version ("straw man") does not count as disagreement.

**Option C: Agree and disagree simultaneously ("the yes and no" response)**
You accept some parts of the position and reject others. This is the most common and often most sophisticated response in academic writing, because real intellectual positions are rarely entirely right or entirely wrong. Signal phrases: "Although I agree with X that ____, I cannot accept her overall conclusion that ____," or "While X is right that ____, she is wrong that ____."

The book emphasizes that Option C is not hedging or intellectual cowardice — it is the appropriate response to complex, multi-faceted claims, which most interesting academic claims are.

#### Move 5: Distinguishing Your View from Theirs (Chapter 5)

One of the most technically demanding skills in academic writing is maintaining a clear line between "they say" and "I say" after the initial framing. As a paper progresses, the writer alternates between representing others' views and asserting their own. Readers can easily lose track of whose voice is currently speaking.

**The problem of "voice bleed":** a passage that shifts from summarizing a source to asserting the writer's own position without a clear marker. Readers may attribute the writer's claim to the source, or vice versa.

**Solutions:**
- Use "I" explicitly. The book defends the first person vigorously against the common pedagogical injunction against it: "I" is the clearest signal that the writer has shifted from representing others to asserting their own view. Avoiding "I" leads to awkward circumlocutions ("it will here be argued") that obscure rather than clarify.
- Use signal phrases at every transition: "Smith argues ____, but I contend ____."
- Use "pointing words" (this, that, these, those) that clearly attach to what they modify, preventing floating referents.
- Use verbs of attribution that characterize the source's stance: "claims," "asserts," "contends" (strong positions); "suggests," "implies," "notes" (tentative or incidental positions); "acknowledges," "concedes," "admits" (positions the source accepts reluctantly, usually because you have pointed them out).

#### Move 6: Planting a Naysayer (Chapter 6)

This is the move that separates sophisticated academic writing from competent writing. Rather than waiting for reviewers or critics to raise objections, the skilled writer **anticipates and raises objections themselves** — and then responds.

**Why this works:**
- It inoculates the argument against objections the reader is already formulating
- It demonstrates intellectual honesty (the writer is aware of the argument's limitations)
- It demonstrates breadth (the writer has thought through multiple perspectives)
- It strengthens the argument by showing it survives adversarial scrutiny

The move has a standard structure:
1. Introduce the objector: "Of course, some might object that ____."
2. State the objection fairly and in its strongest form
3. Respond: concede what is true in the objection, then reassert or qualify your position

The book stresses the "fairly" — the objection must be the strongest version of the opposing view, not a weakened version that is easy to dismiss. The signal phrase "of course some might object" (and variants: "although one might contend," "it might seem that," "admittedly") signals that the writer is introducing a view they do not hold, distinguishing this from "they say" framing.

**How many objections?** Raise as many as the argument requires, but raising too many can signal that you have more doubts than convictions. The rule of thumb: raise the one or two most damaging objections and respond to them fully, rather than cataloguing minor quibbles.

#### Move 7: "So What? Who Cares?" — Saying Why It Matters (Chapter 7)

This is the most commonly omitted move in academic writing. Every argument must eventually answer two questions:

- **"So what?"** — What follows from your argument being correct? What changes if you are right?
- **"Who cares?"** — Who is the audience for this argument? Why should anyone outside the author care?

The book distinguishes between a paper's local significance (advancing a specific debate) and its broader significance (implications for a larger community or set of questions). Strong papers establish both.

The failure mode is a paper that proves its point but never explains why the point matters. The reader completes the argument and thinks: "Okay, interesting. And?" The writer must pre-empt this reaction by explicitly stating the stakes — ideally early in the paper (so readers are invested from the start) and again at the end (to crystallize what has been demonstrated).

For scientific writing specifically, the "so what" often takes the form of implications for future work, practical applications, or the way the finding reshapes how we think about the broader system. The question "What does this open the door to?" is a reliable prompt for generating "so what" content.

### Part 3: Tying It All Together

#### Move 8: Connecting the Parts — Cohesion and Transitions (Chapter 8)

A text is not a list of correctly-made individual moves. The moves must be connected so that readers can follow the argument's internal logic without having to reconstruct it themselves. Chapter 8 addresses the mechanics of cohesion.

**Three tools for connection:**

1. **Transition words and phrases** that signal logical relationships: "therefore," "consequently," "in contrast," "similarly," "as a result," "for example," "in other words," "indeed," "in sum." These are not decorative — they explicitly signal whether the next sentence extends, contrasts, exemplifies, or summarizes the previous one. Stripping them out forces readers to infer the relationship, which is error-prone.

2. **Pointing words** ("this," "these," "such," "the above") that create backward references. But pointing words must attach unambiguously to their referent. "This suggests ____" requires that "this" has a single clear antecedent. "This claim / this approach / this finding" is better than naked "this."

3. **"Repetition with a difference"** — using a key term or concept repeatedly across the text, but each time in a slightly new light. This creates coherence (the term anchors successive paragraphs) without monotony (the term develops rather than loops). This technique is what allows a long argument to feel unified rather than scattered.

The chapter also addresses the architecture of the argument at the paragraph level. Each paragraph should have a clear topic sentence, evidence, analysis, and a link to the next paragraph. The links are as important as the topic sentences — they are what make reading feel like following a continuous thread rather than reading isolated blocks.

#### Move 9: Academic Voice — "Ain't So / Is Not" (Chapter 9)

A widespread misconception is that academic writing requires a tone of impersonal, jargon-heavy formality. The book argues against this. Academic discourse is often perfectly compatible with informal language, colloquialism, and personal voice — provided the reasoning is rigorous and the claims are supported.

The key distinction is between **register** (formal vs. informal vocabulary and sentence structure) and **argument quality** (evidence, logic, responsiveness to objections). Academic writing requires the latter; it does not always require the former.

The book encourages writers to:
- Use their own natural voice, even in formal contexts
- Incorporate colloquial language where it clarifies rather than obscures
- Use "I" and "we" rather than awkward passive constructions
- Write sentences they would actually say aloud

The risk in overly formal or jargon-heavy prose is not that it sounds too sophisticated — it is that it obscures the argument. Clarity of argument and formality of register are not the same thing, and privileging the latter at the expense of the former is a category error.

#### Move 10: Metacommentary — "But Don't Get Me Wrong" (Chapter 10)

Metacommentary is writing about your own writing within the text — telling readers how to interpret what you are saying, flagging what you are not saying, or anticipating misreadings.

**Why metacommentary is necessary:** readers construct meaning actively, and they will sometimes construct the wrong meaning. A claim that seems clear to the writer can be misread as more extreme, more limited, or more concessive than intended. Metacommentary is the preemptive correction.

**Common metacommentary moves:**

- **"Don't misread me":** "I do not mean to suggest that ____; my point is rather ____."
- **"Flagging the larger significance":** "In other words, what I am arguing here is not just ____, but ____."
- **"Signaling structure":** "Having established ____, I now want to turn to ____."
- **"Clarifying scope":** "I am not arguing that ____; rather, ____."
- **"Flagging a concession":** "Although I concede that ____, my core claim remains ____."

The difference between metacommentary and mere repetition is that metacommentary is explicitly self-referential: it comments on the argument rather than restating it. It is the writer stepping briefly outside the argument to guide the reader's interpretation of it.

Metacommentary is also useful for managing emphasis. When a key point risks being buried in a complex passage, metacommentary can flag it: "The crucial point here is ____."

### Part 4: In Specific Academic Settings

#### Reading for the Conversation (Chapter 12)

The book extends its framework to reading, arguing that reading with the "they say / I say" lens improves comprehension. When reading a text, the questions to ask are:

- What view is this text responding to? What is the "they say"?
- What is the author's own position? What is the "I say"?
- How does the author respond — agree, disagree, or both?

Three reading challenges:

1. **The "they say" is present but not explicitly stated.** Many experienced writers assume their readers share the background consensus they are challenging, and so do not name it. Readers must reconstruct the implicit "they say" from context clues — usually the presence of "but," "however," "although," or a contrastive "yet" in the opening.

2. **The "they say" is about something nobody has talked about.** Some arguments respond not to a stated view but to a gap — something researchers have overlooked. The signal is phrases like "little attention has been paid to" or the Swales / Feak formulation: "establishing a niche by indicating a gap in previous research."

3. **Challenging language obscures the "they say / I say" structure.** Writers like Judith Butler use technical vocabulary and abstract syntax that can make the two-tier structure difficult to see. The prescription: translate the passage into everyday terms, building a "bridge" from the unfamiliar vocabulary to the familiar argument structure. Resist collapsing complex arguments into the nearest cliché.

#### Writing in the Sciences (Chapter 13, by Christopher Gillen)

Scientific writing is fundamentally argumentative, not merely informational, despite its objective tone. Data do not speak for themselves — scientists interpret data, assess its quality, challenge prior interpretations, and propose novel mechanisms. The "they say / I say" structure is ubiquitous in science writing; it simply appears in a technical register.

**Distinctive features of science writing:**

- **Data are the currency of scientific argument.** Before making claims, describe the data: present the prevailing theories / hypotheses (what "they say"), explain the methodology (how data were collected), and summarize the findings (what the data show).
- **Verb tense and epistemic hedging encode confidence level.** "The data suggest/imply" vs. "Our results show/demonstrate" vs. "The findings prove." In science writing, "prove" is almost never used for a single study; "suggest" or "indicate" are the appropriate hedges.
- **Scientific consensus makes full disagreement rare.** More commonly, scientists "agree but with a difference" — accepting prior data but reinterpreting it, or extending prior findings to a new domain.
- **"Say why it matters" is mandatory.** Broader implications (for future studies, for practical applications, for the theoretical framework) must be explicitly stated; reviewers and editors treat their absence as a weakness.

#### Writing in the Social Sciences (Chapter 14, by Erin Ackerman)

Social science writing contains three structural components corresponding to the "they say / I say" model:

1. **Introduction and thesis** — establishes what prior literature has said and what the paper will challenge, extend, or fill in
2. **Literature review** — the "they say" in full: fairly representing the scholarly conversation on the topic, including tensions and gaps
3. **Analysis** — the "I say" backed by data (quantitative or qualitative), followed by "anticipate objections" and "say why it matters"

The literature review in social science writing is where summary skills are most taxed, because the writer must characterize multiple competing positions simultaneously without losing their own angle of vision. The cardinal rule: the literature review serves the writer's argument — it is not an exhaustive catalogue of prior work but a selective representation that sets up the paper's intervention.

## How a Writing Agent Should Use This

**Primary owner:** Layer-0 Generic-Writing-Agent. The framework applies to all academic writing regardless of domain.

**Highest-leverage applications for the writing agent stack:**

### In RESPOND mode (the most direct application)
When the agent is generating or revising a section that positions the paper relative to prior work, the three response options (agree / disagree / agree-and-disagree) should be the explicit decision tree:
- What is the "they say" in this section?
- Which of the three response types is the paper making?
- Is the transition from "they say" to "I say" marked with the right contrastive signal?

### For the Paper-Writing-Agent: Related Work and Introduction
The related-work section is the "they say" section of a paper. The literature review should not be a list of prior papers but a representation of the ongoing conversation, with the paper's contribution framed as a response. The introduction should establish the "they say" before the thesis; the thesis is always an "I say" in response to something already in progress.

### For the Paper-Writing-Agent: Anticipating Reviewer Objections
**Planting a naysayer maps directly onto preemptive reviewer response.** In peer review, reviewers raise objections. The strongest papers raise those objections themselves — in a limitations section, a discussion section, or inline as acknowledged trade-offs — before reviewers can. This turns a potential rejection point into evidence of intellectual rigor. The "of course some might object" template and its variants are the operational tools for this.

### For the Proposal-Writing-Agent
Proposals must convince reviewers that the proposed work matters. The "so what / who cares" move is therefore not optional in proposals — it must be answered explicitly in the significance section. The proposal's introduction should also establish the "they say" (the gap, the unsolved problem, the dominant but inadequate approach) before stating the proposed contribution.

### For the Generic-Writing-Agent: Metacommentary pass
When reviewing any draft, the agent should perform a metacommentary audit: are there places where readers are likely to misread the argument's scope, strength, or direction? If so, insert explicit "don't misread me" language at those junctures.

## Connection to SiS / CTPC

Every CTPC paper faces the same rhetorical challenge described in Chapter 1: asserting a new framework without establishing what that framework is responding to makes the contribution unmotivated. The "they say" for CTPC is multi-layered:

1. **The dominant paradigm they say:** "SGP4 is the operational standard; its errors are acceptable; ML corrections can be applied as soft-penalty physics-informed residuals."
2. **The sub-consensus they say:** "Hard architectural encoding of physics is unnecessary when sufficient data exist; soft constraints trained end-to-end perform comparably."
3. **The unspoken assumption they say (gap framing):** "Nobody has systematically enforced dissipation as a hard architectural constraint in the RTN-frame corrector literature."

CTPC's "I say" is a structured response to all three: it agrees that SGP4 remains the physics predictor (Option A — agree), disagrees that soft constraints are sufficient for dissipation (Option B — disagree with evidence), and introduces the Port-Hamiltonian corrector as the architectural solution to what prior work left unresolved.

The "planting a naysayer" move directly maps onto anticipating the most dangerous reviewer objection: that hard dissipation enforcement costs too much in flexibility or expressiveness. This objection must be raised by Bilal before reviewers raise it — and answered with the empirical evidence that Ḣ ≤ 0 as an architectural prior does not degrade prediction accuracy on the CTPC benchmark.

The "so what / who cares" move must connect CTPC's orbital-mechanics contribution to the broader SDA stakes: satellite conjunction analysis, covariance realism, the operational reliability of Space Domain Awareness for DoD and commercial operators. Papers that ground their "so what" in operational stakes rather than only in RMSE improvements are more persuasive to the reviewer populations at AIAA, AAS, and IEEE conferences.

## Open Questions

1. **Depth of "they say" in CTPC papers:** How explicitly should CTPC papers name the SGP4-as-baseline consensus as "they say"? The SDA reviewer community is small and expert — they may not need the consensus named; they may find it condescending if it is over-explained. The right calibration between explicit "they say" framing and assumed shared background is an open question for the CTPC writing strategy.

2. **The "gap" framing vs. the "correction" framing:** CTPC can be framed as responding to a gap (nobody has enforced hard dissipation architecturally) or as a correction (soft-constraint approaches are architecturally wrong). The gap framing is more collegial; the correction framing is more forceful. Which is more effective for the specific reviewer pools at AAS/AIAA vs. NeurIPS/ICLR? The book's framework describes both but does not prescribe; Bilal must decide based on venue norms.

3. **"So what" for the SDA community vs. the ML community:** A CTPC paper submitted to an ML venue needs a "so what" grounded in ML significance (novel architectural constraint mechanism; generalization to other dissipative systems). The same paper submitted to an SDA venue needs a "so what" grounded in operational impact (CDM accuracy; covariance realism; close-approach probability reliability). The agent should flag which "so what" is appropriate for which venue.

4. **Naysayer depth for CTPC:** The most dangerous naysayer for CTPC papers is probably "your Cholesky / PH dissipation constraint is computationally expensive and not worth the accuracy gain." Preparing a thorough and honest response to this specific objection — with ablation data — is a prerequisite for submission.

5. **Chapter 11 (class discussions) and Chapter 14 (social science writing) are not directly relevant to SiS.** They should not be drawn upon by the writing agent unless Bilal is writing for a non-technical audience.
